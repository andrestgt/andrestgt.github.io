# Pakistan & India
**1 March – 3 April 2001**

A journey through Pakistan from Islamabad to Lahore, crossing into India to explore Rajasthan and the north.

---

1 March to 3 April 2001

## Islamabad

Islamabad is essentially a city of public buildings and residential
enclaves for wealthy Pakistanis, planned on the drawing board, but
beautifully set at the foot of a mountain range.

There is one truly impressive sight: the gigantic Faisal Mosque,
dramatically set below a massive ridge.

## Madyan, Swat Valley

Madyan is a small town in the beautiful Swat Valley, below the
snow-capped Himalayan peaks.

It used to be something of a hippie stronghold, but these days there are
only a handful of guesthouses, with the odd traveller occasionally
wandering in.

It is no paradise, but it is a very pleasant place to relax.

Dinner at the guesthouse (50 Rp per person) was alright, though a little

old town: bazaars, tea houses, donkey carts, and streets packed with an
extraordinary number of friendly, curious people, to whom you are, quite

## Food

We had a superb speciality from Balochistan at Belochi Sajji in Fortress
Stadium.

Sajji is a leg of mutton or chicken, hung raw over a huge charcoal grill
and then left for hours, turning deliciously crisp and flavoursome from
smoke and heat alone.

## Wagah

A fascinating spectacle takes place every afternoon at the
Pakistani-Indian border.

The “flag ceremony” takes place every day; it’s when the border is closed for the day. Hundreds of spectators
on grandstands on both sides watch the procedure, clapping and shouting
loudly, each side trying to outdo the other.

Completely ridiculous, but amusingly harmless: a perfect example of
childish patriotism in two rival countries.

## Amritsar

Amritsar, the stronghold of Sikhism, is a fairly tidy city, though not
exactly inviting. Everywhere you seem to run into turbaned men with long
beards and extremely grim expressions.

The Golden Temple, the Sikhs’ most important shrine, is undeniably an impressive complex, but you do not really feel comfortable or welcome.

## Delhi

Delhi consists of two completely different districts.

On the one hand there is New Delhi, with its broad boulevards, green
spaces and government buildings; on the other there is Old Delhi, with
the massive Red Fort and the chaotic markets.

## Agra

For good reason, one of the most touristy, and also one of the most
nerve-racking (“Hello Sir, Hello Boss!”), places in India.

Agra Fort: impressive from the outside and surprisingly well preserved
inside, with lovely views of the Taj Mahal; it would be a real highlight
if you did not constantly feel a simmering rage at the shamelessly
inflated entrance fee (10 USD!!) and the relentless “Hello Postcards”,
“You buy this” types.

Taj Mahal: the Taj exceeded even our high expectations. The breathtaking
monument leaves you speechless at first, one of those places in the
world you really should see.

houses in the old town are not pink, but painted orange.

Winds), there are other pretty buildings within the partly walled old
centre, but what is truly great about Jaipur are the several massive
forts that stand impressively on the barren hills above the city.

## Pushkar

Pushkar is a wonderfully pretty little town on a holy lake. All you can
really do here is relax in the sun, take in the view, wander through the
lanes and recover from the exertions of India’s big cities.

Given these virtues, it is no surprise that Pushkar is a magnet for
tourists of every kind, which, however, does not mean the town loses
its atmosphere.

## Accommodation

Lake View Guesthouse; we had a room with a balcony and a magnificent
view over the lake. The room itself was very basic, and the shared

But the view alone might justify the 200 Rp.

## Food

Pushkar is a holy place and therefore there is no alcohol, no meat and
no eggs (but bhang in abundance).

Even so, we ate very well, at Venus Hotel and at Moondance (whose bhang
lassi for 30 Rp left us spaced out for hours).

Prices were generally very reasonable, except on the terrace of Pushkar
Palace. Here, though, the banana lassi was not only exceptionally good,
but you could also watch the sun set over the lake beneath coconut
palms.

## Mt. Abu

Mt. Abu is a very unusual destination for Rajasthan.

For one thing, the fresh air and pleasant temperature of this hill
station at 1,200 metres provide a welcome break from the desert heat.
For another, Mt. Abu is popular above all with Indian holidaymakers, and
there are relatively few Westerners, which keeps the hassle factor
pleasantly low.

The mountain scenery around the small lake is pretty, and you can go on
nice walks, for instance to Sunset Point, where the plateau ends
abruptly and offers dramatic views over the valley and the setting sun.

marble temples are fairly small and set in an unromantic location, but

## Accommodation

We decided to treat ourselves properly and stayed at Bikaner House, the
former summer residence of the Maharaja of Bikaner.

It was full of atmosphere and had plenty of charm, without being turned
into some luxury renovation project.

The suites for 2,000 Rp were phenomenally large and stylish, if not
luxurious in the conventional sense; rather, you could almost breathe in
the spirit of a bygone era.

## Food

Food in our hotel’s grand dining room was rather disappointing. Sure,
the ambience was great, but the quality of the dishes was by no means
better than what you get on the street for a fraction of the price (290
Rp for soup, five dishes and dessert).

Madras Cafe served tasty burgers, pizzas and chips (what heresy!).

the mountain range in the background are simply straight out of a fairy
tale, and it is hard to tear yourself away.

## Accommodation

Hotel Gangaur Ghat; right by the lake and excellent value for money.

Good, clean en-suite rooms for 200 Rp; terrific rooftop views.

## Food

Around Lake Pichola there is a vast choice of rooftop restaurants and
other establishments catering mostly to tourists. Even so, the quality
seems consistently high, we always ate well in Udaipur.

Our hotel’s rooftop served tasty Indian and Western food, as did the
rooftop of Poonam Haveli next door.

For a step up in price and ambience, you could dine at Jagat Niwas
Palace.

El Parador is a little outside on Lake Pichola and fairly expensive, but
worth the detour: the spinach burger for 80 Rp was divine, there was
fresh mango juice, and you could choose the music.

## Ahmedabad

Ahmedabad is, at first glance, a sticky, dusty and filthy moloch. We did
not have time for a second look, as we immediately headed straight to
the best hotel in town for dinner before our train left for Bombay.

The stylish Waterfall Restaurant at the Holiday Inn was surprisingly
affordable, and we were looked after with great courtesy.

## Bombay

Our first encounter with Bombay was dreadful. We spent half a day
trudging through Colaba with our luggage, pursued by touts and
taxi-wallahs, searching for a decent hotel room.

Although Bombay, especially Colaba, is a very nerve-racking city
(“Hello! Wanna Buy? One Pen, Ten Rupeeeeeeeees!!”), we still ended up
having a great time there. There may not be many standout sights, but
exploring this vast city on foot is hugely interesting, and Bombay has its pleasant sides too.

All in all, Bombay is a world city full of character and life, where you
are never bored.

## Accommodation

Hotels in Bombay are two to three times as expensive as in the rest of
India, and the quality of the rooms is very modest.

We looked at around 15 different hotels before we found a room that felt
halfway decent for the money.

Godwin in Colaba is a good mid-range hotel in a pleasantly quiet spot,
and our room for 1,700 Rp had a wall of windows with a view of the Taj
Hotel and the harbour, though the bathroom was fairly basic.

## Food

We ate a lot, excellently and luxuriously. Even the fashionable places
are still cheaper than in Europe (though extravagantly so by Indian
standards), and the quality of the food is extremely good.

Cloud 9 on our hotel’s roof had really creative cooking (a mix of
Western, Indian and African influences) and delicious cocktails.

The seafood brochette at the chic Cafe Royal was also very tasty, and at
Palkhi, one of Bombay’s best restaurants, we enjoyed the finest (and
most expensive) Indian food of our holiday.
