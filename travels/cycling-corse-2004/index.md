# Cycling Corsica
**16 June – 6 July 2004**

A cycling journey from Bilbao through the South of France, then across Corsica.

---

## Day 1 – 16 June

HLX flight to Bilbao (€19.99). The holiday got off to a stressful start,
because one of my bike panniers didn’t arrive at the airport. After
filing the claim, we picked up our hire car and drove to the campsite in
Sopelana, on a lovely stretch of coast about 15 kilometres north-west of
Bilbao. Nice grassy pitches, decent facilities, €15. In the afternoon we
headed into Bilbao for a look around, starting with the city’s biggest
sight: the Guggenheim Museum, architecturally outrageous and hugely
impressive. Even if you don’t go inside, the building alone is worth the
visit.

I’d always imagined Bilbao as a grey industrial town with little
to offer beyond that famous museum, but I couldn’t have been more wrong.
The central shopping boulevard, with its interesting mix of stately old
buildings and modern architecture, is very appealing; and on the other
side of the river there’s also a compact but gorgeous old town, packed
with bars, cafés and a superb central plaza surrounded by arcades. With
such an absurdly large selection of fantastic tapas bars, you really
have to try a few bits and pieces. We chose the venerable, beautifully
decorated Café Iruña.

is, at times, magical: green, forested headlands and unbelievably
beautiful bays with golden sand. For me, the stand-out spots were
Urdaibai Biosphere Reserve at the mouth of the Ría de Gernika, with its
great beaches, and the postcard-perfect, undeveloped Playa de Laga.

## Day 3 – 18 June (88 km)

San Sebastián — Irun — Biarritz — Hossegor\
Thankfully my bike panniers were delivered during the night, so the tour
could finally begin today. The route was hilly throughout and at times
busy with traffic, but it also had several very worthwhile sections and
towns. The best bit was the Corniche between the beautiful long beach at
Hendaye and the venerable seaside resort of St Jean de Luz.

Biarritz is
also pretty, even if it’s no longer glowing with the glamour of the old
days, though the many grand old villas still are still testimony to a time when it
was one of Europe’s most fashionable resorts.

## Day 4 – 19 June (144 km)

Hossegor — Lit-et-Mixe — Escources — Sabres — Roquefort\
We had a tailwind across the flat emptiness of the Landes. Kilometre
after kilometre of straight road, with forest or fields on either side,
hardly any settlements; at least we made good time. At the start there
were cycle paths as far as Lit-et-Mixe. Overnight at the rather remote Camping Municipal in Roquefort (grass, very clean and good facilities),
the only pretty little town for miles around.

## Day 5 – 20 June (148 km)

Roquefort — St Gor — Lavardac — Agen — Valence d’Agen — Moissac\
Today was fairly flat again and, thanks to the favourable wind, pleasant
to ride. Only the section between Durance and Feugarolles was quite
hilly. The pick of the towns today were Valence d’Agen and our stage
finish, Moissac. Agen, the biggest town we passed today, was nothing
special. Overnight at the Camping Municipal (€8) in Moissac. Lovely
large grassy pitches with space to lie around.

## Day 6 – 21 June (124 km)

Moissac — Toulouse — Avignonet\
Another mostly flat stage without much in the way of scenery. We covered
the first 40-odd kilometres on the lightly trafficked N113; shortly
after Grisolles we switched to the pleasant cycle path along the Canal
de la Garonne into Toulouse.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingCorse2004-0024.jpg" alt="Canal de la Garonne">
  <em>Canal de la Garonne</em>
</figure>

We spent the afternoon in this very
attractive city, which was buzzing because of the Fête de la Musique.
Although I’d passed Toulouse countless times, today was the first time I
actually visited the centre. With its splendid old town between the
Garonne and the Canal du Midi, “la ville rose” is one of the most
beautiful cities in France and absolutely worth seeing.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingCorse2004-0023.jpg" alt="Toulouse">
  <em>Toulouse</em>
</figure>

In the evening
we cycled about 40 kilometres along the cycle path by the Canal du
Midi, familiar territory by now, and pitched our tent at Camping du Radel
near Avignonet. Nice grassy pitches, but basic, rather outdated
facilities (only squat toilets). €8.30.

Avignonet — Castelnaudary — Bram — Carcassonne — Homps — Oupia\
Today was hilly almost the whole way, and a strong headwind made
progress even harder. We followed the cycle path along the Canal du Midi
for a bit, then took the N113 to Castelnaudary.

## Day 9 – 24 June (147 km)

Vic — Palavas — La Grande-Motte — Le Grau-du-Roi — Aigues-Mortes —
Salin-de-Giraud — Fos — Port-de-Bouc\
The first few kilometres took us, partly on cycle paths, through
faceless holiday resorts, only interrupted by the beautiful, undeveloped
dune beach of La Grande Traverse. Our first worthwhile stop was in the
very pretty little harbour town of Le Grau-du-Roi, which grew
organically, where we indulged in an oyster-and-mussels platter on the
promenade for a very late breakfast.

national road, partly four-lane; luckily, with a tailwind we made quick
progress, and there was a wide hard shoulder. Overnight at the Camping
Municipal in Port-de-Bouc. Poor, dusty pitches, but good facilities and
cheap (€6).

Corte is still rough around the edges and wonderfully
unpolished; with many of the old houses it’s almost unthinkable that
anyone still lives in them. But that’s exactly what adds to the charm of
this town, one of the most unusual places in the Mediterranean. The
influence of the university is especially noticeable in the quality and
variety of cafés and bars (remarkable for a place this small). Corte is
also fairly cheap; even in the chic Rex Lounge on the main corso we paid
less for our drinks than in budget pubs elsewhere on the island.
Overnight at the pleasant Camping O Sognu with decent pitches (€14.50)
and a great view of the town. Nice restaurant and good, rustic
facilities in converted farm buildings.

## Day 13 – 28 June (72 km)

Pinarellu — Porto-Vecchio — Bonifacio — Pianottoli\
After a coffee in the beautiful bay of Pinarellu, the day started off
very hilly on the way into Porto-Vecchio. The region around
Porto-Vecchio is one of the island’s main tourist centres, but even here
the motto is “soft tourism”; thankfully you won’t find any high-rise
hotel monstrosities. The town itself isn’t Corsica’s most attractive,
but the surrounding area with its many bays and beaches is lovely.

After a long stay in town we rode another 20-odd
kilometres on a demanding but scenic route to Pianottoli, where we
pitched our tent at Camping Le Domier, unfortunately quite a long way off
our route down towards the beach. Average pitches, decent facilities,
fairly cheap (€9.20).

## Day 14 – 29 June (75 km)

Pianottoli — Sartène — Propriano — Serra-di-Ferro\
Breakfast came in the form of a long climb from the campsite back up to
the main road. But that hardly mattered, because the whole day was tough
anyway. First we rode over Cap di Roccapina, with spectacular views
across a somewhat barren yet still beautiful coastal landscape.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingCorse2004-0073.jpg" alt="Cap di Roccapina">
  <em>Cap di Roccapina</em>
</figure>

After
the descent from the cape came a kilometre-long climb up to Sartène, a
town perched over 300 metres above sea level.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingCorse2004-0074.jpg" alt="Sartène">
  <em>Sartène</em>
</figure>

This place is one of the
main towns away from the coast and very much worth visiting. The pretty
main square lined with cafés is also a viewing terrace over the wide
rolling landscape that surrounds the town. Straight from the square you
pass through a gate into Sartène’s tiny old town, whose grey stone
buildings and narrow alleys feel like a classic Corsican mountain
village. There are a few restaurants with tables in the lanes and a
couple of souvenir and delicatessen shops; otherwise the old town is
purely residential. From Sartène it was about 13 kilometres down to the
coast and Propriano, a larger harbour town that isn’t especially
dramatic beyond a pleasant waterfront promenade, but it wins you over
because of its glorious position on the Golfe de Valinco, one of
Corsica’s most beautiful coastal areas, with countless beaches and a
gentle shoreline.

After a refreshing swim in the sea, we had to climb
steeply once more to reach the start of the D157, which then ran for a
few kilometres along the north side of the gulf, a very beautiful ride
with great views. Back down by the sea, we could actually enjoy three
whole kilometres of flat road for once, before the final 4-kilometre
climb up to Serra-di-Ferro. The campsite of this small mountain village
sits on a hill high above the sea and offers lovely views, good pitches
and decent solar-powered facilities; sadly, it was far too expensive
(€20.60).

## Day 15 – 30 June (74 km)

Serra-di-Ferro — Portiglio — Ajaccio\
The first part of the day was all about long, steep climbs and descents
through empty, beautiful coastal mountains on the narrow D155, which
was in miserable condition in places. First we climbed to a belvedere
with wonderful views over the Anse d’Orzo; then we climbed again, past
Aqua Doria, followed by a long descent down to Portiglio, the first town
on the Gulf of Ajaccio.

<figure>

  <em>Portiglio</em>

From here we could already see the city. From
this point on the route became flatter and the coastal scenery
gentler, and more built up. The road followed the coastline for a long
time and we passed numerous lovely bays with beaches; especially
beautiful was the long Plage d’Agosta with views of Ajaccio. To get into
the city we had to take a four-lane approach road, but it wasn’t a
problem. We first cycled through Ajaccio and continued along the
Corniche by the coast towards the Îles Sanguinaires. Here as well, the
coastal scenery was wonderful with plenty of beaches, effectively Ajaccio’s local beaches. Because we didn’t like the campsite (and it was also quite far
from the centre), we rode back into Ajaccio and took a room at Hôtel Le
Dauphin by the harbour: €60 with breakfast for a simple but pleasant
room with an en-suite bathroom, balcony and TV. In the afternoon we set
off on foot to explore Ajaccio. The city has little of Bastia’s or
Corte’s rugged charm. It’s much more polished, brighter, with friendlier
colours, a typical, lovely Mediterranean harbour town. The old town is
small and very pretty, with countless restaurants and cafés squeezing
tables into narrow lanes.

Place Foch and the area around the fishing harbour) is also very
attractive, offering food and café culture in abundance. One place worth
singling out is Fashion Café on Avenue 1er Consul, with extravagant
seating and an extensive bistro menu. We had dinner at the friendly
little restaurant L’Arche, which offered both North African and Corsican
specialities. Particularly good were the couscous brochette (€15) and
the kefimi (€8.50), but the Corsican charcuterie (€12) and especially
the cheese with fig jam were outstanding too. Very attentive, friendly
service.

## Day 18 – 3 July (85 km)

Calvi — Île-Rousse — St Florent — Marina de Farinole\

had to climb up to the mountain village of Lumio at 200 metres, then we
followed the coast through the comparatively gentle northern Balagne.
Île-Rousse is the most French-feeling town in Corsica, with a beautiful,
large square in the pretty centre shaded by plane trees.

We passed great secluded bays and

## Day 20 – 5 July (Epilogue – 33 km)

Bastia — Nice — Cagnes\
We took the 08:00 ferry (Corsica Ferries) to Nice and reached the
Vieux-Port around 13:00. We wandered for a while through the narrow
lanes of the large, handsome old town of Vieux Nice, then cycled out of
the city westwards.

at Palais d’Asie at the entrance to Cagnes: perfect Vietnamese cuisine
