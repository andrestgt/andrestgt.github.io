**January 2009**

## Getting to Myitkyina

We once again took a cheap AirAsia flight from Bangkok to Rangoon (US$38), where our guesthouse, Ocean Pearl Inn — booked online — provided a free transfer from the airport into the city. This was one of its advantages. The guesthouse is about a fifteen-minute walk east of Sule Paya; other positives were a small balcony and a proper hot shower. Still, next time I’d choose to stay at Okinawa again — mainly because of its better, quieter location and its more homely atmosphere. Ocean Pearl Inn was perfectly fine, though: US$15 for a clean room with two beds and air-conditioning.

We spent two days in Rangoon, then on the third morning took the 6 a.m. express train to Toungoo — a very comfortable seven-hour ride in Upper Class (US$16). Toungoo may not have any major sights, but it is the ideal place to break the long journey to Mandalay, especially because it is home to Myanmar Beauty, arguably the nicest guesthouse in the country. I stayed there for the second time in just a few months and again enjoyed it thoroughly. The home-cooked dinner (3,500 kyat) was excellent, as was the generous breakfast included in the price, with plenty of fresh fruit.

At midday the next day we continued to Mandalay, again in Upper Class on the express train. The ten-hour journey (US$20) isn’t particularly scenic, but it is far more comfortable than an overnight bus.

We did not reach Mandalay until very late in the evening and, since the popular Royal Guesthouse was full, took a room around the corner at E.T. Hotel — not a bad choice. It is not particularly atmospheric and a little worn, but the rooms (US$12) are clean and have private bathrooms and hot water, which is very welcome in the cool season (while the air-conditioning is currently utterly unnecessary). Breakfast is served on request, and the large plate of delicious mohinga was infinitely better than the usual toast with jam and a fried egg.

We had already booked our Air Bagan flight to Myitkyina (US$86) back in Rangoon, at the very friendly and helpful Good News Travel agency on the fourth floor of the FMI Centre. Mandalay, however, is far from an ideal place to fly out of. For reasons nobody can quite explain, the airport lies more than 40 kilometres from the city centre and there is no public transport. That leaves you at the mercy of taxi drivers, who demand made-up prices of up to 20,000 kyat from tourists. Even the so-called official fare of 11,000 kyat at the large taxi stand on 79th Street feels steep and hard to justify given today’s low fuel prices. But in recent years some Burmese — at least in the more touristy areas — seem to have developed a surprisingly brazen rip-off mentality, and it has become harder for foreigners to get fair prices for goods and services — which, in truth, is not very surprising when the government itself officially runs a two-tier pricing system where foreigners are expected to pay several times the local rate. Why wouldn’t private businesses try their luck as well?

## By bus, boat and train back to Mandalay

The flight to Myitkyina took a little over an hour and we landed in the afternoon at the provincial capital’s small airport in Kachin State. At the moment, with no permits for Putao being issued, Myitkyina is the northernmost point tourists can reach in Burma. From here it's not far to the foothills of the Himalayas, and beyond the eastern ridge lies Yunnan, with Dali and Lake Erhai. Although Myitkyina sits at only 150 metres, winter here is quite chilly, especially at night. Another noticeable difference from the rest of the country is that most Kachin are Christians rather than Buddhists, and there are more churches than temples in town.

Myitkyina is often described in guidebooks as unattractive and uninteresting, merely a starting point for trips down the Irrawaddy, but I rather liked it. The centre is dominated by a large, lively market, and the location on the bank of the broad Irrawaddy is very attractive. Myitkyina also feels relatively prosperous: the town is well kept and, by Burmese standards, fairly modern, yet beyond the standard Chinese concrete blocks there are also plenty of pretty older buildings along the shady avenues in the centre.

Since foreigners can reach Myitkyina only by plane, by an almost thirty-hour bone-jarring train ride, or by a boat trip that takes days, even in high season not that many Western travellers make it to town — and accommodation is expensive by local standards. At the friendly YMCA, the most popular traveller haunt in town, a simple room with two beds and a hot shower costs US$14.

The surroundings of Myitkyina are not especially spectacular, but there is a pleasant pagoda with hundreds of seated Buddha statues in a park near the airport, and the river landscape north of town is very beautiful in places, with beaches and swimming spots. We rented a Chinese motorbike for a day for 7,000 kyat. We had actually wanted to explore the area by bicycle, but the town’s only rental shop was asking 5,000 kyat for a rickety Chinese bike — a price not worth paying.

There’s a very nice restaurant in Myitkyina called River View, with — as the name suggests — a terrace looking out over the Irrawaddy. They have draught beer and simple but tasty Chinese dishes for around 2,000 kyat.

The plain Kashmir Restaurant served very cheap but perfectly decent biryanis and delicious lamb meatballs.

The bus to Bhamo was one of those irritating rip-offs that seem to have become increasingly common in Burma lately (we heard similar stories from travellers elsewhere). As foreigners, we had to pay 12,000 kyat for the six-hour journey (180 km), more than double the local price; complaining is pointless — if you do not pay, you do not go. The road to Bhamo started out as a rough but at least dust-free cobbled track; the last hour, though, was catastrophic, with crater-sized potholes and broken asphalt. The bus was old, rattling, and about as uncomfortable as possible — a typical provincial Burmese bus ride, but at a hefty price. The route was quite pretty, though, through the foothills of the border mountains towards China.

Bhamo is a remote provincial town and, as far as we could tell, has only a single hotel that is authorised to accommodate foreigners. At Friendship Hotel, somewhat worn rooms with a modest shared bathroom cost US$7 per person; for a double room with a private bathroom they demanded, quite seriously, US$25. The breakfast included in the price, however, was very good — a lavish buffet — as you’d expect in a mid-range hotel.

Bhamo is smaller, more traditional, and more Buddhist than Myitkyina. Strand Road along the Irrawaddy has a certain charm, with most buildings still made of wood. If you follow the riverside road north and turn left behind the prison, you reach Shwe Kyina Pagoda after about an hour, with a miniature version of the famous Golden Rock. The main attraction here, though, is the almost clichédly beautiful landscape, with brilliantly green rice fields along the Irrawaddy. A few minutes from the pagoda, a bamboo bridge — hundreds of metres long and built in a traditional style — leads to an island in the river. In the soft afternoon light the area looks absolutely glorious, and it makes for a wonderful walk — the perfect way to spend an afternoon in Bhamo. On the way back we had dinner at the simple Sein Sein on Strand Road, one of the few real restaurants in town, again with cheap Chinese-influenced food and draught beer. The pork paprika (1,900 kyat) was perfectly decent.

The next morning we took the 9 o’clock “speed boat” to Katha (15,000 kyat). Fast it certainly isn’t — the trip took a good seven hours — but it was a relaxed day and a very rewarding journey. Above all, the stretch before Shwegu, where the river narrows and is framed left and right by pagoda-studded mountains, is scenically stunning. Otherwise the Irrawaddy is broad and the landscape flat and dry, but along the way there are villages where time seems to have stood still.

The approach to Katha, with green chains of hills in the background, is the second highlight of this boat trip. The little town is the smallest, most traditional and most charming of the three “stops” along the Irrawaddy north of Mandalay. Despite its literary fame — Katha is the original setting of George Orwell’s Burmese Days — very few tourists spend the night here, and there are only a handful of simple places on the riverside road. Ayeyarwady Guesthouse, where we stayed, is still considered the best, even though a room (5,000 kyat per person) consists of no more than a bed and a bedside table, and in the shared bathroom there is no running water, let alone Western toilets.

As an Orwell fan, it is, of course, a must to visit the old English Club, which now serves as the headquarters of an agricultural cooperative. The people were reserved but polite. We were able to look around at our leisure and were even offered tea. The old prison, surrounded by a massive fortification wall, is still there as well.

Of course Katha has changed a great deal over the last eighty years; you'll look in vain for jungle in the immediate surroundings. These days, though, the place is very picturesque, and one of the most pleasant in the country, atmosphere-wise.

Unfortunately we did not find a single good restaurant. When the vegetables taste a bit musty, the soup is full of innards, and the pork curry consists mainly of fat, you know you are in a typical provincial place serving “Myanmar Food”. Luckily, though, Katha also has plenty of nice teashops with tasty snacks.

From Katha there were three ways back to Mandalay: about a fifteen-hour bus ride on a rattling local bus; the big ferry down the Irrawaddy, which at the moment runs on an erratic schedule because the water level is low, and takes a very long time; and finally the good old trains — though the nearest station is in Naba, 25 kilometres west of Katha. We chose the last option and took the 2 p.m. bus to Naba, which took a good hour to cover the short distance on an atrocious road.

Since you cannot reserve train tickets in Katha and we definitely wanted to travel Upper Class, we simply tried our luck as early as we could, because we had been told that four trains — at 4 p.m., 6 p.m., 8 p.m., and midnight — were supposed to arrive in Naba each day. The 4 p.m. train, unfortunately, did not exist at all; the 6 p.m. train was an hour and a half late and full; on the 8 p.m. train we were lucky enough to find two seats in Upper Class (US$20), and that one was even fairly punctual and the journey took only eleven hours.

We spent the five hours in Naba — as so often in Burma — waiting and drinking tea on the dusty station forecourt. Naba is one of those places the government would dearly like to keep off-limits to foreigners; if it didn’t have an important railway station, it almost certainly would be. The village is shockingly poor, wretched and run-down. Begging children with scabies hope for handouts from travellers. It’s a kind of poverty you, as a tourist in Burma, very rarely get to see.

## Mandalay and its surroundings

After a bumpy overnight train ride we arrived in Mandalay early in the morning and checked into E.T. Hotel again. After a good, cheap breakfast at the famous chapati stand (corner of 82nd/27th), we rented bicycles and set off to explore the city. We went down to the Irrawaddy waterfront first. There are plenty of basic teashops with no view, but further south there are also a few pleasant beer gardens with verandas. After that we visited Shwe In Bin Monastery, which lies in one of the prettiest neighbourhoods of an otherwise rather faceless city that seems to go on forever — one of the few areas where a short walk is genuinely worthwhile. From there we went a few kilometres south of the centre to Mahamuni Pagoda, then had lunch at Café Brolly, a fairly upmarket café-restaurant in a quiet area not far from the eastern palace walls. It served perfectly decent Thai and Western dishes at civilised prices (3,000–5,000 kyat), fresh fruit shakes, and had a shady courtyard — the ideal place for a midday break before visiting the pagodas around Mandalay Hill.

This is the part of Mandalay where the city finally lives up to its exotic name — and to whatever a romantic might want to associate with it. Splendid monasteries and pagodas sit close together here, away from the noise and the Chinese concrete blocks that dominate the rest of town. The views are magnificent, especially at sunset from the viewing platforms on Mandalay Hill.

Less pleasant was our encounter with the staff of the Ministry of Culture and Archaeology (or something along those lines). At the ticket booth, they refused to accept our US$20 note for the entrance tickets and tried to bar us from Atumashi Monastery — the only place where the “flat-rate ticket” is checked at that time of day. Since we really were willing to pay, we told them quite plainly that we would be visiting the sights regardless, with or without a ticket, and suggested they could call the police if they did not like it. In the end they did nothing, of course, and we were able to see everything included in the US$10 ticket free of charge.

The next day we rented a motorbike (12,000 kyat) to visit the former royal capitals of Sagaing and Inwa, more than 20 kilometres south of Mandalay on opposite sides of the river. It was a very good day out.

We first rode across the new Ayeyarwady Bridge, completed only a few months ago. This huge four-lane bridge is a complete oddity in a country whose infrastructure is otherwise so underdeveloped — but from the separate pedestrian and bicycle path you can stop for photos whenever you like and enjoy the fine views over Sagaing’s hills, lined with golden pagodas.

Sagaing is not only a quiet, picturesque excursion spot; it is also considered the spiritual centre of the country. The many monasteries in the surrounding area house countless monks and nuns. There are plenty of paths up to the viewpoint, and there’s absolutely no need to feed the government’s greed by paying the entrance fee. We simply parked the motorbike at a slightly more out-of-the-way monastery and took one of the stairways up. On the way we were intercepted by a monk who was critical of the regime who offered to accompany us. He had a lot to say and showed us some very rarely visited cave paintings from the 17th century — something we would never have found on our own. Since he spent so much time with us and flatly refused to accept any money for it, we offered to take him back to his monastery on the motorbike, and we exchanged email addresses.

In the afternoon we crossed back to the east bank via the old Ava Bridge and visited Inwa, another former royal capital. It is a little short on grand remains — but all the richer in atmosphere. In a vast area amid beautiful rice fields, between traditional villages, you keep coming across small gems: ancient stupas, unrestored teak monasteries, and even the “leaning tower of Inwa”, which you can climb for the view.

Back in Mandalay we enjoyed something that would have been unthinkable on my last visit six years ago: an air-conditioned shopping centre with escalators and a well-stocked supermarket where you could buy almost anything from Australian wine to French cheese. In the foreseeable future, however, this 78 Shopping Centre will likely play second fiddle — a gigantic new shopping mall is currently being built on the same street, on a scale you don’t even find in Rangoon. In Mandalay the Chinese influence is stronger, and modernisation is moving much faster.

Right next to 78 Shopping Centre there is an excellent lounge restaurant called JJ, with international food, a well-equipped bar, wonderful avocado shakes, and real espresso. Even if a place like this doesn’t exactly fit the tourist cliché of an underdeveloped, backward Burma, it was a welcome change after a week in the traditional north: large portions, moderate prices (2,000–5,000 kyat), and good cooking. Highly recommended: the king prawns — although among the local high society, the burgers seemed to be the clear favourite.
