# Eastern Europe
**July-August 2002**

A road trip through Eastern and Southeastern Europe, from Hungary and Romania through Bulgaria and Greece to Croatia and Venice.

---

## Day 1 – 21 July

Stuttgart — Passau — Vienna — Budapest

Today we drove straight through to Budapest in one go. In the evening we still managed a quick beer in the pricey Rio Club on the Danube embankment.

Overnight in the car, in a central spot near the Danube on the Buda side.



## Day 2 – 22 July

Budapest — Gyöngyös — Sirok

We spent today in Budapest until late afternoon.

In the morning we visited the castle and Buda’s medieval quarter; afterwards we strolled through Pest and had a good cappuccino at Café Mediteran on the pretty Ferenc tér.

Budapest is always worth a trip.

Around 6 p.m. we left Budapest heading east on the N3, then turned off in Gyöngyös onto Route 24. It took us through idyllic countryside to an old castle ruin on a hill about 20 km before Eger, where we parked the car on a plateau with a magnificent view.



## Day 3 – 23 July

Sirok — Eger — Nyiregyháza — Satu Mare — Sighet — Prislop Pass

Early in the morning we paid a visit to the lovely little Baroque town of Eger, then spent a long time driving through rather uninteresting scenery as far as the Romanian border. From there we drove via Satu Mare through the remote and very unspoilt Maramureş region and visited the wooden churches in beautiful Izatal, which are part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Then on over the Prislop Pass through alpine landscapes, and along a mountain stream on whose bank we spent the night.

The road conditions were far better than I’d expected in Romania. At least up here in the north, huge potholes are actually quite rare even on minor roads; the only thing that really demands concentration is the countless horse-drawn carts on the roads.



## Day 4 – 24 July

Prislop — Moldoviţa — Suceviţa — Humor — Voroneţ — Ostra — Bicaz — Sovata — Sighişoara

In the morning we looked at the famous painted monasteries of Bucovina, of which Suceviţa was the most impressive.

Then we carried on over Carpathian passes, past lakes, and through a wonderfully narrow gorge in Cheile Bicazului National Park to Sighişoara, a beautifully preserved medieval town, where we treated ourselves to a beer in the so‑called birthplace of Vlad Țepeş.



## Day 5 – 25 July

Dumbrăveni — Sibiu — Curtea de Argeş — Câmpulung — Bran — Râşnov — Poiana Braşov

In the morning we visited Sibiu’s beautiful medieval centre and had a cappuccino on Piaţa Mică. Then we drove on over a pass more than 2,000 metres high into the Făgăraş Mountains, and although the weather was poor, the landscape and the views were fantastic.

The route between Câmpulung and Bran was also very scenic; the famous Dracula Castle of Bran was, however, a bit of a disappointment. The castle itself does look quite pretty from the outside, but it only bears the name “Dracula Castle” for tourist purposes because it’s in Transylvania—and it certainly doesn’t justify the, for Romania, unusual tourist circus.

More interesting was the nearby fortress ruin of Râşnov, from which you had, on one side, a magnificent view of the forests of the Carpathians. Unfortunately the view of ugly industrial buildings in the west spoilt the illusion a bit.



## Day 6 – 26 July

Poiana Braşov — Braşov — Prejmer — Hărman — Braşov — Sinaia — Ploieşti — Buzău — Făurei — Balta Albă

In the morning we drove down from Poiana Braşov into town; even with rain and fog we still had lovely views of Kronstadt.

All the same, we initially gave Braşov a miss and visited the medieval fortified churches in two nearby old Saxon villages. From the outside, the fortifications at Hărman looked more impressive; far more interesting, though, was the older fortress church of Prejmer, whose interior was astonishingly well preserved.

At midday we then spent many hours in Braşov in miserable weather. Its dramatic medieval old town at the foot of the Carpathians was the most beautiful thing Romania had offered so far: narrow alleyways, a splendid main square and colourful, well‑kept rows of houses. Not surprisingly, Braşov—together with Bran—was also the most touristy place in Romania up to that point.

We had lunch (unfortunately in the rain) right on the main square at the lovely Sirena Gustari. The food was, as expected, simple, but the rabbit liver was okay and the meal—despite the prime location—was very cheap (8 euros for two people including starter and dessert).

Towards evening we drove through the lovely scenery of the Bucegi Mountains to the main resort town of Sinaia, which—like the other places around here—is beautifully situated but rather ugly and overbuilt, as the area is a recreation spot for big‑city Romanians. A little way outside Sinaia, though, lies the dreamy fairy‑tale Peleş Castle, which is absolutely worth a visit.

Then we made the mistake of driving on into the dark to escape the bad weather and pulled up on a meadow somewhere in the middle of nowhere in Wallachia. Some people with guns didn’t like that at all and made it very clear that we should find somewhere else to sleep—so we spent the night on a car park by the country road.



## Day 7 – 27 July

Balta Albă — Brăila — Tulcea — Mahmudia — Babadag — Mamaia

In the morning we drove to Tulcea, the starting point for excursions into the Danube Delta. If we’d taken the boat to the end point, Salina, we would have had to spend the night there, so we decided to drive as far as we could into the delta landscape by car. The area is very pretty and we had a lovely picnic by a branch of the Danube opposite Mahmudia.

In the afternoon we continued on to the Black Sea coast to Mamaia, a beach resort that can easily hold its own against those in Spain—except that here it’s basically only Romanians on holiday and the prices are very low. After the quiet atmosphere of central Romania it was a real culture shock, but also the perfect place to drink a few beers on the beach and watch the goings‑on.



## Day 8 – 28 July

Mamaia — Constanţa — Bucharest

We spent the morning in Constanţa, Romania’s second‑largest city. Constanţa is not really anything special, but it does have a few nice corners such as the harbour promenade and a park with Roman excavations.

After a good snack at the Lebanese place, we drove on to Bucharest, where we arrived in the early afternoon and visited the city’s main sights. Bucharest is far better than its reputation. True, the prefabricated housing estates stretch for miles through the outer districts and the city somehow feels grey, but there are also lovely parks, interesting historic buildings, the gigantic Palace of the Republic, and an old town full of great, cheap street cafés. Overall I liked the city’s atmosphere very much, at least at this time of year; the winters here must be very grey and depressing.

Today we wanted, for the first time, to spend the night in a guesthouse, have a shower and get some laundry done. ‘Elvis Villa’ is fairly central, in a lovely house in a quiet residential area. Unfortunately all the beds were taken and we were meant to sleep on mattresses in the TV room. As the room was used until late at night, we preferred to sleep in the car again. The facilities were very good, though, and our laundry was washed free of charge, so we could live with the 12 euros (which, by Romanian standards, is already quite steep).



## Day 9 – 29 July

Bucharest — Ruse — Veliko Tarnovo

We spent the morning in Bucharest because we had to wait for our laundry; then we drove on to the border. After paying an environmental tax (10 euros) and the fee for the Danube bridge (7 euros) on the Romanian side, we entered Bulgaria, where we paid 14 euros road tax and 2 euros for whatever‑it‑was. Otherwise the border crossing went smoothly.

Bulgarian roads are, surprisingly, not as good as the Romanian ones—but they’re not really bad either. We spent the evening in wonderfully situated Veliko Tarnovo, a pretty old town with lots of cafés and bars.



## Day 10 – 30 July

Tarnovo — Shumen — Varna

In the morning we visited Tarnovo’s impressive citadel (4 leva), and from its walls had splendid views of the town and the ravine‑ and valley‑shaped landscape.

In the afternoon we drove on to Varna, where its boulevards and parks had a wonderful summer festival atmosphere, so it hardly mattered that the city wasn’t exactly beautiful.



## Day 11 – 31 July

Varna — Sunny Beach — Nesebar — Burgas — Sozopol

After leaving Varna’s ugly harbour installations behind us, we drove through attractive coastal scenery to Sunny Beach, a resort with very high prices by Bulgarian standards and a 6 km beach that was unbelievably crowded. German and English are spoken here. Horrible!

Nesebar, on a peninsula directly south of Sunny Beach, is a beautiful place with old wooden houses and churches and wonderful views of the Black Sea. The only problem is its proximity to Sunny Beach, which means souvenir stalls and package tourists. Still, Nesebar was a highlight of the trip so far.

Also on a peninsula—and scenically even more beautiful—is Sozopol, a pretty holiday town for Bulgarians, with a festival vibe, lovely cafés along the great, narrow coastal path, and hardly any foreign tourists.



## Day 12 – 1 August

Sozopol — Burgas — Grudovo — Haskovo — Plovdiv

We drove on small roads through sparsely populated countryside from Sozopol to Plovdiv, where we spent the rest of the day.

Plovdiv was another pleasant surprise and a real insider tip. Apart from a lovely car‑free boulevard with plenty of street cafés, it’s above all the old town—still unrenovated in many places, with mosques and two ancient amphitheatres—that has enormous tourism potential. For now, though, tourists are still thin on the ground here.



## Day 13 – 2 August

Plovdiv — Smoljan — Mugla — Trigrad Canyon — Delčev — Bansko

Today was not only one of the most beautiful days in terms of scenery, but also the most adventurous day of the trip so far. On our way through the wild Rhodope Mountains we first visited Bachkovo Monastery, which wasn’t particularly impressive.

In Smoljan we then turned west onto a road that our map showed as a normal country road. The route was indeed correct—but it turned out to be drivable only in a 4x4. The first obstacle was boulders on the track, which we had to clear away together. Shortly afterwards the wooden bridge over the stream had collapsed, so we had to drive through the fairly deep water, and afterwards we lived in constant fear that the next bridge might collapse as well. The whole thing felt more like an off‑road adventure, and without four‑wheel drive we would have been stuck.

At some point we reached a tarmac road again, which took us to the spectacular Trigrad Canyon, a remote but popular hiking area.

Our destination for today was Bansko, a pretty old village at the foot of the Pirin Mountains. In winter it’s Bulgaria’s most popular ski resort; in summer, however, it’s very relaxed and quiet. For once we spent the night in a hostel again and had a good room with a spectacular mountain view for 10 leva per person.

## Day 14 – 3 August

Bansko — Rila — Melnik — Thessaloniki

From Bansko it was an hour and a half to Rila Monastery, the most impressively situated, largest, but also most touristy monastery we’d visited so far.

Our next—and for the time being last—stop in Bulgaria was Melnik, a pretty village with lots of tavernas that already had a strong Greek feel.

After spending almost two hours stuck in a queue on the Bulgarian side of the border in oppressive heat, things thankfully moved much faster with the well‑organised Greeks; and from the border it was only a hop to Thessaloniki, Greece’s second‑largest city, where we spent the evening and night.

Thessaloniki doesn’t offer all that many tourist sights, but it’s on the sea and has a fantastic selection of smart street cafés and bars along the seafront promenade, where you can have a very good time in a lovely atmosphere (once you’ve digested the initial shock at the horrendous prices: 3 euros for a small bottle of beer or a cappuccino).



## Day 15 – 4 August

Thessaloniki — Nea Moudania — Nikitas — Paleohori — Amfipoli — Drama — Kavala — Thassos

We spent today driving along the coast east of Thessaloniki. Unfortunately it was Sunday, so both the beaches and the roads were very busy. Apart from that, the coast is beautiful and the colour of the sea is simply dreamy.

We picnicked and swam in a pretty spot at the beginning of the Sithonia peninsula. Right behind it, between Ormos Panagias and the picturesque fishing village of Pirgadikia, began the most impressive stretch of coastline.

After some drama in Drama (the electric window mechanism broke), we reached Kavala in the evening, a spectacularly situated harbour town with a fort that looked very interesting at first glance. Unfortunately the car window wouldn’t go back up, so we couldn’t just park the car and explore the town. So we decided to catch the 8.30 p.m. ferry to Thassos (22.50 euros for three people plus the car, 1 hour 20 minutes), where we spent the night on the beach.



## Day 16 – 5 August

Thassos

Today, after repairing the window, we explored part of the island—though at a very leisurely pace. We followed the ring road to the main town of Thassos, then drove on via the pretty inland village of Panagia to Golden Beach, which—like many places in Greece—was beautiful but also very crowded.

Our next stop was Paradise Beach, which can only be reached via a steep, unpaved road.

The day’s highlight was Aliki, an especially lovely spot on an already very attractive coastline. We spent several hours here in the tavernas on a spit of land with two bays and a wonderful view of the turquoise‑blue sea.

We spent the night in a great, lonely bay between Aliki and Astis in the south of the island.



## Day 17 – 6 August

Thassos

Another day on Thassos. First we drove to Theologiko, a pretty, traditional village in the island’s interior.

Then we had a wonderful lunch in a taverna on the beach at Potos: stuffed vine leaves, peppers filled with sheep’s cheese, and fresh fish, at surprisingly low prices (17 euros for two).

After that we drove 17 km of bone‑shaking track to Kastro, an almost abandoned old village in the mountainous interior, whose only two residents run a small taverna for tourists in 4x4s.

In the afternoon we spent a few hours at the lovely, not‑too‑crowded Metallia Beach behind Limenaria, below an old fortress.

We spent the night on a beach near Thassos Town.



## Day 18 – 7 August

Thassos — Keramoti — Xanthi — Alexandroupoli — Istanbul

In the morning we took the ferry (15.50 euros) to Keramoti, from where a brand‑new motorway led right up to the Turkish border.

Once you’ve worked out the system at that border despite the chaos, processing is actually quite fast and straightforward (6 euros road tax). But because we were boxed in by lorries, the whole thing still took an hour.

Then on along the Sea of Marmara to Istanbul. The traffic here is a nightmare and I was in a cold sweat with nerves until we finally managed to park the car in a guarded car park in Sultanahmet (20 million lira for four days).

In the evening we only took a short walk through Istanbul’s wonderful old city, ate köfte and stuffed aubergines, and then smoked a water pipe and drank Turkish coffee at Café Mesala.

Overnight on the roof of the friendly Mavi GH in the old town for 5 euros including breakfast.



## Day 19 – 8 August

Istanbul

In the morning we took the boat across the Bosphorus to Üsküdar on the Asian side. From there we took a taxi to Istanbul’s highest point, where there were lovely views over the immense city.

From Üsküdar we took the boat back to the European side to Beşiktaş, where we wanted to visit Dolmabahçe Palace, but unfortunately it was closed today. So we walked up to Taksim Square and along the “pedestrian zone” back down to Galata Bridge, which spans the Golden Horn between historic and “new” Istanbul.

In the evening we had nargileh and Turkish coffee again at Mesale Restaurant, where dervishes danced today. Oh, brilliant!!



## Day 20 – 9 August

Istanbul

Today we spent a lot of money sightseeing in Sultanahmet. Luckily I had my student card with me, which cut the entrance fees by two‑thirds (!).

First we went to Süleymaniye Mosque, which looks especially impressive from a distance.

Back through the bazaar to Topkapı Palace (15 million / 5 million lira), a slightly disappointing tourist hell. The most interesting part would probably have been the harem, but an hour in the queue and another 5 million lira wasn’t worth it to us.

I found the famous Basilica Cistern very impressive (8 million / 4 million lira), as well as the 1,500‑year‑old, gigantic cathedral‑mosque‑museum Hagia Sophia (30 million / 10 million lira).

The last sight was free again: the Blue Mosque, by far the most visited in Istanbul; from the inside it wasn’t any more impressive than the city’s other large mosques.

We spent the evening in Beyoğlu, the centre of Istanbul’s great nightlife. First we had an excellent dinner and a bottle of Turkish wine at the wine bar Pano; afterwards we simply wandered aimlessly through the streets and alleys with all the countless pubs, cafés and clubs, before finishing late at night with a raki as a nightcap at the atmospheric live‑music Café Galata.



## Day 21 – 10 August

Istanbul

After all the sightseeing yesterday, we took our last day in Istanbul at an easy pace. We had ourselves washed and massaged in a hammam, and ate a cheap lunch in a small fast‑food place in a non‑touristy neighbourhood.

After chatting away the whole afternoon and evening in front of our guesthouse, we didn’t go to Beyoğlu again as planned; instead we rounded off the evening cosily with a water pipe and coffee at the nargileh place under Galata Bridge.

At 2 a.m. we drove on towards Bulgaria.



## Day 22 – 11 August

Istanbul — Edirne — Plovdiv — Koprivshtitsa — Sofia

Around 4 a.m. we crossed the Turkish–Bulgarian border, which took just under an hour and cost us 25 euros (20 euros road tax, 5 euros “disinfection”).

We continued to Koprivshtitsa, a historically important village with beautiful, traditional houses, cobbled lanes and museums explaining the Bulgarian resistance fighters of the 19th century. Definitely a highlight in Bulgaria—and, surprisingly, not even very touristy.

Good, cheap lunch at a restaurant.

In the afternoon we continued to Sofia, where the evening was ruined by the theft of the car radio.



## Day 23 – 12 August

Sofia

We had to spend today in Sofia too because of the paperwork connected to the theft, which, in retrospect, I don’t regret.

Sofia doesn’t have any major sights—nor even a nice shopping boulevard—but it’s still quite a friendly city with very pretty corners, especially around the city park and the National Theatre, plus fantastic cafés.

We also had a good, cheap Chinese lunch (huge portions for 5 leva).

In the evening we went to an atmospheric club in a disused secret printing press and drank cheap beer.



## Day 24 – 13 August

Sofia — Skopje

I spent the morning in Sofia getting acquainted with Bulgarian bureaucracy and various public buildings, which I had to visit with an interpreter in order to get the final stamp for the police report.

At midday we had a drink at the lovely café in front of the theatre and ate good Bulgarian food in the pleasant courtyard of Hotel Nicky.

After that we drove towards Skopje. The formalities at the Bulgarian–Macedonian border went quickly and smoothly (2.50 euros for disinfection); the waiting time in the queue, however, took about an hour, so we didn’t reach Skopje until around 6 p.m.

We spent the evening in cafés—both in the new town to the south and in the wonderful old town to the north of the river. This evening alone was enough to make Skopje one of the most interesting and fascinating cities of the trip so far.

The city has two completely different faces, which reflect the social conditions of a country that was affected by civil war not so long ago.

In the new “Macedonian” part they are currently trying to create a city with a western atmosphere—smart cafés and shopping malls—though in places it still felt a bit unfinished and chaotic.

The “Albanian” old town has a traditional, oriental flair, and the nightlife in the pubs and cafés was firmly in male hands.



## Day 25 – 14 August

Skopje — Tetovo — Ohrid

We spent a few more hours in Skopje today, and I didn’t have to revise my impressions from last night.

First we went up to the fortress above the old town, behind whose walls there was a nice, well‑kept park offering great views over the city and the impressive mountains surrounding Skopje.

Then we wandered through the lively bazaars of the old town, whose atmosphere is anything but European.

With a heavy heart we left this magnificent city at midday and drove on good roads through beautiful mountain scenery to Ohrid, where yet another highlight awaited us.

Ohrid is not only wonderfully situated on the lake of the same name, but also a UNESCO World Heritage Site—and it’s not hard to see why. Besides pretty old churches, there is a still‑used ancient amphitheatre, a citadel high on the hill with grand views, and wandering through the city’s narrow lanes is a joy.

Ohrid is also the Macedonians’ holiday resort, and in the evening there was a real festival atmosphere along the lakeside promenade and at the countless street cafés.

It’s beyond me why so few foreign tourists visit this place.



## Day 26 – 15 August

Ohrid — Sveti Naum — Ohrid — Bitola — Kozani — Grevena — Kalambaka

After a refreshing morning swim in the lake, we drove along the shore, accompanied by gorgeous scenery, to Sveti Naum Monastery on the Albanian border, from whose walls there were wonderful views.

Below the monastery there is an idyllic lagoon with a restaurant, where we had tasty—but slightly pricey—trout (400 denar).

We continued through the typically beautiful, empty mountain landscape of Macedonia to Bitola, the country’s second‑largest city. Its long, lively pedestrian zone with restored rows of houses and street cafés makes it well worth a stop. The ruins of Heraclea in the industrial district are probably only interesting for amateur archaeologists.

From Bitola it was only a hop to the Greek border, which we crossed quickly and without problems.

The area near the Macedonian border is dull and mostly industrial. From Grevena onwards, though, the scenery becomes very beautiful, and we found a dreamy place to swim in the little canyon of the turquoise‑green Venetikos.

When it was already getting dark we reached Kalambaka, a touristy but pleasant town with lots of cafés and restaurants, dramatically situated below the bizarre rock formations of Meteora.



## Day 27 – 16 August

Kalambaka — Ioannina — Monodendri

We got up extra early to avoid the tourist hordes that one of Europe’s most extraordinary and impressive sights inevitably attracts.

The monasteries of Meteora are built on the peaks of volcanic rock formations and have even served as a backdrop for a Bond film.

From Meteora it was 140 km through the mountains to Ioannina, the area’s only large city.

The quiet, pretty old town enclosed by walls, and its setting on a lake surrounded by mountains, made Ioannina a worthwhile destination.

On the lakeshore there are plenty of restaurants; in one of them I had a tasty seafood salad with king prawns for a cheap 4.70 euros.

Our last destination in Greece was the very beautiful, but somewhat touristy, mountain village of Monodendri. For a place this remote, it draws plenty of tourists; the reason is the impressive Vikos Canyon right on its doorstep.

From the old monastery you have a wonderful view into the 900‑metre‑deep gorge, and there is a great—but unfortunately quite short—hiking trail along the cliffs.

After an expensive frappé in a lovely taverna in Monodendri, we drove a few more kilometres in the evening to just before the Albanian border.



## Day 28 – 17 August

Kakavijë — Gjirokastër — Sarandë — Butrint — Sarandë

Crossing into Albania went smoothly, though it involved a total of 45 minutes’ waiting around.

As Germans, we were surprisingly given the visa for Albania free of charge.

From the border a new road led straight to Gjirokastër, a pretty historic little town below a massive fort.

Visiting the fort cost 200 lek and, apart from the mildly interesting military museum, offered lovely views over Gjirokastër and the surrounding mountains.

We continued on a narrow, bumpy mountain road to Sarandë, a typical Mediterranean holiday resort for Albanians, where we also spent the night.

After a frappé on the seafront promenade, we first drove another 20 km south to the ruins of Butrint (700 lek entrance).

The ruins of the ancient city are a World Heritage Site and in places still really well preserved.

From the old fort above the site you have great views over the ruins and the sea.

Back in Sarandë we enjoyed the lively mood in the evening on the lovely seafront promenade.



## Day 29 – 18 August

Sarandë — Vlorë — Durrës — Tirana

The Ionian coast—also called the Flower Riviera—between Sarandë and Vlorë is beautiful, non‑touristy and in places very spectacular, with high mountains that only end at long, lonely sandy beaches.

The prettiest part of the coast is shortly before the pass, where there was also a café with a wonderful panoramic terrace.

The character of the “national road” adds a special flair as well: it seems not to have been repaired since the Second World War and winds spectacularly through the coastal mountains.

The many crosses by the roadside made it obvious that the road—often running right along the drop—is really too narrow for two vehicles.

From Vlorë to Durrës the landscape is rather boring; the road was a bit better, though, so we made good time.

Durrës itself isn’t pretty, but it’s a fairly relaxed harbour town with Byzantine city walls and a rather crumbling amphitheatre where cows graze.

From Durrës the only motorway in Albania leads to the capital, Tirana.

Tirana doesn’t really have any major sights, but it’s still a friendly, pleasant city with the spacious Skanderbeg Square in the centre and many avenues with pretty street cafés and bars.

What sets Tirana apart a little from other cities is the bright paintwork on the buildings in the centre, which successfully takes the sterility out of socialist architecture.

Tirana’s nightlife is fairly lively even on Sundays, and the many smart cafés were absolutely packed with young people.

If someone had told me that before this trip, I’d probably have taken it for a joke—but by now nothing surprises me anymore.



## Day 30 – 19 August

Tirana — Kukës — Prizren

Although it’s only 200 km from Tirana to Kukës, the drive took over six hours.

In places the road was in a miserable condition, but the rugged mountain landscape was often magnificent, and it was fascinating to drive through one of the most disadvantaged and poorest regions of Europe.

Just two years ago the road through Albania’s mountains was extremely dangerous, but thanks to strong military and police presence that has apparently improved considerably.

The only thing that’s still life‑threatening now is the road itself.

Kukës is beautifully situated, but a sterile, unfriendly little hole, with the lowest prices in all of Albania.

Crossing from Albania into Kosovo went smoothly. The Albanians demanded 4 euros road tax.

For Kosovo, under a UN regulation, you had to buy expensive supplementary insurance for the car (42 euros) and pay another 2.50 euros for disinfection; otherwise everything was quick.

Prizren was one of the positive surprises of the trip: a beautiful town divided by a river, with an intact old town, countless pubs and cafés, and interesting architecture.

The traces of the war were really only noticeable in the strong KFOR presence, and for a Monday evening there was an incredible amount going on in town.



## Day 31 – 20 August

Prizren — Pejë — Podgorica — Cetinje

In the morning we drove from Prizren to Pejë, a town that the war had hit much harder.

The historic old town must have been almost completely destroyed, as nearly three years after the end of the war they were still busy rebuilding the houses.

The power supply must also have been problematic, because throughout the city you could hear the noise of generators.

From Pejë it went steeply up into the mountains towards Montenegro—another quick, low‑bureaucracy border crossing in the middle of nowhere (1 euro for disinfection).

Montenegro greeted us with grand mountain scenery and rural tranquillity.

The road led through green valleys, mostly along rivers.

The last 50 km before Podgorica went through an impressive canyon and many tunnels, always along a turquoise river.

Podgorica is Montenegro’s capital and a rather boring place planned on a drawing board, though with lots of parks and street cafés that add a bit of life.

In the evening we continued on to Cetinje, Montenegro’s beautifully situated old capital.

These days the town is very laid‑back, with many green avenues, historic buildings and a pleasant pedestrian zone.



## Day 32 – 21 August

Cetinje — Kotor — Dubrovnik

From Cetinje we drove down a hair‑raising winding road through dreamy scenery—plus even better views of the coast—to Kotor, on the impressive fjord of the same name.

Kotor is not only spectacularly situated, but also has a well‑preserved old town completely surrounded by city walls.

Because Dubrovnik is nearby, Kotor is somewhat overshadowed.

As far as the Croatian border, the road runs directly along the coast, with pretty little towns and fine views again and again.

The border crossing from Montenegro into Croatia was no problem at all, and Dubrovnik was not far.

When you look down from above onto the walled old town, you can hardly believe such a picture‑book city can exist—and the worst thing is: seen from the inside, and from the city walls, Dubrovnik is just as impressive.

After the war, the tourist masses have long since returned; still, in some lanes away from the main drag you can walk completely alone.

We spent the late evening having cocktails at La Bohème, one of the most relaxed and cosy open‑air bars I’ve ever seen.



## Day 33 – 22 August

Dubrovnik — Neum — Mostar — Sarajevo

The drive along the Dalmatian coast offers beautiful coastal scenery with many offshore islands; unfortunately the weather was exceptionally bad today.

After Neum we left the coast and drove into Bosnia and Herzegovina.

The landscape between Mostar and Sarajevo is very pretty and idyllic, but the scars of the war are still visible: destroyed houses or buildings riddled with bullet holes stand by the roadside.

This is particularly true of Sarajevo, which the war damaged quite badly in places.

Nevertheless, the city is attractive again, with its historic old town and oriental bazaar; a large part of the centre is pedestrianised.

We couldn’t fully enjoy the city’s beauty, though, because it’s hard to enjoy it when, in the heart of Europe, you see young people walking around with war injuries and many buildings full of bullet holes.

We ate very well in the evening at Americana Restaurant: tasty Bosnian food (stuffed vegetables, shish kebab), not exactly cheap though (10 euros per person).

Lovely evening atmosphere in the street cafés of the pedestrian zone.



## Day 34 – 23 August

Sarajevo — Mostar — Ploče — Drvenik — Hvar

On our way back to the Croatian coast we visited Mostar, which before the war was certainly one of the most beautiful places in south‑eastern Europe.

Unfortunately both the Serbs and the Croats did quite a bit of damage here, even blowing up the medieval bridge, the heart of the town.

But despite the clearly visible traces of war, Mostar’s old town—on both banks of the deep‑blue Neretva—is once again a romantic place, with wonderful café terraces on the riverside slopes, and the bridge is being rebuilt.

From the Croatian seaside town of Drvenik we took the car ferry to the island of Hvar (92 kuna for two people plus the car).

From the eastern end of the island, where our ferry arrived, we drove about 80 km along the narrow island road to Hvar Town.

By Croatian standards the island is quite green, but its nickname “the Madeira of Croatia” is pure exaggeration.

There are relatively few easily accessible swimming spots, and the few that exist were very crowded.

Hvar Town is a wonderful medieval place, overlooked by a fortress with a splendid view of the old town and the yacht harbour.

In the evening the mood along the harbour promenade was a well‑balanced mix of sophisticated and casual, which I liked a lot.



## Day 35 – 24 August

Hvar — Stari Grad — Split

After one last swim on Hvar, in a beautiful, fairly secluded pebble bay south of the tunnel, we took the ferry to Split in the morning (280 kuna, two hours).

We spent the rest of the day pottering about Split’s lovely old town, eating ice cream and drinking cappuccino.

Naturally we also visited the magnificent Palace of Diocletian and strolled leisurely through the narrow lanes.

In the evening we had a good, expensive meal at the pretty Kod Joze: grilled fish (25 euros), octopus salad, grilled mushrooms, in a refined atmosphere.



## Day 36 – 25 August

Split — Trogir — Pirovac — Šibenik

In the morning we had a cappuccino in Trogir, another medieval picture‑book town on the coast, and then continued along the coastal road to Pirovac, where we visited Toni.

Pirovac itself is a boring family resort, but the surroundings—Murter in particular—are stunning: little islands in deep‑blue sea as far as the eye can see, and pretty (if touristy) holiday towns.

Toni’s family looked after us wonderfully and we got a super‑cute room for the night.

In the evening we drove on to Šibenik, the biggest city in the area. Its lovely waterfront promenade and historic old town are hidden behind the prefabricated blocks and factories on the main road, so when we drove through on the way to Pirovac we thought the town was dreadful—which is completely untrue.

At night we went to a huge open‑air club near Pirovac which, unfortunately, was almost empty due to the season (they shut on 24 August).



## Day 37 – 26 August

Pirovac — Zadar — Plitvice — Zagreb

After spending the night—properly—for the first time in ages, and consequently sleeping in outrageously late, we set off around midday towards Zagreb.

In the afternoon we stopped at the Plitvice Lakes, but decided not to pay the 90 kuna entrance fee and only sneaked as far as the viewpoint platform, from where there was a postcard view of the canyon with its turquoise‑green pools.

The area was swarming with German tourists; in spring or autumn it must be a wonderful place.

We spent the evening in Zagreb, a very attractive big city whose historic centre has an almost village‑like character—making Croatia’s capital rather unique.

The evening atmosphere in the pedestrian zone was great: the perfect place to round off the day with cocktails in one of the nice cafés.

Before that we also had a decent Indian meal at Maharaja, in a quiet old‑town lane.



## Day 38 – 27 August

Zagreb — Ljubljana — Bled

Today we were properly inspected at a border for the first time.

Slovenian customs officers turned the whole car upside down and even used a sniffer dog.

Naturally they found nothing, which seemed to annoy them enormously.

After this harassment we spent a few hours in Ljubljana’s very pretty old town, which doesn’t feel eastern at all, but rather very Germanic.

We climbed up to the castle above the old town and drank cappuccinos in various lovely cafés in the pedestrian areas.

In the evening we drove on into the Julian Alps to Lake Bled and felt entirely as if we were in Bavaria or Austria.

The lake, with the castle perched above it, is a visual dream; Bled itself, however, is rather stuffy and sterile.

So after a Chinese dinner we bought beer and went down to the lake.

There you could sit on the landing stage in one of the wooden boats and drink.

Very atmospheric!



## Day 39 – 28 August

Bled — Bohinj — Bled — Kranj — Škofja Loka — Ljubljana

After being woken at 6.30 a.m. by a grim park ranger who told us that sleeping in the car in the national park was forbidden and that we had ten minutes to clear off, we drove to Bohinj, a larger lake beautifully situated below high mountains.

There we took a short walk to the not‑all‑that‑impressive Savica Waterfall.

Our swim in the lake was interrupted again by a park ranger, who this time told us that parking by the lake outside the designated paid car parks was forbidden.

As beautiful as Slovenia is, the regulations here are really dreadful.

At midday we then took a genuinely lovely short walk through Vintgar Gorge to a waterfall—which, naturally, like everything here, cost money.

After that we drove back towards Ljubljana.

On the way back we also visited the pretty little towns of Kranj and Škofja Loka, though after everything we’d already seen they couldn’t really excite us anymore.

At least in Kranj there was a great ice‑cream café with a terrace view.

We spent the evening in Ljubljana, where we first filled ourselves up at the tasty salad buffet at Pizzeria Foculus and then spent the rest of the evening hanging out in the brilliant Makalonca pub under one of the river bridges, where a glass of drinkable merlot cost just 70 cents.



## Day 40 – 29 August

Ljubljana — Škocjan — Piran — Trieste — Portogruaro

We left Ljubljana with heavy hearts towards Slovenia’s karst region, where we visited the impressive Škocjan Caves on a 90‑minute tour (5 euros).

The caves are impressive above all because inside one of the chambers there is a 90‑metre‑deep canyon through which the River Reka forces its way.

I’d never seen anything like that before.

The karst landscape around the caves is also very beautiful.

We spent the last hours in Slovenia in the pleasant, relaxed old town of Piran, a popular seaside resort near the Italian border.

We ate grilled squid one last time and enjoyed the late‑season atmosphere.

In the afternoon we crossed the border to Trieste, an industrial port city with a fairly attractive centre that you can wander around at ease—and the café prices here are realistic.

As we didn’t want to spend the night in a big city again, we drove on towards Venice in the evening and, by chance, found the wonderful little town of Portogruaro, whose old town consists entirely of rows of houses with colonnades.

The town also has a leaning campanile, romantic water wheels on the stream, and lovely, cheap cafés.

La dolce vita—and without the tourist circus.



## Day 41 – 30 August

Portogruaro — Mestre — Venice — Castelfranco — Borgo

Once we reached Mestre, we parked the car for free near the station and took the train to Venice.

Venice is exactly the cliché you imagine, and after so many days on the move it managed to get me properly excited again.

It’s unbelievable that such a city can exist: no cars, fairy‑tale architecture, all those canals, gondolas and piazzas in such a small space.

What I hadn’t expected was how easy it was, even in August, to escape the tourist masses in Venice.

There are enough quiet residential neighbourhoods that are beautiful and where you can stroll almost alone.

Visiting the Doge’s Palace was worthwhile, as you could see all the rooms in which so many decisions were made in the Middle Ages.

In the evening we had Chinese food by a canal in Cavareggio.

The food was really bad, but the setting was fantastic and it was cheap.

Back in Mestre we drove another 100 km or so towards Austria.



## Day 42 – 31 August

Borgo — Bolzano — Innsbruck — Füssen — Stuttgart

We took the Brenner motorway across the Alps to Innsbruck, where we stopped for lunch.

The town has a fantastic setting, directly below a high chain of Alpine mountains, and the old town is rich in beautiful houses and tourists from all over the world.

Then on over the Fern Pass through wonderful Alpine scenery to the two Bavarian fairy‑tale royal castles, Hohenschwangau and Neuschwanstein, where, because of the rainy weather, we only lingered briefly.

Motorway back to Stuttgart.
