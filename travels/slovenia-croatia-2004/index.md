# Slovenia & Croatia
**2 – 9 June 2004**

A road trip from Germany through the Austrian Alps to Slovenia and the Croatian coast, returning via Istria and northern Italy.

---

2 June 2004 - 9 June 2004

## Day 1 — 2 June

Amid the beautiful Alpine scenery around Berchtesgaden, I made my first
stop. Unfortunately the weather was pretty awful, and it didn’t improve
when I reached Salzburg either: grey and rainy. I still had a look
around the old town, which would no doubt have been lovely in sunshine.

Salzburg is hugely touristy, which must be Mozart’s fault, because the
baroque old town is certainly pretty and picturesque, but in Europe
there are dozens of more beautiful cities. Salzburg is dominated by the
fortress perched on a hill, with the old town spreading out on both
sides of the river below. The part directly under the fortress is where
the tourists congregate; the original quarter on the other side of the
river seemed more interesting at first glance, and it also had a few
very good pubs and wine taverns.

Because of the weather I decided to take the motorway south. It runs
through the Tauern Tunnel and past splendid Alpine scenery. Today’s
destination was Klagenfurt, Haiderland, in other words. I was expecting
a boring, buttoned-up provincial town, but was pleasantly surprised.
Klagenfurt is cosy and very pretty. The baroque centre is full of
pavement cafés; even on a weekday there was plenty going on, and
everyone was very friendly.

I had dinner at the Mexican place La Vista: a pleasantly furnished,
friendly restaurant. The food was fine and fairly inexpensive: €12 for a
mixed starter platter, quesadillas and Apfelschorle. I spent the night
in a quiet street on the shore of the Wörthersee.

## Day 2 — 3 June

After the drive over the spectacular Loibl Pass I reached Slovenia.
First I drove to Lake Bled, the place the word “picturesque” was surely
invented for: a small emerald-green lake with a little island, framed by
mountains, and high above it a medieval castle.

On to Bohinj, a far larger and more unspoilt alpine lake, ringed by even
higher, more impressive mountains. Then, on narrow little roads, I drove
through the Pokljuka ski area via Lubenice to Kranjska Gora. Against
this glorious mountain backdrop began the breathtaking pass road down
into the Soča Valley.

The Soča is the most turquoise river I have ever seen, a popular
rafting area, and stunningly beautiful. I continued through non-touristy
country on lonely little roads to Škofja Loka, a pleasant town near
Ljubljana, where I had an espresso on the lovely old-town square.

In the afternoon I took the motorway to Ptuj in eastern Slovenia. It is
arguably the prettiest little town in the country, with a neat old town
on the river below the ruins of a castle. What I particularly liked was
that Ptuj felt less polished than other Slovenian places and therefore
gave off more of an Eastern European vibe.

In the evening I went on to Maribor, Slovenia’s second-largest city.
Maribor isn’t a tourist town, and it’s not the most attractive place
either, but the pretty old town, largely pedestrianised, was well
worth a visit. Overnight at a motorway service area between Maribor and
Ljubljana.

## Day 3 — 4 June

In the morning I drove on to Ljubljana and checked into Hostel Celica.
This unusual hostel used to be a prison and the cells had been converted
into rooms. A bed in a triple room cost €22, not exactly cheap, but it
was very clean.

The hostel also offered unusual things such as a funky shisha café,
where I spent the morning with cappuccino and a water pipe, as the
weather still wasn’t good enough for an old-town tour. Around midday the
sun came out and I headed into the city. The hostel was only a
ten-minute walk from the centre.

Ljubljana is one of Europe’s prettiest capitals: a cosy, Central
European little city with a beautiful old town on both sides of the
river, and the city’s castle enthroned high above on the hill.

When it comes to cafés and restaurants, Ljubljana can hold its own
against much bigger cities; in practice the entire old town and the
riverbank are one long promenade lined with outdoor dining. Even on a
not-very-warm early-summer evening, the atmosphere in the city is
terrific. Another nice thing about Ljubljana is that you can explore the
whole old town on foot in a few hours.

I treated myself to a delicious dinner at the upmarket Pri sv.
Florijanu, in a very pretty, quiet corner of the old town at the end of
Gornji Trg. What was special about the place was that it offered both
Slovenian and Moroccan specialities. So I went for a Moroccan starter
platter, and for main course a tender chicken fillet with Mediterranean
vegetables.

Later I had a beer in the old town with an Australian from the hostel.

## Day 4 — 5 June

In the morning I drove to the coast to Piran, the prettiest place on
Slovenia’s short Adriatic coastline. A medieval little town and holiday
resort on a tight, small peninsula, with a lovely piazza and a small
harbour. Unlike the Croatian coastal towns, Piran is quite a bright,
colourful place.

Then over the Croatian border and into the interior of the Istrian
peninsula, where countless small medieval towns perch on hilltops.
Particularly pretty was Motovun, the truffle capital par excellence. It
was a strenuous walk all the way up to the old town, but absolutely
worth it for the panoramic view across Istria and the town’s atmosphere,
which has remained almost unchanged for decades; truffle restaurants
tempted on every corner, though I resisted.

Back to the sea and on to Poreč, one of Croatia’s most popular holiday
spots. Elsewhere, the handsome old town, with its pleasant cafés
enlivening the pedestrian zone, would be a real highlight; here in
Croatia the place falls more into the category of: quite nice for a
short stroll and a coffee break.

I spent the evening in Rovinj, Istria’s highlight and, after Dubrovnik,
arguably Croatia’s prettiest little coastal town. The old town sits
almost like a sphere on a peninsula; stairways and narrow alleys lead
through it, there’s a wonderful harbour promenade and superb cafés and
restaurants, some of them perched on the rocks along the coast.

In terms of places to stop (quantity, but also quality and variety)
Rovinj is simply top of the class. I had my cocktails (45 kuna) at the
excellent, outrageously comfortable Zanzibar to chill-out music. For
dinner I went to the very pretty Konoba Veli Joze, outside with a sea
view. Delicious stuffed squid for 55 kuna, plus a glass of Croatian red
wine.

## Day 5 — 6 June

I had my morning coffee in Pula, Istria’s largest city. It isn’t
outstandingly attractive, but thanks to its magnificently preserved
amphitheatre right in the centre, and its fairly pleasant old town, it’s
well worth a visit.

Around midday I drove on to Lubin on Istria’s east coast. The town lies
a few kilometres inland on a hill and, with its lovely old town and
views over the islands of Kvarner Bay, is the prototype of Istria’s
medieval hill towns. Here, however, there are also industrial facilities
and prefab housing estates in the lower town.

From Brestovo I took the ferry (70 kuna for the car, 16 kuna per person)
to the island of Cres in Kvarner Bay. The crossing took only 20 minutes
and I reached a wild no-man’s-land in the mountainous, rocky desert of
the island’s north: an almost unreal, very inhospitable landscape.

In contrast, the town of Cres is a delightful little place on a
deep-blue bay with a tempting harbour promenade. Here I met Achim and
Janine. We continued over a swing bridge to the much greener island of
Losinj, a very popular holiday island with the twin towns of Mali and
Veli Losinj.

Veli is the smaller of the two: a quiet, romantic old place on a
dreamily beautiful little natural harbour, whose promenade lets you dine
or drink coffee right by the water. Mali is larger and more touristy
than Veli, but also a storybook harbour town, with more gelaterias than
anywhere else on earth; surprisingly, there are very few restaurants.

We rented a pleasant private apartment in Mali for two days for €40,
with a kitchen and terrace.

## Day 6 — 7 June

We drove back to the island of Cres, to Lubenice, a tiny old mountain
village 300 metres above the coast, reachable only via a hair-raisingly
narrow road. From here you could walk down to two picture-perfect coves.

We first went left at the fork and, across a field of scree, reached a
deserted pebble cove (before the day-trippers arrived by boat) with
wonderfully cool, clear water. After a quick swim we went back to the
fork and turned right towards Lubenice’s main beach, which from above
had looked like a white, crescent-shaped dream against the indescribably
blue sea.

Hardly anyone was there, though from below the beach wasn’t quite as
fantastic as the other cove; still, a genuine postcard scene, with the
village of Lubenice perched high above on the edge of the cliff.

The walk back up took a full hour and was extremely sweaty, by no means
a leisurely stroll. In the village we ate grilled squid and a tasty
cold-cuts platter at the pretty (and only) konoba.

## Day 7 — 8 June

Broken up by a coffee stop in Cres Town and a short ferry crossing, we
drove from Losinj to Krk Town. The main town of the island of the same
name is a typical Croatian harbour town with a pretty little old centre
and a few cafés on the harbour promenade. Nothing extraordinary, but
certainly worth a longer lunch break.

Then we drove to Baska at the southern end of Krk. If the rest of the
island is already barren and rocky, this is where it gets extreme. A
spectacular long, crescent-shaped beach enclosed by an impressive
wilderness of bare rock. Baska is also a very pleasant little town, and
the water is simply crystal-clear.

Unfortunately, even in early June the beach was hopelessly overcrowded,
so we decided to look for another spot for a swim. We made a detour to
the little place of Vrh, beautifully situated on a hill above the sea,
and then continued to the north-west coast where, near Njivice, lots of
tiny “private pebble coves” invited you to swim, even if the water
quality here no longer seemed quite as good.

We crossed the Krk Bridge back to the mainland. Beyond Rijeka the
Opatija Riviera begins. Stately villas line a Mediterranean landscape
that, for Croatia, is unusually gentle. Opatija is a splendid, grand
little place with a very mixed crowd, from British retirees to Croatia’s
party youth; everyone enjoys life in Opatija, which is still
astonishingly inexpensive.

We took a pleasant private room right in the centre for €12 per person
and had dinner at Sveti Jakov, one of the town’s chicest restaurants, in
Hotel Millennium. Stylish, atmospheric surroundings under arcades, and
very good fish dishes. Scallops (22 kuna each), grilled squid (65 kuna),
truffle risotto (90 kuna) and for dessert cheese from the island of Pag
(60 kuna).

## Day 8 — 9 June

Back towards Germany via the pretty town of Portogruaro. At lunchtime we
spent a few more hours in Venice, then drove into the Alps and spent the
night at a lovely campsite (€17.50) on the Adige between Bolzano and
Meran.
