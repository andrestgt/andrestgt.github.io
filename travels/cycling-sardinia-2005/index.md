# Cycling Sardinia
**June 2005**

A cycling tour around the Mediterranean island of Sardinia.

---


**16 June – 8 July 2005**

## **Day 1 – 16 June – 44 km**

**Olbia – Porto Cervo – Baia Sardinia**

We arrived at Olbia airport around midday and, after a cappuccino, set
off immediately for the Costa Smeralda. We left Olbia behind and, after
about 15 kilometres, reached the start of the coastal road towards Porto
Cervo. The road rarely ran right along the shoreline; most of the time
it wound through the inland hills, with the occasional viewpoint
offering glorious views of the beautiful, jagged coast and its white
sandy bays, though you could usually reach them only via long, dusty
tracks.

There were hardly any flat sections. More often than not, a long
descent was followed by an equally long climb. Since the Costa Smeralda
is still one of Europe’s most exclusive holiday areas, there were at
least plenty of smart cafés with panoramic views for a break. My
favourite was Belvedere in Abbiadori, perched high above the coast. It
had comfy sofas, and the cappuccino cost a scandalous €2. We didn’t stop
to explore Porto Cervo, the main town and millionaire rendezvous of the
Costa Smeralda, because it was already getting late and the next campsite
was still a long way off. But as the route continued relentlessly up and
down, we eventually decided to take a small, far-too-expensive room
(€100) at Hotel Il Gemmello in Baia Sardinia. Still, breakfast the next
morning was, by Italian standards, very good, and included in the price.

## **Day 2 – 17 June – 60 km**

**Baia Sardinia – Cannigione – Palau – Santa Teresa**

Today was another constant rollercoaster: first along the very beautiful
Golfo di Arzachena, then over Capo d’Orso with superb views across the
Maddalena archipelago to Palau, a very busy tourist town, though not
especially attractive otherwise.

appealing. The town sits on a headland high above the sea, and from its
viewpoint terrace you get fantastic views of Corsica’s famous south

coves, before the road left the immediate coastline near Vignola and
ran, without shade, for 20 kilometres through complete solitude
inland, mostly uphill, until the village of Padduleddu, a little over 300
metres up.

o’clock. The first destination was Castelsardo, a delightful little
place dramatically set on a rocky promontory high above the coast, with

## **Day 5 – 20 June – 72 km**

**Marina di Sorso – Porto Torres – Fertilia – Alghero**

For once, the day felt fairly relaxed. The remaining kilometres to
Porto Torres, an unappealing port town, were mostly flat, and the spur
road through the interior towards Alghero was only gently hilly, though
the scenery wasn’t particularly interesting. Alghero, on the other hand,
is certainly one of Sardinia’s biggest highlights. Framed by gorgeous
white-sand beaches, the picturesque old town sits on a headland,
surrounded by fortification walls.

of the island’s major tourist centres. We arrived in the early afternoon
and stayed at the town campsite, about two kilometres outside the centre
on the coast. Despite the undemanding route today, Achim decided to
abandon the tour and fly back the next day with Ryanair. We still
explored the old town at length, had an aperitif at Mirador on the
walls, and ate an indulgent dinner at Il Macchiavello, good food served
with a magnificent view from the ramparts over the Gulf of Alghero and the
setting sun (Antipasti Sardi, Zupetta di Cozze, swordfish carpaccio and
horse steak (€10), plus seafood risotto with porcini (€22 for two)).

## **Day 6 – 21 June – 58 km**

**Alghero – Bosa – Turas**

So today I continued alone, and looking at the route profile, I began to
wonder whether I should have flown back as well. Fifty kilometres of
breathtaking coastal road through a sun-blasted emptiness, a true
rollercoaster of steep climbs, long descents and grand panoramas,
eventually brought me to Bosa: a truly pretty, laid-back little town on
the River Temo, a few kilometres inland, with a palm-lined riverside
promenade and a charmingly shabby old town that isn’t overly touristy.

## **Day 7 – 22 June – 86 km**

**Turas – Cuglieri – Oristano – Marina di Arborea**

Today began with a long climb that went on for miles up to
Cuglieri, a mountain village at 500 metres. Apart from an authentic,
tourist-free atmosphere, it didn’t have much else to offer.

## **Day 8 – 23 June – 71 km**

**Arborea – Guspini – Arbus – Portixeddu**

The first 20 kilometres to Guspini still ran across the Arborea
plain, nice and flat, before a climb of almost the same length began over
the Arburese mountains: first to the mountain village of Arbus, then
down a little again, and then steadily up over a pass just under 500
metres high, almost without shade, in the merciless midday heat.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSarde05-0140.jpg" alt="Around Arbus">
  <em>Around Arbus</em>
</figure>

Shortly
before total physical collapse, I thankfully reached the top and
promised myself never again to ride a pass in the midday heat. The long
descent down to the coast was wonderful, though, just like the landscape
in this region, which I hadn’t been able to appreciate on the way up. I
stayed the night at the small Camping Ortus di Mari in Portixeddu.

## **Day 9 – 24 June – 97 km**

**Portixeddu – Nebida – Portoscuso – Carloforte (ferry) – Calasetta
(ferry) – Sant’Antioco – Porto Pino**

After breakfast, another brute of a climb was waiting for me: the
coastal road to Nebida, just as beautiful as it was punishing, another
500-metre climb over a short distance, with sections of gradients that
bordered on the absurd. Despite some unattractive mining facilities, it
ran through truly magnificent scenery.

so of course I couldn’t resist trying Tonno alla Carloforte in one of

tasty. The town itself is really lovely too, with colourful pastel

## **Day 11 – 26 June – 43 km**

**Santa Margherita – Pula – Cagliari**

Today was Sunday, so it was pleasantly easy riding the 40-odd kilometres
into Cagliari, because, so it seemed, every single city-dweller was
driving in the opposite direction, towards the beaches. I arrived in
Cagliari around 11 o’clock and took a room at Albergo La Palma on the
well-known eating street Via Sardegna, right in the central Marina
district. I was very surprised to get an entirely acceptable, if somewhat
gloomy, single room with a clean shared bathroom for €23. First I had
something small to eat (Spaghetti alle vongole e bottarga; €5.50) at
Trattoria da Serafino and drank a caffè under the arcades of Via Roma.
Once the sun was no longer quite so merciless, I went on a long city
walk: up into the medieval Castello quarter, enclosed by defensive
walls, with its atmospheric, often rather run-down, palazzi.

## **Day 16 – 1 July – 62 km**

**Cala Gonone – Dorgali – Orosei – Santa Lucia**

With the thousand-metre pass behind me, I’d lost all respect for what
was still to come, so it wasn’t much of a struggle to climb the 400-plus
metres from Cala Gonone back up to the main road. The rest of the day
was more rolling than mountainous, and since I still had far too much
time anyway, I decided not to race straight to Olbia but to take it
easy, detouring to coves off the route and enjoying a bit of beach and
sea. My first stop was the pleasant, laid-back little town of Orosei,
with its nice old centre and shady piazza, just the place for a latte
macchiato under the trees.

very pretty: Cala Ginepro, and the long white Spiaggia s’Ena e sa

beach that stretches for four kilometres to the next town, La Caletta.

## **Day 17 – 2 July – 60 km**

**Santa Lucia – La Caletta – Posada – Budoni – San Teodoro – Lu Impostu
– Porto della Taverna**

My plan today was to leave the bike behind again and head inland for a
bit, but I wasn’t prepared to pay €40 for a battered 125cc scooter, so I
carried on towards Olbia, with plenty of stops. First in the old town of
Posada, picturesquely crowned by a castle on a hill, a truly authentic and idyllic place, despite its proximity to the tourist centres of
Baronia.

The coastal stretch that followed is surely one of the most beautiful in
Europe, but unfortunately it’s also very accessible, and lies directly
south-east of Olbia, which of course means you really shouldn’t come

of Isola Tavolara in view, and absolute dream beaches like Spiaggia La
Cinta, Cala di Lu Impostu or Porto della Taverna, are a feast for the
eyes: white sand, blue sea, so wonderfully refreshing after sweating it
out under Sardinia’s relentless sun.

## **Day 18 – 3 July – 28 km**

**Porto della Taverna – Olbia – Cugnana**

It wasn’t far to Olbia, where I drank a cappuccino and stocked up on a
few books for the next few days, my days off. I saved sightseeing for
another day and rode straight towards the Costa Smeralda, to Camping
Cugnana, where I’ll stay for the rest of the time until my flight home.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSarde05-0380.jpg" alt="Camping Cugnana">
  <em>Camping Cugnana</em>
</figure>

A very good choice: the sanitary facilities are miserably Italian, but
the huge pool with its sunbathing lawn right on the Golfo di Cugnana is
excellent. It’s also not too busy yet, I have peace and quiet, and the
restaurant serves wood-fired pizza. I don’t plan to spend the entire
rest of the time at the pool, but most of it. I do still have a few aims,
though: a thorough exploration of the Costa Smeralda and the Aranci
peninsula, plus Olbia.

## **Days 19–23 – 4 July – 8 July – 102 km**

The last days were exactly as I’d imagined. I spent two days by the
pool, reading and doing absolutely nothing else. On Monday I cycled the
Costa Smeralda once more as far as Porto Cervo, but it was the same
problem as south of Olbia: even though most of the dream bays can only
be reached via dusty, bumpy tracks, you can be sure you’ll hardly see
any sand for sun loungers. That doesn’t diminish the coast’s beauty, of
course, and in May or October the area is guaranteed to be a dream.
