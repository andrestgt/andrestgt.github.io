# Cycling Germany and the UK
**10 July – 9 August 2007**

A solo bicycle journey from Münster across northern Germany, then from Stansted through eastern England to Edinburgh.

---

## Day 1 (10 July) – 130 km

### Münster – Warendorf – Marienfeld – Bielefeld – Herford – Vlotho – Rinteln

Up to Bielefeld, the route looked flat, but it still came with a few
long climbs and descents that were barely visible, yet very noticeable
with 25 kg of luggage. Most of it was on cycle paths or lanes beside the
road.

Warendorf has a charming old town, ideal for a cappuccino stop.

From here the terrain became hillier, gently rolling as far as Herford,
where a thunderstorm forced me to take an unplanned shelter break in the
only moderately attractive old town.

Beyond Herford came the hilliest part of the day. In rain and cold I
crossed a few small ridges to Vlotho on the Weser.

Here began the most scenic part of the route: along the Weser Cycle
Route through the floodplains, very rural country with sleepy villages
and windmills by the roadside.

## Day 2 (11 July) – 126 km

### Rinteln – Hameln – Hildesheim – Söhlde – Salzgitter

As far as Hameln it stayed flat, following the Weser Cycle Route on
signposted minor roads.

After a lunch stop in the pretty old town, I took the B1 to Hildesheim.
For most of the way there was a cycle path beside the road, and the
route ran through gently rolling country.

Hildesheim is a less attractive city, though that impression may also
have been due to the dull, cold rain. There is an imposing old-town
square with a dozen pompously reconstructed medieval buildings; the rest
of the centre is post-war functionalism, pure and simple.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingGerUK2007-0008.jpg" alt="Hildesheim">
  <em>Hildesheim</em>
</figure>

From Hildesheim I continued on side roads through rural, gently
undulating country to Salzgitter, easily one of the most sterile,
unattractive cities in Germany. A conglomerate of faceless districts
without a centre; social housing, petty-bourgeois estates and factories
set the tone. But at least there is Salzgittersee: a rather pleasant
area with cycling and walking paths in green surroundings.

There was supposed to be a campsite here as well, but nobody knew
anything about it, so I simply pitched my tent in a sheltered spot on
the lakeshore.

## Day 3 (12 July) – 141 km

### Salzgitter – Wolfenbüttel – Halberstadt – Quedlinburg – Aschersleben – Giersleben – Bernburg

A very monotonous day, mostly flat across Lower Saxony, with hillier
terrain around Halberstadt and Quedlinburg.

In Lower Saxony, there were again cycle paths or lanes beside the road
for most of the way; in Saxony-Anhalt there were stretches without even
a hard shoulder, though traffic was not particularly heavy either.

The consolation for the boring scenery was the towns I passed today.
Wolfenbüttel, and above all fairy-tale Quedlinburg, are among the most
beautiful medieval towns in Germany, and Halberstadt, Aschersleben and
Bernburg, beautifully situated on the Saale, were also worth visiting.

Small provincial towns in the former GDR simply have more charm than
small towns in the West. No 1950s or 1960s functionalist architecture
spoils the look of the old centres here. And all those cobbled streets
and lanes may be a cyclist’s nightmare, but they look wonderfully
original and lend the places a very special atmosphere. In some villages
you can even still see a Trabi on the cobbles in front of a building in
need of renovation, the classic GDR image, even 18 years after
reunification.

Prices are only marginally lower than in the West now, but on
Quedlinburg’s magnificent market square you can at least still get a
cappuccino for under €2.

I got lucky with accommodation today. At the Bernburg canoe and
water-sports club, right by the river, I was able to set up my own
little home in the guesthouse loft, lined with carpet. There were clean
sanitary facilities too, and it cost €3 for the night.

## Day 4 (13 July) – 147 km

### Bernburg – Köthen – Quellendorf – Dessau – Wörlitz – Wittenberg – Torgau

On a mostly flat route, alternating between main and side roads, I rode
through the East German provinces as far as Dessau, a larger town that
is, visually, rather unattractive, with a centre made up largely of
prefabricated blocks and modern shopping complexes.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingGerUK2007-0022.jpg" alt="Dessau">

</figure>

Beyond Dessau the landscape became quite pretty, and the narrow, lightly
trafficked back road to Wörlitz was very pleasant to ride. Wörlitz has a
lovely landscape park with a lake and palace; after that came 7 km of
cobbles (fortunately with a tarmac cycle strip along the edge) to the
idyllic Elbe ferry opposite the little place of Coswig. After crossing
the river, Wittenberg was quickly reached on a cycle path beside the
federal road. Wittenberg is genuinely pretty, with a well-preserved old
town, an impressive market square and a lovely pedestrian zone, and
despite plenty of tourists from all over the world it was still very
good value (€1.80 for a cappuccino in a prime spot).

## Day 5 (14 July) – 167 km

### Torgau – Riesa – Nünchritz – Meissen – Dresden – Radeberg – Grossröhrsdorf – Bautzen

From Torgau to Riesa it was still mostly gently rolling federal road.
From Nünchritz onwards I followed the very busy Elbe Cycle Route, which
is well built apart from a few short narrow passages, flat through the
pretty Elbe Valley into beautiful Meissen. After a cappuccino in the old
town I took the B6 to Dresden, a road that saw hardly any traffic
today. Dresden is always worth a visit, especially on a sunny summer’s
day. But as it was already late afternoon and I still wanted to get to
Bautzen, I did not stay all that long in the city.

The rest of the way to Bautzen also ran through hilly country on quiet
rural lanes.

Bautzen looked like a very pleasant little town at first glance, but
unfortunately I did not have time to stay longer, as I wanted to reach
the campsite at the reservoir 5 km north of town before dark. The
campsite, however, no longer existed, so I had to wild camp again, and
there was plenty of space by the lake.

## Day 6 (15 July) – 126 km

### Bautzen – Reichenbach – Görlitz – Bad Muskau – Döbern

As far as Görlitz the route was fairly hilly again: first on narrow
country lanes to Reichenbach, then into town on the federal road. The
heat was extreme even in the morning, so it worked out well that the
remote border town of Görlitz is exceptionally attractive and a pleasant
place to spend the hottest hours of the day. Hardly any other German
city of this size made it through the Second World War so unscathed, so
Görlitz does not look hugely different today from what it did before the
war. Many of the magnificent rows of houses are still quite run-down,
but in the old town quite a few buildings have already been restored and
now house restaurants and cafés. There really ought to be more
pedestrian zones, though.

Down by the Neisse there is a very nice viewing terrace and a footbridge
to Poland. Here I also joined the Oder–Neisse Cycle Route, which I
followed north.

Apart from a few short sections on roads that were barely used anyway,
this really is a dedicated cycle path on newly tarmacked forest and farm
tracks, always along the German–Polish border. There are enough beer
gardens along the way that you will not die of thirst even on a Sunday,
and the route is popular with both touring cyclists and day-trippers. It
is mostly flat, though there are also a few short, steep climbs and
descents.

border town with an extremely pretty park: the UNESCO-listed Fürst

and found a campsite by a small lake shortly before Döbern, where I
could take a refreshing swim, after all, even at 8 p.m. it was still
about 30°C today.

## Day 7 (16 July) – unplanned rest day

### It was even a few degrees hotter than yesterday, by far the hottest day of the year, but severe pain in my knee and ankle made riding impossible. So I spent the day in the shade by the lake.

## Day 8 (17 July) – 150 km

### Döbern – Cottbus – Eisenhüttenstadt – Frankfurt (Oder) – Alt-Zeschdorf

Luckily it was not quite as hot today, and although I was still in pain
I could carry on. The 35 km into Cottbus I rode mostly on signposted
cycle streets, partly through lovely dense pine forest.

After passing the wonderful Branitz Park I reached Cottbus’s pleasant
town centre, where there were enough pavement cafés for a longer break.

Eisenhüttenstadt is certainly among Germany’s ugliest towns. Even the

Oder, has little to offer beyond a small pedestrian zone, but you can,

## Day 9 (18 July) – 163 km

### Alt-Zeschdorf – Seelow – Bad Freienwalde – Eberswalde – Joachimsthal – Templin – Lychen

A mostly gently rolling ride, often on tree-lined avenues through the
Brandenburg March as far as Eberswalde, a pleasant, relaxed little town
with a large market square and inviting outdoor cafés.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingGerUK2007-0056.jpg" alt="Eberswalde">
  <em>Eberswalde</em>
</figure>

About 10 km beyond Eberswalde I turned north into the Uckermark, where
the first outliers of the Mecklenburg Lake District begin. The most
scenic part was the stretch to Joachimsthal: first flat along a canal,
then hilly through the woods along Lake Werbellin. Beyond Joachimsthal a
lonely route led through the forested, sparsely populated, gently
undulating landscape of the Uckermark to the sleepy little town of
Templin on the lake of the same name.

From there it was another 20 km to Lychen, a holiday town in the heart
of the Uckermark lakes, where I pitched my tent at the lakeside campsite
(€7: poor woodland pitches, but good sanitary facilities).

## Day 10 (19 July) – 60 km

### Lychen – Wehkul – Neustrelitz – Penzlin – Wustrow – Neubrandenburg

When I woke up today I was in severe pain again. Both my knee and my
ankle were badly swollen and full of fluid, so I went to see a doctor.
The diagnosis was obvious, but the prescribed pills worked wonders and,
after a visit to the hairdresser (out here in the provinces you can
still get your hair carefully cut in a salon for €6), I was able to ride
on again almost pain-free by early afternoon.

Today truly took me through the deepest East German provinces: partly on
sandy tracks and cobbled roads through godforsaken hamlets, a landscape
of lakes and woods that strongly reminded me of the eastern Baltic.

In the rather attractive little town of Neustrelitz I briefly re-entered
civilisation at an extremely smart bar-café on the pedestrian zone,
where, sitting in comfortable wicker chairs, I enjoyed the best and most
expensive cappuccino so far in East Germany (€2.50).

## Day 11 (20 July) – 149 km

### Neubrandenburg – Altentreptow – Greifswald – Stahlbrode – Putbus – Binz – Prora

After 6 km on a mixed-quality cycle path along Lake Tollense, I reached
the superb lakeside city park of the strange town of Neubrandenburg.
Strange because it is encircled by a magnificent medieval fortress wall
with splendid city gates, something you can still admire in Germany
only in Rothenburg. Instead of a medieval town with half-timbered houses
and narrow alleys, however, inside Neubrandenburg’s walls you find a
spacious centre of socialist prefab blocks and modern buildings that
simply do not fit the picture.

to”, because up to the turn-off for Stahlbrode that meant 15 km of

subject or for shooting historical films, but not for pleasant cycling.

From Stahlbrode it was only a short ferry ride over to the island of
Rügen, the Baltic holiday paradise and one of Europe’s most beautiful
islands. Via the neoclassical oddity that is Putbus I rode through
gentle rolling countryside (and again, partly over cobbles) to the
seaside resort of Binz, the island’s elegant tourist centre, with its
old magnificent villas and the bustling pedestrian zone that leads onto
the famous pier, with grand views of the coast and the dazzling white
sandy beach.

## Day 15 (24 July) – 79 km

### Tesperhude – Hamburg

Although it rained almost all night, I was able to pack up relatively
dry and followed the Elbe Cycle Route into Hamburg. The city limit was
reached after only a few kilometres, but it was still a long way to the
centre, over many kilometres through fields, villages and along the Elbe
dykes.

I reached central Hamburg in the early afternoon and, for once, took a
hotel room, as today brought proper Hamburg weather and I wanted to stay
central. I found what I needed at the simple Hotel Stern: perfect
location right on the Reeperbahn, a basic but fairly spacious and clean
room with its own bathroom for €38, which struck me as excellent value.
In hindsight I am very glad I chose it, because in the evening it began
to rain as if someone were tipping out buckets, so I also (with a heavy
heart) skipped a night-time wander through St Pauli.

Before that, though, the weather was changeable and the sun even came
out a few times, so I was able to make the most of my time in the city.
I did most of it by bike: Speicherstadt, the harbour, the
Schanzenviertel and a full loop around the Außenalster. I explored the
old town with its canals and Binnenalster on foot. As it was my first
stay in the city, I had set myself the full programme, but by bicycle
you can see an enormous amount in a short time, and there was still
plenty of time for café breaks and a fishy dinner at a snack bar in the
Schanzenviertel.

Hamburg is a brilliant city with a unique atmosphere that, apart from
good weather, offers pretty much everything you would expect from a
world city. Unmistakably cosmopolitan and multifaceted, from smart
villa districts to charmingly run-down multicultural quarters (my clear
favourite is the Schanzenviertel with its gastronomic variety and
bohemian vibe), it has a bit of everything. And then there is, of
course, the water: canals, lakes and rivers that shape the city’s feel.
Thanks to them, even the architecturally not particularly beautiful city
centre feels attractive.

enough to limp, more or less accident-free, to the next petrol station
about 5 km away. Stupidly, my valve adaptor had vanished, and by then

half-hour walk into the nearest place, Stortford, where I had to wait an
hour until the shops opened.

The only pump I could find in the local shop was a disaster: I could
not even get 2 bar into the tube, but at least I was finally able to
set off shortly before 10, and a few kilometres later I fortunately
found a bike workshop that could sort out everything necessary. So I
still managed to reach Cambridge around midday. This famous university

The Cambridge area, and the city itself, is ideal cycling country:
mostly flat and served by plenty of signposted cycle routes. Otherwise,
from my first impressions, as a cyclist you have less comfort in England
than in Germany. Cycle lanes (and even hard shoulders) are, outside
Cambridge, barely existent, and country roads are often narrow and busy.
To reach Bury, for lack of alternatives, I had to use a four-lane dual
carriageway, where cycling is, in principle, allowed in England.

Bury is also a quite pleasant town, with a very pretty park surrounding
the ruins of an old abbey.

South of Bury began the most beautiful part of the day: through
Suffolk’s gentle, picturesque rolling hills. This is rural English idyll
as you imagine it, with photogenic medieval villages, green hills and
colourful fields. The prettiest was storybook Lavenham, with its
crooked, colourful half-timbered houses that could have transported you
back centuries, if only the dreamily beautiful market square were not
used as a car park. But the lack of café culture (this would be a prime
spot for pavement cafés) is simply typically English.

The largest town in the area, Sudbury, is less spectacular, but it
offers a surprisingly good range of places to eat for the English
provinces.

I stayed at a quiet campsite in the hilly landscape on the road to
Ipswich for the outrageously expensive £12.50. I do not know the general
price level of campsites in England, but it seems, as with almost

in England, was very poor.

## Day 29 (7 August) – 106 km

### West Kyloe – Berwick – Coldstream – Kelso – Melrose – Galashiels – Killochyett

In the morning it was about 15 km to Berwick, England’s northernmost
town, mostly on quiet country lanes away from the A1. Berwick looks
spectacular at first glance: a historic town beautifully set at the
mouth of the Tweed and completely enclosed by fortifications, a perfect
photo motif. Inside the walls, however, Berwick is a fairly ordinary
place without much atmosphere or any particularly nice cafés.

Here I left the coast and chose the longer inland route to Edinburgh for
two reasons: first, the Scottish Borders are supposed to be especially
scenic; second, I assumed it would be less stormy inland than out on the
coast. The first part at least was true; the cold, stormy headwind,
however, did not stop blowing all day and made cycling thoroughly
unpleasant. It was a very cold summer’s day anyway, but at least it
didn’t rain.

Putting wind and weather to one side, this area would be lovely cycling
country: gently rolling, little traffic, and plenty of fine views over
picture-perfect river valleys and hills, plus medieval abbey ruins,
castles, and pretty old villages like Melrose and Coldstream. The
Borders aren’t the Highlands, but they still feel like “classic
Scotland” in the clichéd sense.

## Days 30–31 (8–9 August) – 64 km

### Killochyett – Edinburgh

After a generous full Scottish breakfast, I set off for Edinburgh, the
final destination of my tour. The first half of the roughly 40 km
continued through pretty, sparsely populated countryside; the remainder,
still hilly but trending downhill, ran through denser suburban
outskirts.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingGerUK2007-0277.jpg" alt="Borders Region">
  <em>Borders Region</em>
</figure>

Surprisingly, even at festival time it wasn’t hard (just expensive) to
find a bed. I stayed very centrally, between the Royal Mile and Cowgate,
at Smart City Hostel, more like a budget hotel than a backpacker place,
with sterile but very modern and comfortable en-suite dorms (£20.95 for
a bed in an eight-bed room). As I couldn’t check in until the afternoon,
I dumped my luggage and took a long bike ride through Edinburgh’s
glorious Holyrood wilderness, out to the now rather chic harbour
district of Leith, with its many pubs and pricey seafood restaurants,
and back to the centre along the Water of Leith, via the very attractive
nightlife districts of Stockbridge and Broughton.

When I booked my flight back from Edinburgh, I had no idea that Europe’s
biggest cultural festival would be happening at exactly that time:
hundreds of venues and thousands of shows: comedy, theatre, bands, and
everything in between. You could almost say the Fringe takes over the
entire city. With that sheer volume of choice (and average ticket prices
of around £10 per show), it’s nearly impossible to keep track and avoid
wasting money and time on something mediocre, but I threw myself into
the chaos as best I could.

On Wednesday I danced until early morning at the Liquid Room to
excellent indie music (all drinks were only £1.50), then the next day,
straight after getting up, went for lunch at La Maison Bleu, a very
pleasant Mediterranean restaurant where, by coincidence, Sean Connery
was also having lunch, without it causing much fuss. The food (salade
chèvre chaud for £3.75 and a tuna fillet burger for £6.95) was simple
but fresh, well cooked, and good value.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingGerUK2007-0303.jpg" alt="Edinburgh - Maison Bleue">
  <em>Edinburgh - Maison Bleue</em>
</figure>

For dinner I discovered a real culinary gem. The unassuming Indian place
Saffrani in the student district served astonishingly good, authentic
North Indian dishes in a pleasant atmosphere, and even in the evening
at genuinely fair prices. The aubergine starter with almond cream
(£3.45) came with a heavenly tamarind sauce with fresh coriander and a
refreshing mint dip. The lamb in its perfectly balanced curry (£7.45)
was so tender it melted on the tongue, and the mango lassi was spot-on
as well. Reginald Hunter's stand-up show afterwards was fantastic too.
An ideal end to the trip.

I could happily spend the rest of the month like this. Unfortunately,
I’d then be not only bankrupt, but also fat and an alcoholic, and even
cycling wouldn’t help much with that.
