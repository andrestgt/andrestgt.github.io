# Venezuela & Colombia
**10 September – 21 October 2001**

A journey from Caracas along the Venezuelan coast to Colombia, including the Ciudad Perdida trek near Santa Marta.

---


## Day 1 – 10 Sep

My flight from Stuttgart was delayed, so I missed my connection to
Caracas. That mishap was, of course, the best thing that could have
happened: it finally gave me time to have a proper look at Amsterdam,
and spared me the joy of hunting for a hotel in Caracas in the middle of
the night.

I liked Amsterdam a lot: a fascinating mix of neat façades and grubby
subculture.

## Day 2 – 11 Sep

I arrived in Maiquetía in the morning. The drive from the airport up
into the mountains to Caracas is spectacular, as is the city’s setting.
Still, my priority was to get out of this monster of a place as quickly
as possible.

My first stop was Puerto Colombia on the coast of Henri Pittier National
Park. The ride there from Maracay was absolutely magnificent, and
hair-raising. The adventurous road runs straight through the park’s
gorgeous mountain scenery, which makes it all the more surprising that
at the end of it you find a full-blown tourist resort.

From Puerto Colombia it was only a few minutes' walk to beautiful Playa
Grande, a bay fringed with coconut palms where I could pitch my tent.
Despite the crowds of (local) tourists, it was a lovely place on earth.

## Day 3 – 12 Sep

In the morning I walked to Choroní, a pretty, quiet little colonial town
a few kilometres inland.

## Day 4 – 13 Sep (travel day)

In the morning I took the bus back to Maracay. From there I continued to
Coro on a deluxe coach (the next regular bus wouldn’t have left until
the evening). It really was extremely comfortable, and extremely
expensive (13,000 Bs for a six-hour ride).

The cheapest posada in Coro was unfortunately full, but I still managed
to get a good room with air con and a private bathroom at Posada La
Momona, and at the student rate, too: 8,000 instead of 10,000 Bs.

## Day 5 – 14 Sep

A lazy day in the pretty, pleasant colonial town of Coro (a UNESCO World
Heritage Site, no less). There isn’t much to do here beyond strolling
the streets and soaking up the calm atmosphere.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/VenezuelaColombia2001-0004.jpg" alt="Coro">

For budget reasons, I switched accommodation, an excellent decision.
Posada El Gallo is a beautiful colonial house with a courtyard and a
real guesthouse vibe. A good place to lie in a hammock, drink cold beer,
and chat to other travellers. 4,000 Bs for a bed in the dorm.

## Day 6 – 15 Sep

A day trip organised by the posada (15,000 Bs) to the Paraguaná
Peninsula.

At first the tour wasn’t all that exciting, as the peninsula’s
vegetation is very sparse. In the afternoon, though, there were two
highlights that made it worthwhile: Las Salinas, a large pink salt lake
that looks utterly unreal (its colour comes from natural plankton), and
the Médanos de Coro, huge sand dunes that really make you feel as if
you’ve landed in the desert.

## Day 7 – 16 Sep (travel day)

In the morning I took the bus to Maracaibo (across Lake Maracaibo on the
longest bridge in South America). From there I continued over the
Colombian border to Maicao, then another four hours on to Santa Marta.

Santa Marta has a wonderfully pleasant evening atmosphere, with a lively
seafront promenade and restaurants where you could get a large grilled
fish for under 10 DM.

I stayed at the travellers’ hotel Casa Familiar, where a bed in a basic
four-bed room cost only 5,000 pesos (nothing like Venezuela’s prices).

Why did I suddenly decide to travel to the supposedly “notorious”
Colombia? In the posada in Coro I met an Austrian who had originally
planned to travel only in Venezuela, then made a detour into Colombia,
and did nothing but rave about it. The country, he said, was at least as
beautiful as Venezuela, but a good deal cheaper. A Dutch woman in the
posada overheard our conversation and was instantly keen as well, so I
didn’t even have to travel alone.

## Day 8 – 17 Sep

Today we went to Taganga and then walked a bit along the coast. What
fascinated me was how little it felt like the tropics and how
Mediterranean it seemed instead: rocky headlands alternating with small
shingle coves, sparse vegetation, and cacti rather than coconut palms. A
very beautiful area.

## Day 9 – 18 Sep

In the morning we travelled to Cartagena, said to be one of the most
beautiful colonial cities in South America. Unfortunately, it poured
down for the first time today, so I put off sightseeing until tomorrow.
I only explored the area around my posada, in Getsemaní, the less
touristy part of the old town outside the city walls. Even so, this
neighbourhood is already lively and interesting.

I spent the night in the dorm at Casa Viena, another simple travellers’
posada with a terrace (6,000 pesos).

## Day 10 – 19 Sep

The weather was good again, so I could take my time exploring Cartagena.
And this city really is astonishing. I wouldn’t have thought the old
quarter could be so well preserved. The historic centre is surrounded by
a wall you can walk along, and the rows of colonial houses look simply
magical.

simple restaurants in Getsemaní you could eat grilled fish with side
dishes for just 2,000–3,000 pesos.

## Day 11 – 20 Sep

A very quiet day. In the morning I took the bus back to Santa Marta,
where I stayed once more at Casa Familiar (today I had the dorm to
myself). There I also booked the six-day trekking tour to Ciudad Perdida
for tomorrow (280,000 pesos, all inclusive).

In the evening I ate grilled fish again on Santa Marta’s seafront
promenade and drank fresh fruit juices.

## Day 12 – 21 Sep

Today the trekking tour to Ciudad Perdida began.

First we bounced along a rough “road” into the Sierra in a chiva, the
typical colourful, open Colombian truck, to reach our starting point.
After lunch we finally set off. Only three hours of walking were
scheduled for day one, but two of those hours were steeply uphill.
Still, it wasn’t too exhausting, as we walked at a fairly moderate pace
and took plenty of breaks.

The path ran mostly through jungle, and there was a nice place to swim
along the way. Best of all, though, was a waterfall near our destination
with a deep pool where you could cool off, soaked in sweat as we were.

We slept in hammocks.

## Day 13 – 22 Sep

Our actual walking time today was only four hours again: one hour
steeply uphill, one hour steeply downhill. Even so, it was tougher than
yesterday because it had rained overnight and the path was therefore
very slippery.

We reached our accommodation around 1 p.m. It was by a river, where we
could have a lovely swim again.

## Day 14 – 23 Sep

Today we had another five hours to walk before reaching Ciudad Perdida.
It was the most scenic day of the whole tour, and also the most
adventurous. We had to cross the river eight times (sometimes quite
torrential) and scramble over slick rocks along the edge of a drop. All
of it through dense jungle, with waterfalls constantly tumbling down
from the slopes.

We ate lunch right by the river, where a waterfall plunged into a
wonderful pool. From there we climbed an ancient staircase for almost an
hour up to Ciudad Perdida, beautifully set in the middle of the jungle
on the slopes of a deep ravine.

## Day 15 – 24 Sep

We spent several hours today visiting the Lost City, abandoned by the
Tayrona people during the Conquest and only cleared of jungle in the
1970s. You can still see the stone terraces where wooden huts once
stood, as well as countless ancient paths and stairways.

What’s most fascinating, though, is the location deep in the jungle,
and the fact that you can only reach it on foot after a long trek. As a
result, only one tourist group a week makes it here.

The ruined city ranks among the most important indigenous sites in South
America.

Annoyingly, my camera battery died today of all days, so I could only
take a few photos, though I suspect you can’t really capture this
incredible nature and the magic of this place in pictures anyway.

## Day 16 – 25 Sep

The hardest stage! We walked days two and three back in one go: eight
hours of hiking, including one hour down slippery stone steps, ten river
crossings, one hour uphill at about a 20% gradient, and sliding about
for most of the route.

I can guarantee I’ve never sweated so much in my life. Still, we were
rewarded with superb scenery, and two wonderfully cool places to swim.

## Day 17 – 26 Sep

Today it was just under three hours back to our starting point (two of
those hours downhill), plus another two hours back to Santa Marta by
chiva.

The trekking tour was truly brilliant, and very reasonably priced.
Sleeping in hammocks was genuinely comfortable, and in Ciudad Perdida,
where it got really cold at night, we even got blankets. Our guide was
very nice and we had no complaints about the food, either. Alongside the
usual rice with meat and sandwiches, there was plenty of fresh veg, and
in between we were given fresh fruit.

Another night, inevitably, at Casa Familiar.

## Day 18 – 27 Sep

In the morning I took the bus to the entrance of Tayrona National Park
and paid the 5,000-peso entry fee. From here, taxis are supposed to take
you five kilometres further to Cañaveral. Unfortunately, there weren’t
any. Luckily two Colombian women gave me a lift in their brand-new
pick-up.

From Cañaveral it was another strenuous 45-minute walk (only strenuous
because I was carrying all my luggage) to Arrecifes, a beautiful beach
fringed with coconut palms with two restaurants. You could either pitch
your tent for 3,000 pesos or rent a hammock for 4,000. Out of
convenience I chose the latter.

Tayrona National Park is a gorgeous coastal landscape of coconut palms,
rock formations and sandy beaches. I only walked about an hour along the
coast to the next beach, which, as its name La Piscina suggests, was
good for swimming. Otherwise I spent the whole day in a hammock under
coconut palms, looking out at the sea.

## Day 19 – 28 Sep (travel day)

In the morning I walked the 45 minutes back to Cañaveral and took a jeep
to the main road, where I caught the bus to Maicao.

Then I headed back to Venezuela by por puesto (shared taxi). Crossing
the border in this direction was no problem at all, and Venezuelan
customs even searched only my daypack, unusual when you’re leaving
Colombia.

In Maracaibo I had to wait a few hours for the bus to Mérida, but I ate
a delicious, hefty piece of tuna with garlic in a terminal restaurant
for a fairly reasonable 3,000 Bs.

## Day 20 – 29 Sep

A quiet day in Mérida. I arrived at 5 a.m., which gave me plenty of time
to explore the city, and in the afternoon I even caught up on the sleep
I’d missed the night before.

Architecturally Mérida doesn’t offer much beyond its small historic
centre, but it’s wonderfully located between two mountain ranges nearly
5,000 metres high, has a very pleasant climate, and is simply a
wonderfully relaxed city where you feel completely safe. Add to that the
fact that, by Venezuelan standards, the price level is fairly low.

I found accommodation at the pleasant Posada Mundo Mérida near the
teleférico. The room was little more than a bed and a bit of shelf
space, but the bathroom area was spacious and well kept, and there was
even hot water for showers, very welcome on the rather chilly nights up
here. 6,000 Bs for a single room!

## Day 21 – 30 Sep

In the morning I took Mérida’s teleférico, the longest and highest
cable car in the world, up to Pico Espejo at 4,800 metres (12,500 Bs).
The views over Mérida and the surrounding mountains were fantastic.
Right at the top it was foggy and bitterly cold.

After the summit I rode back down to 4,000 metres, to the Loma Redonda
station. From there I went on a superb five-hour hike to the pretty
Andean village of Los Nevados. The first 45 minutes were a tough climb
through barren high-mountain scenery, but after the pass it was almost
all downhill through a dreamlike valley carpeted with plants in every
possible colour, with waterfalls plunging down from the green slopes.

I’d brought all my camping kit because I wanted to camp in Los Nevados,
but the meadow was covered in cow dung, so I opted for a bed instead. It
turned out to be the right decision. For one thing, it rained at night;
for another, I found accommodation at the pretty Posada Guamanchi, whose
terrace with hammocks and a view of the mountains was simply a dream
after the strenuous hike. The meal, included in the 7,000 Bs price, was
perfectly fine, and although the room was basic it came with two thick
wool blankets, which, at almost 3,000 metres above sea level, is
nothing to be sniffed at.

## Day 22 – 1 Oct

The four-hour ride back from Los Nevados to Mérida in a clattering jeep
was spectacular, and at times nerve-racking. The narrow, unpaved road
winds up in a series of switchbacks, often right along the edge of a
drop, through fantastic mountain scenery.

Back in Mérida I needed to calm my nerves and relax. I had my laundry
done, went online, and in the evening I arranged the canyoning trip for
the next day with Arassari Tours.

Another night at Mundo Mérida; I even got a larger room for the same
price.

## Day 23 – 2 Oct

Today: canyoning, the most extreme, hippest outdoor activity in Mérida,
guaranteed to deliver the ultimate kick and adrenaline rush, if you
believe Arassari’s guestbook.

People were exaggerating a bit. The tour was neither particularly
hardcore nor did it give me some life-altering rush.

Still, I absolutely don’t regret spending 35,000 Bs on the day.
Abseiling down an impressive 35-metre waterfall while secured only by a
rope, tearing down a natural waterslide at ridiculous speed, or jumping
from a rock into rapids less than a metre wide was great fun, and it
did take some nerve. All in all, a very successful outing on my last day
in Mérida’s wonderful landscape.

## Day 24 – 3 Oct (travel day)

For reasons that remain baffling, there were no morning buses from
Mérida to Valencia or Caracas. I didn’t want to lose a whole day, so I
simply got on the next bus to Barinas, hoping to pick up a connection
from there. It worked out perfectly: I arrived in Puerto La Cruz at
night, and from there I immediately caught a connection to Ciudad
Bolívar.

All told, it was 24 hours of travel, but in comfortable buses where I
could sleep well. The route from Mérida to Barinas was spectacular, too:
over a 3,500-metre Andean pass and down through a deep green canyon.

## Day 25 – 4 Oct

After the long bus journey I did the bare minimum today. For tomorrow I
booked a three-day combined all-inclusive flight-and-boat tour to
Canaima, which I booked at Hotel Caracas for a surprisingly “cheap”
\$180.

Otherwise I took a relaxed look around Ciudad Bolívar, a pleasant city
with a bit of colonial architecture and a riverside promenade on the
mighty Orinoco. I also spent lots of time at my pretty Posada Amor
Patrio. It was in a lovely colonial house with a view of the cathedral
and had a German manager. The room I slept in was still under
construction and therefore open-air, but it wasn’t unpleasant. 7,000 Bs!

## Day 26 – 5 Oct

First day of the Canaima tour. Because I’d booked the cheapest tour
available, the other participants and I first had to travel two and a
half hours to an airport (La Paragua) that was closer to Canaima. From
there we took an old, rattling Cessna with a drunk bush pilot to
Canaima.

It was an interesting low-level flight over the jungle that gave you a
sense of how fantastic this region must be. Arriving in Canaima itself
was something of an anticlimax: Japanese tour groups sat around the
airport with stickers, and foreigners had to pay an inflated entry fee
of 8,000 Bs.

The lagoon, however, was beautiful, mainly because of its strange
colour combinations. The rivers were cola-coloured, the waterfalls
yellow like beer, and the beaches white with a faint pinkish touch.

Right after lunch we took a boat to our camp in the direction of Angel
Falls. On the way we had our first highlight: Salto El Sapo, a powerful
waterfall you can walk behind, a unique experience.

We reached our camp only after dark, had dinner, and slept in hammocks.

## Day 27 – 6 Oct

Today we had a good ninety minutes of a wet but cheerful boat ride
through magnificent table-mountain scenery to Isla Ratón. From there it
was another hour’s walk through the jungle to the viewpoint of Angel
Falls, the world’s highest waterfall.

The view of the falls from here was already breathtaking. The absolute
highlight, though, was swimming in the pool below the waterfall. On one
side you had a view of the falls; on the other you could admire the
grand landscape around you. It was simply brilliant.

Back at camp we went for another swim in the river, and otherwise lazed
about in hammocks. A fantastic day!!

## Day 28 – 7 Oct

We travelled by boat back to Canaima, where there was still enough time
to enjoy the beach by the wonderful lagoon and go for a swim before our
plane took off.

This time (for reasons beyond me) we were lucky enough to fly directly
to Ciudad Bolívar.

I spent the night in a hammock for 3,000 Bs on the wonderful (though
still unfinished) terrace of Amor Patrio.

## Day 29 – 8 Oct

The Roraima tour was scheduled to start this evening. Unfortunately our
guide’s mother was ill (yeah, sure!), so the tour was postponed by a
day.

In Ciudad Bolívar there was, sadly, nothing to do, even today, and now
I also had to kill time somehow tomorrow. Still, the day was quite
pleasant because many people from the Canaima tour were still in town,
and we passed the time drinking beer, playing backgammon, playing pool,
and going out for a Chinese dinner.

At least my accommodation was paid for, even if the rooms at Hotel
Caracas were absolute dives.

## Day 30 – 9 Oct

Another day waiting in Ciudad Bolívar, though we kept ourselves
entertained at Hotel Caracas with pool and table football, and beer.

Night bus to Santa Elena.

## Day 31 – 10 Oct

The trek to Roraima normally takes six days, but for the tour price of
200,000 Bs we also got a free extra day of sightseeing, which went
something like this: we were taken by taxi to a few waterfalls and
swimming spots. The tour itself was nothing special, but the landscape
of the Gran Sabana really is fantastic.

## Day 32 – 11 Oct

Today the trek was finally meant to begin, but our nightmare guide
clearly couldn’t be bothered to set off (even though we were already in
San Francisco, the starting point) and invented one excuse after
another. Even though we put pressure on him, and even calling the
travel agency in Ciudad Bolívar, we didn’t get going.

So we bought a bottle of rum and emptied it in the afternoon. In the
evening we went back to the bar with a few other travellers for cheap
caipirinhas.

At least the food for the day was paid for, and we took full advantage:
for dinner we had a tasty pizza.

This time we stayed at the equally good Posada La Aventura.

## Day 33 – 12 Oct

Today the trek to Roraima that I’d waited for so long finally started.
Since it’s meant to be one of the most beautiful hikes in South America,
and everyone I met raved about it, all that mattered to me was getting
on the road at last.

We now had to do the whole thing in five rather than six days, but I
didn’t think that would be a problem the walking time. Today, in any
case, wasn’t too hard. Most of the six hours were relatively flat; only
the last hour was a steady, though not too steep, climb.

We had fantastic weather and could see the impressive bulk of Roraima
clearly, watching as we drew closer and closer. The whole route ran
through the gentle rolling hills of the Gran Sabana, and the landscape
and views were wonderful.

## Day 34 – 13 Oct

Yesterday was easy; today was the opposite. The hour across the savannah
to base camp was already fairly brutal, but the three hours up to the
summit through dense vegetation, over roots and boulders, were
absolutely hardcore. Still, we were constantly rewarded with fantastic
views of the sheer cliff face and the endless savannah.

The landscape up here is utterly unreal, and unique in the world: dark

turtles, atomic mushrooms, bridges, windows (and no, I hadn’t been
smoking anything). The view from the edge down to the Gran Sabana was
breathtakingly beautiful and repaid every ounce of effort.

## Day 35 – 14 Oct

I can’t really write much about today, even though it was easily the
highlight of the trip. We had a whole day on the summit of Roraima, and
the lunar landscape with its rock formations up here is so unique that
you can’t really put it into words.

## Day 37 – 16 Oct

Today it was only two and a half hours’ brisk walking back to
Paraitepuí. After a bit of a delay we were greeted there with cold beer,
Coke and a wonderful hamburger, after all the deprivations, a treat.

In Santa Elena we were given a room to shower in, and I went to the

finally felt human again.

In the evening we ate very good Chinese food at Tommy Town, then took
the bus to Ciudad Bolívar.

## Day 38 – 17 Oct

After the night bus ride I’d actually wanted to head straight to the
beach, but it took until midday for us to get even the token \$25
compensation for the day we’d lost. So I decided to stay one more night
in Ciudad Bolívar, on the terrace of my lovely, and best of all cheap,
posada, for 3,000 Bs.

The day flew by: Champions League on TV, pool, and beer.

## Day 39 – 18 Oct

At last, the beach. I chose Santa Fe because I didn’t have much time
left and it was the most conveniently located beach between Ciudad
Bolívar and Caracas.

On the way I stopped briefly in Puerto La Cruz, the main beach resort
for wealthy Venezuelans, but the place wasn’t particularly attractive
and shockingly expensive.

Santa Fe lies in Mochima National Park, a very beautiful hilly coastal
region with plenty of offshore islands whose coral reefs are said to be
great for snorkelling. Apart from that, the village doesn’t have much to
offer, even if it is one of the most popular traveller hangouts in
Venezuela. The beach is decent enough, there are a few foreign-run
posadas right by the sea, and the cocktails are cheap.

## Résumé

## Colombia

With just under two weeks in Colombia, I could of course only get a
brief taste of the country, but it definitely whetted my appetite for
more.

Colombia’s north coast alone has everything a traveller could want:
palm-fringed beaches, jungle-covered mountain ranges with legendary ruin
cities, and Cartagena, one of the prettiest cities on the continent, if
not the world. On top of that, travelling is comfortable and
inexpensive, and the same goes for accommodation.

On the Caribbean coast, meals are mostly grilled fish with chilli sauce
and rice, plus wonderful fruit, some of it with names I’d never even
heard before.

The security situation keeps many travellers away from Colombia, which
is a bit puzzling, since the north in particular is considered safe, and
I personally had no problems whatsoever. Many Colombians are well aware
of foreigners’ prejudices about their country and treat those who come
anyway with great friendliness and courtesy.

## Venezuela

Venezuela is an Americanised, expensive country to travel in, where you
can often see the main sights only on expensive tours. The level of
comfort is almost as high as in Europe, and Venezuelans seem, given
their relative wealth, to think they are something special; that is the
only way I can explain their arrogance and unfriendliness.

And yet it is the country’s fabulous natural beauty that makes you put
up with every inconvenience and return home overwhelmed. From
palm-fringed Caribbean beaches to flower-covered Andean valleys; from
the world’s highest waterfall in the middle of the jungle to the unique
landscapes and vegetation of the tepuis, describing Venezuela’s nature
is almost impossible; one quickly runs out of superlatives. You simply
have to experience it yourself.
