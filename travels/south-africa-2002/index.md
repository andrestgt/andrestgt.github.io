# South Africa
**September-October 2002**

A road trip through South Africa, from Johannesburg and Kruger National Park through the Drakensberg and Garden Route to Cape Town.

---

## Day 1 – 3 September

Our flight to Madrid was three hours late and we also had to change in Barcelona, so we didn’t get in until about 8pm. We spent about two hours wandering through the wonderful old town, then had very good tapas at the stylish Café Oriente. After another hour’s delay, our flight to Johannesburg finally took off shortly before 3am.

## Day 2 – 4 September

We arrived in Jo’burg around midday, picked up our hire car and drove straight on towards Kruger National Park. After a surprisingly decent and inexpensive Chinese dinner in Lydenburg, we spent the night a few kilometres outside town at the pretty Longtom Farm Guesthouse. Excellent, comfortable rooms with a great bathroom and a kitchen for R165 per person including breakfast.

## Day 3 – 5 September

After breakfast we drove from Lydenburg over a pass to Sabie, where we visited the beautiful Bridal Veil Falls nearby. Afterwards I went for a swim in the Mac Mac Pools and we also took a look at the impressive waterfall of the same name.

North of Graskop the scenery became even more spectacular. We first visited another impressive waterfall, Berlin Falls, and then looked out through “God’s Window” over the Lowveld lying 1,000 metres below.

On the one hand, the small canyon with its bizarre rock formations is beautiful; on the other, the area has been disfigured with awful railings and bridges, put there so the inevitable coachloads of package tourists, first greeted by a kitschy dance troupe, don’t fall into the water.

After that, Blyde Canyon proper began: a magnificent gorge that you could look down into from several viewpoints. We spent the night at the wonderfully located Aventura Eco Blydepoort Resort, in perfectly decent lodges with kitchen, bathroom and a barbecue terrace for R300. The buffet in the restaurant, however, was catastrophic.

## Day 4 – 6 September

After crossing the scenic Abel Erasmus Pass, we drove through the Lowveld into Kruger National Park (entry: R84 for two people). We entered through Orpen Gate and drove (partly on tarmac, partly on dirt tracks) to Olifants Camp. The landscape really did match the kitsch “Out of Africa” image, and we saw masses of zebras, giraffes and antelope.

We were also lucky enough to come across a herd of buffalo and two herds of elephants.

We stayed in a typical rondavel at beautifully situated Olifants Camp (neatly furnished, with a braai area) for R315. From the resort terrace in the evening we could even watch a few hippos bathing.

## Day 5 – 7 September

At 5.30am we set off on the three‑hour morning safari we’d booked the day before (R70 per person). In the open truck (and, thanks to the low season, only seven of us) we drove through the park and, right at the start, spotted lions returning from a hunt. We were also taken to a spot that was perfect for hippo‑watching and saw two crocodile heads in the distance; otherwise we’d seen more animals yesterday.

We left Kruger via Phalaborwa Gate and drove to Tzaneen, a modern but pleasant provincial town, where we had an excellent, good‑value lunch at Villa Italia on the main road: carpaccio for R23, grilled squid (R40), and a tasty mushroom‑spinach mixture topped with mozzarella (R21).

Over the misty, green Magoebaskloof Pass we reached the motorway and drove on to Warmbad, a spa town with hot springs, where we stayed at the very pretty Palmeira Guesthouse (R180 per person including breakfast). “Very pretty” applied mainly to the communal areas; the rooms themselves had plenty of character and were spacious, but a touch neglected.

## Day 6 – 8 September

In the morning we took the shortest route to Pilanesberg National Park (which meant partly on unpaved roads). It’s a park that is much smaller and scenically more beautiful than Kruger, but it also doesn’t offer the same density of wildlife. Still, we managed to spot a rhino, elephants, hippos, wildebeest and baboons, and watched a mighty lion stroll calmly across the road right in front of us. Right in the middle of the park, in an old colonial building, there’s a restaurant with a beautiful panoramic terrace. All in all, a very worthwhile destination.

## Day 7 – 9 September

Today we set off for Jo’burg, with an extended lunch break in Pretoria, whose centre feels very American. A few colonial buildings still remain, and the government buildings are quite impressive.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/SouthAfrica2002-0010.jpg" alt="Pretoria">

</figure>

Otherwise the city isn’t a must-see, but it isn’t unattractive either.

We had lunch in Hatfield, Pretoria’s pub and restaurant district, on a pleasant square at the Italian place “Mozarella’s”. Here too the food was generally good and inexpensive; unfortunately the vinaigrette on both the spinach salad and the carpaccio was too sour.

Back in Johannesburg we checked into our hotel. “Protea Wanderers” is in a ‘safe’ suburb about a 10-minute drive from the centre, and we had a very comfortable room for R600.

In the afternoon we strolled around the huge Rosebank Mall and in the evening drove to the pretty restaurant district of Melville. Here there’s one café and restaurant after another. We were spoilt for choice and decided on “Chaplin’s”, which is said to be among the 100 best restaurants in South Africa. The ambience was wonderful; the presentation, however, was too pretentious and the food was, at best, average. The ostrich fillet was very good, the crème brûlée was quite OK, but the Thai prawn and chicken curry was an absolute joke and tasted of nothing. When we asked about it, we were told that this was how it had to be, because Thai cuisine is fundamentally very mild (!!!).

## Day 8 – 10 September

Today we took a guided tour of Johannesburg and Soweto. For R250 per person we had a minibus and a guide all to ourselves, which was rather surprising. Our guide’s aim was to show us that reports of crime and danger in Jo’burg don’t reflect reality and are largely a thing of the past, and he was so successful that, in the afternoon, we went back into the supposedly dangerous city centre on our own.

Jo’burg is a huge city with ultra‑rich suburbs, a downtown where a few colonial buildings still stand among the skyscrapers, and then the vast townships, the suburbs where Black people were forced to live during apartheid.

The highlight of Jo’burg was the view over the city from the 52nd floor of the Carlton Building.

We also had lunch in Soweto, at Wandie’s Place, where there were as many tourists as locals; the all‑you‑can‑eat buffet for R50 in the atmospheric dining hall was perfectly fine. Traditional African food, very simple, but tasty.

That evening, however, we were the only guests in this beautiful place. The food was excellent, the Moroccan dessert was a revelation, and the prices were surprisingly moderate.

## Day 9 – 11 September

It’s about 400 km from Jo’burg to Swaziland through dull agricultural and industrial scenery. The border formalities were quick and easy, and we drove another 35 km to the Malotja Wilderness Area, where we rented a comfortable wooden cabin with a kitchen and fireplace (R300).

On the bumpy, unpaved roads of the reserve we drove through wonderful wild mountain scenery to a few viewpoints, before doing a two‑hour hike along a canyon to Malotja Falls. The walk was very strenuous because of the steep climb, but the fantastic views, and the solitude of the wilderness, made up for the effort.

## Day 12 – 14 September

Early in the morning, before breakfast, we took our last excursion and saw three of the Big Five once more: elephants, buffalo and rhinos.

<figure>

Then, sadly, it was time to say goodbye to the camp and drive towards KwaZulu‑Natal.

Swaziland gave us a fine send-off: a fantastic lunch at Lubombo Lobster Restaurant in Big Bend: magnificent king prawns (R140 a dozen), tasty seafood curry and grilled calamari; seafood that cheap, cooked that well, is hard to find.

Back in South Africa we drove over the spectacular Lebombo Mountains to Sodwana Bay in Maputaland. For many kilometres the road was unpaved but still quite easy to drive. In the national park we rented a large chalet, with terrace and a view over the Indian Ocean, surrounded by dense green vegetation, another wonderful little corner of the earth for R320.



## Day 13 – 15 September

Today we had an easy day in Sodwana Bay. In the morning we spent a few hours on the kilometre‑long, deserted beach, backed by wonderfully dense, green vegetation. At lunchtime we had tasty fish in the nice restaurant at Sodwana Bay Lodge, then we passed the rest of the day in our lovely chalet.

Then it was on through impressive scenery to Fouriesburg, near the border with Lesotho, where we spent the night a little way out of town in a chalet at the secluded Meiringskloof Camp. The chalet was simple but spacious, wonderfully situated below high cliffs, and excellent value (R180).

## Day 17 – 19 September

In the morning we drove to the capital, Maseru, but didn’t stay long, as there are neither sights nor any of the other big‑city amenities, inviting cafés and the like. By late morning we arrived at Malealea Lodge, wonderfully located and more or less the hub of tourism in Lesotho.

While there are hardly any places to stay, let alone tourist infrastructure, in the rest of the country, even tour groups stop here, which doesn’t exactly help the atmosphere. Still, the surroundings of the lodge offer a magnificent mountain backdrop.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/SouthAfrica2002-0042.jpg" alt="Lesotho - Malealea">

</figure>

First we drove along a track to Botsoela Waterfall, a glorious spot we had all to ourselves. The “road” continued to a viewpoint from which you could survey the whole area, with its mountain chains and gorges.

Our accommodation at the lodge was fine: a simply furnished hut with bathroom cost 220 Maloti. Dinner for 60 Maloti was surprisingly good, chicken with vegetables, simple, but the flavours were spot-on.



## Day 18 – 20 September

In the morning we did the by-now almost obligatory ride on Basotho ponies (90 Maloti per person). For two hours we followed the dramatic Pitseng Canyon through Lesotho’s wild mountain landscape. The ride was a real highlight, but two hours was absolutely enough for my backside and my back.

Via Mohale’s Hoek and Quthing we crossed back into South Africa, and after many kilometres on dirt tracks we finally reached a proper road again. The scenery here is still interesting in places, but the drive from Aliwal North to Queenstown took us for many kilometres through utter emptiness.

In Queenstown, a fairly big town in the middle of nowhere, we stayed in very neat little rooms at Val’s GH (R145 per person). When it comes to food, your choice is basically steakhouse or steakhouse, but the dishes (fillet steak on a hot stone, mushroom skewers) at the absolutely packed Buffalo Springs Spur were good and good value.

Back on the N2, the Great Kei River Pass served up more spectacular views before the stretch to the turn‑off for Coffee Bay became rather dull. This part of South Africa seems especially poor, and we repeatedly passed bleak corrugated‑iron shanty settlements.

The route through the coastal region of the Transkei was more cheerful again: lovely traditional Xhosa villages of round huts dot the beautiful rolling hills all the way to the sea. Despite its relative remoteness, Coffee Bay is quite touristy, but still a relaxed little place with a lagoon on a wildly romantic green coast.

We spent the night at Kwa Tshezi Lodge, a would-be luxury hotel, which was highly entertaining. The rooms were nice and very spacious, but far too expensive (R255 per person B&B) and somehow felt unfinished. The lounge, on the other hand, was genuinely tasteful and luxurious, and in the evening there were free oysters and other snacks at the bar.

The service was completely inept, and the head chef seemed to think he was Bocuse in person. His beautifully arranged “nouvelle cuisine” was, dessert aside, pretty generic in flavour, and the tiny portions were overpriced. In this wild, remote region, the whole scenario was an almost surreal experience.



## Day 20 – 22 September

Because it rained today, we drove the 400 km straight through to Durban. The route took us through the former Transkei homeland and wasn’t especially scenic, apart from the spectacular Kei River Pass.

We arrived fairly late in Durban and checked into City Lodge, not far from the beach. The room was small, but very comfortable (R405).

Since Durban is strongly influenced by Indian culture, we decided to go for an Indian meal. Jewel of India at Holiday Inn North Beach is meant to be one of the city’s best. The restaurant was rather upmarket and pleasantly furnished. The food was excellent too and, for that standard, good value.



## Day 21 – 23 September

Today we spent the whole day in Durban, an attractive, and above all interesting, city, because Indian, African and Western influences mix here. The markets are lively and entertaining; the centre is a combination of modern, sleek shopping arcades and old colonial buildings; and the city sits on a 6 km beachfront promenade, a seaside resort right in the middle of a big city.

## Day 23 – 25 September

Further south along the Hibiscus Coast it’s still lovely, but more built‑up than the Sunshine Coast. There’s even a resort called Margate where, coconut palms aside, you could almost imagine you were on the Costa Brava.

From Port Edward the road turned inland through the Transkei. The landscape and villages here aren’t as beautiful as further south. The approach to Port St Johns, at a river mouth, was breathtaking though.

Port St Johns itself is small and not particularly attractive, but the surroundings are wonderful: tropical vegetation, lagoons and beaches; Second Beach in particular is of magical beauty.

## Day 28 – 30 September

Today we passed Storms River Canyon, the official start of South Africa’s famous (and touristy) Garden Route. Our first stop was the “Big Tree”, as the name suggests, a very big tree, marketed as a major attraction in woodland‑poor South Africa. It’s a pleasant little walk along a boardwalk through dense forest to reach it; the tree is impressive enough, but not in the same league as California’s redwoods.

A different calibre altogether is Storms River Mouth in Tsitsikamma National Park. Unfortunately it’s also a stop for coach tours, but the coastal scenery here is enchanting, and a welcome change from the dune beaches so far: steep, densely wooded cliffs; the mouth of the Storms River Canyon spanned by a suspension bridge; small sand-and-shingle bays; and grand views from the View Point, a punishing 45‑minute walk from the resort (so punishing that the package tourists don’t make it up there, which, honestly, is half the appeal).

The whole region is magnificent, with vineyards nestled between high mountains. Dinner was at the La Couronne wine estate in Franschhoek. In the afternoon, in glorious weather, we took the cable car (R85) up Table Mountain. This experience once again belonged in the category “unforgettable”, and above all, “impossible to describe”.

When we came back down after sunset, the day still wasn’t over: at Saigon in Gardens we were served the best Vietnamese food outside Vietnam. Pure bliss!

## Day 44 – 16 October

After breakfast at Tuscany Beach in Camps Bay, we made a brief detour to Llandudno, an exclusive, quiet Atlantic suburb with a gorgeous sandy bay.

Then we took the inland route to Noordhoek, where we had lunch on a lovely terrace at Red Herring, before driving the reopened southern section of Chapman’s Peak Drive, which served up yet more unbelievable views, this time over the five-kilometre stretch of white dune beach between Noordhoek and Kommetjie.

## Days 45–48 – 17–20 October

We spent these last days pretty much just enjoying life in Cape Town. The day usually began with breakfast (around 11) at Tuscany Beach in Camps Bay; other activities included Waterfront shopping, visiting the bars and cafés of Long Street, a harbour cruise on a sailing boat, and going out to eat.

Yindees, The Restaurant, Balduccis … and the obligatory, sobering visit to Robben Island as a contrast to our decadent life of indulgence.
