# Cycling Southeast Europe
**1 August – 15 September 2005**

A bicycle journey from Venice through the western Balkans to Hungary.

---


## Day 1 — 1 Aug — 54 km

*Venice Airport – Jesolo – Cortellazzo – Caorle*

We only landed in Venice late in the afternoon (HLX, €25) and, without
so much as a glance at that gorgeous city, pointed our bikes straight
east through dead-flat, fairly uninspiring lowlands. The only “climbs”
were the endless little bridges over assorted canals.

Up to Jesolo the traffic was unpleasantly heavy; after that, though, we
had cosy little back roads all the way into darkness. We didn’t roll
into Campsite Lagomare (€13.50 p.p.), just before Caorle, until about 10
p.m., and to our great surprise we were almost the only guests. The
pitches were excellent and the toilet and shower facilities were the
best and most modern I’ve ever seen in Italy: everything looked brand
new.

## Day 2 — 2 Aug — 94 km

*Caorle – Latisana – Ronchi – Monfalcone*

Another dead-flat day, this time with a headwind, and neither the
scenery nor the culture offered much to get excited about; mostly we
ground along the busy SS14. Still, as everywhere in Italy, you can get a
superb cappuccino in virtually any bar along the way, and that counts
for something.

We spent the night in the very basic Hotel alla Carlina on the SS14 at
the entrance to Monfalcone. The room (€48) was old-fashioned but
functional, and came with a large balcony.

## Day 3 — 3 Aug — 75 km

*Monfalcone – Trieste – Basovizza – Kozina – Obrov*

The flat part was over today: just outside Monfalcone the Trieste
Riviera begins, a very pretty, green, steep coastline where the SS14
runs high above the sea. Under normal circumstances it would have meant
glorious views. Unfortunately, the weather was miserable: rain, cold,
and leaden skies, easily one of the worst August days in years on this
stretch of the Mediterranean.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0010.jpg" alt="Riviera di Trieste">
  <em>Riviera di Trieste</em>
</figure>

We did manage to use a longer dry spell in the morning to sit outside in
one of Trieste’s wonderful cafés and have a quick look around. Despite
being on the Mediterranean, Trieste feels more Central European, hardly
surprising given its history. Either way, it’s an attractive city:
beautifully set, with plenty of splendid 19th-century buildings and that
famous café culture. Trieste is always worth a visit.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0018.jpg" alt="Trieste">
  <em>Trieste</em>
</figure>

After the city, it was kilometres of steep climbing up to the Slovenian
border at 500 metres. A thoroughly nasty stretch: narrow road, lots of
lorries. In the last sizeable place before the border, the already very
Slovenian-feeling Basovizza, we ended up taking an unexpectedly long
lunch break as heavy rain returned. At least the village had Trattoria
alla Pesa, which produced a huge portion of delicious calamari with
chard and a potato-and-rocket salad (€13). And Ice Café served some of
the best ice cream I’ve ever eaten.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0026.jpg" alt="Bassovizza - Trattoria alla Pesa">
  <em>Bassovizza - Trattoria alla Pesa</em>
</figure>

Across the border the road ran through green, rolling hills, mostly
short climbs and descents, and despite rough tarmac and plenty of
lorries it was still rideable. Luckily the rain eventually gave up. We
stayed at Motel Finida right by the road, but in lovely countryside. For
a motel, both the rooms (€40) and the restaurant and bar were rather
smart and tastefully done. The food was cheap and very good: spit-roast
suckling pig (1100 SIT) and grilled calamari (1300 SIT).

## Day 4 — 4 Aug — 98 km

*Obrov – Rijeka – Crikvenica*

It rained all night. At 10 a.m. it still hadn’t stopped, so we set off
anyway, riding in driving rain and cold for the first 40-odd kilometres
to Rijeka, hilly and generally uphill to the Croatian border, followed
by a long descent down to the coast.

Once we reached Rijeka, the rain stopped for the first time and it was
just about warm enough to sit in a street café. The big, hectic port
city isn’t much to look at overall, except for Korzo: a long, wide
pedestrian zone with a few handsome façades and cosy street cafés that
were packed even in this miserable weather.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0032.jpg" alt="Rijeka">

</figure>

After Rijeka we joined the Adriatic Highway, with some long but not
particularly steep climbs and descents. Traffic on the sometimes quite
narrow road was a disaster, lorries and holiday traffic, but the views
of Krk and the coastline below were lovely. In the afternoon we had our
usual lunch in a tavern on the highway above the Bay of Bakar: tasty
seafood risotto (40 K), grilled squid (40 K) and meat skewers (27 K).
Overnight in a private room (sobe) just above the road shortly before
Crikvenica: 200 Kuna for a simple room with kitchen and bathroom.

## Day 5 — 5 Aug — 105 km

*Crikvenica – Senj – Jablanac – Rab*

The first 40 kilometres to Senj were easy riding. The road stayed mostly
right along the coast, very hilly but without truly long climbs, and
with less traffic than yesterday. The views were fabulous all along the
Adriatic Highway: a nearly bare island world in deep-blue sea.

We spent the morning in Senj, maybe not Croatia’s most visually
impressive harbour town, but certainly one of the most dramatically
located, right under the massive coastal mountains, opposite the island
of Krk. In the old town there were a few cafés and konobas, but far
fewer than in more touristy places. Down at the harbour it was less
pleasant, thanks to the busy main road running right along the water.

We ate tasty mussels (50 K) and a very average risotto nero (50 K) in
friendly Konoba Stari Grad. The next 40 kilometres to Jablanac were much
more demanding than the earlier stretch, and just as spectacular. It
was kilometres of climbing, with dream views of Rab, Krk, Pag and almost
the whole Kvarner island world.

Up on the ridge the wind today was sometimes so extreme (Bora in

slope, luckily mostly from behind or the side. When gusts hit head-on,
riding was simply impossible. A long descent finally brought us into the
picturesque little village of Jablanac, where we took the ferry to the
island of Rab. The crossing (10 K) took only about 20 minutes.

The remaining 10-plus kilometres on Rab to Rab Town were scenic but very
hilly, with lots of short, steep climbs and drops. Rab is, at first
glance quite rightly, one of the prettiest little towns on the Adriatic,
with its picturesque old town and palm-lined harbour promenade, but
right now it’s terribly hectic and overcrowded. We also arrived quite
late, so there wasn’t much time to explore before dark.

Dinner, however, was an excellent choice: on the lovely terrace of
Konoba Labirint in a narrow old-town alley. A fish platter with two
grilled fish, calamari, mussels, scampi and risotto (220 K for two).
Finding a room was a bit of a challenge: many places were full, and most
wouldn’t rent for just one night. After some asking around we did manage
to find something decent and close to the old town for 120 K p.p.

## Day 6 — 6 Aug — 69 km

*Rab – Lun – Pag – Dinjista*

Because the only ferry to Pag didn’t leave until noon, we had plenty of
time in the morning for a proper look around. Rab really is beautiful, 
architecturally and in terms of setting, and in the morning it wasn’t
yet so frantic and crowded.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0098.jpg" alt="Rab Town">
  <em>Rab Town</em>
</figure>

The best café in town is Astoria, wonderfully placed right on the main
square, with extremely comfortable armchairs and excellent cappuccino (7
K). Before leaving Rab we had a bite to eat at Vinoteka Paradiso, the
chicest, most stylish place in town in terms of ambience and interior,
with a lovely patio. Unfortunately the food was a bit uninspired and
overpriced (octopus salad 70 K; grilled squid 80 K).

The “ferry” to Pag turned out to be an overcrowded old motorboat, while
our bikes were transported separately in a little cargo barge to Lun,
the northern tip of Pag. Everything felt highly improvised, I almost
felt like I was back in Southeast Asia. The crossing (60 K) took about
an hour and was very pleasant; from the bow, where we sat, we had
terrific views.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0100.jpg" alt="Rab - Lun Ferry">
  <em>Rab - Lun Ferry</em>
</figure>

The first 20 kilometres on Pag were a rollercoaster on a quiet, partly
poor road through bleak, dry-stone-wall scenery, with sea views on both
sides. Near Novalja we reached the busier main road, which twice crossed
the island’s central ridge in long climbs and descents, with wonderful
views of the mainland mountains on one side and a multitude of little
islands on the other, before finally dropping in steep hairpins down to
Pag Town.

The island’s main town, bright and Renaissance in style, may not have
the grandeur and beauty of some other Croatian island capitals, but it’s
still very pretty. In a few winding lanes it even feels quite unspoilt, 
a place that turns into a tourist hub in the short summer season, yet
where locals still live normal lives.

We had dinner in prime position on the harbour promenade at Restaurant
Dubrava: Paški sir (35 K), of course, the island’s famous sheep’s
cheese; Dalmatian air-dried ham (40 K); and two large grilled sardines
with chard and potatoes (40 K), which even I liked. A very good choice.

Afterwards we cycled another 15 kilometres south, an easy ride with a
tailwind and hardly any climbing. In Dinjista we pitched our tent at
Kamp Milka: more the garden of a private house than a campsite, tiny,
with grassy pitches right by the sea and good toilet and shower
facilities (100 K). Beautiful.

## Day 7 — 7 Aug — 74 km

*Dinjista – Zadar – Preko – Tkon – Biograd*

The first 20 kilometres weren’t technically demanding, but with the
gusty wind blasting down from the mainland they were barely rideable.
Over the next 20 kilometres inland to Zadar we at least had the wind at
our backs, though the road was very hilly.

Zadar is a large city (by Croatian standards) with two faces. The modern
part is unattractive, with many surprisingly run-down prefab blocks. The
old town, enclosed and largely ringed by fortifications, is one of
Dalmatia’s tourist highlights, and on this August Sunday it was
accordingly packed and busy. The whole area is car-free, with paths
partly paved in polished marble. The prettiest piazzas and lanes are
blessed with countless street cafés: ideal for people-watching.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0137.jpg" alt="Zadar">
  <em>Zadar</em>
</figure>

We ate a small lunch in one of the few restaurants in the old town and
missed the ferry to Preko by less than a minute, which earned us another
75 minutes of killing time in Zadar. Meanwhile the already rather dull
weather deteriorated further, and shortly after the 20-minute crossing
(29 K) to the island of Ugljan it started pouring and didn’t stop.

The 30 kilometres across Ugljan and Pašman would undoubtedly have been a
pleasant ride (nice scenery, no dramatic climbs, the sea always in
view, little traffic), but I couldn’t enjoy any of it. It was bucketing
down, everything grey on grey, and by the time I reached Tkon I was
soaked through and shivering with cold (August in Croatia!).

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0143.jpg" alt="Ugljan Island">
  <em>Ugljan Island</em>
</figure>

We took the ferry back to the mainland to Biograd (15 min, 20 K), a
pretty town in a beautiful setting… in theory… because today it didn’t
matter in the slightest. I just wanted to get out of the rain: a decent
room and a hot shower. An agency sorted us out quickly; we moved into a
room in the old town, quite pleasant, with a nice (though shared)
bathroom, for €38.

Dinner at friendly and very popular Restaurant Guste around the corner
was excellent: both the mussels (60 K/kg) and the very generous seafood
platter (90 K).

## Day 8 — 8 Aug — 120 km

*Biograd – Šibenik – Primošten – Marina – Seget Donji*

With bright sunshine in the morning I naturally treated myself to a
cappuccino on Biograd’s lovely harbour promenade before setting off,
south on the highway towards Split. Gentle hills, easy riding and fine
views of the Kornati islands marked the first 50 kilometres to Šibenik,
where we took a long lunch break.

From the bypass road, which we luckily avoided, Šibenik looks like one
of the ugliest cities in Europe, with industrial plants and prefab
blocks beyond description. The historic centre by the sea, however, is
perhaps Croatia’s finest old town (fairy-tale Dubrovnik excepted):
clinging to the hillside above the harbour, a labyrinth of winding
alleys and steps, a fortress, café-lined piazzas and unspoilt stone
houses. Naturally it was heaving today, too.

We had an excellent Dalmatian lunch at Highland Pub, despite the name:
mussels in tomato-onion sauce (60 K) and a large portion of delicious
grilled calamari (88 K). Highly recommended.

The next stage to Primošten was pure enjoyment: always right along the
coast, blazing sunshine, turquoise sea, the offshore islands, a feast
for the eyes. Primošten itself lies slightly off the road on a
peninsula: a charming little town, partly enclosed by walls, with a
palm-lined harbour promenade and cosy cafés. From above, from the
viewpoints along the road, Primošten looks utterly stunning, mainly
thanks to its setting in gorgeous scenery.

The hilliest part of the day was between Primošten and Marina, where the
road left the coast for about 20 kilometres before returning to the
water near the pretty little town of Marina. In terms of both scenery
and weather, today was the high point of the tour so far.

We stayed in a lovely private room with an enormous sea-view terrace
(200 K) in Seget Donji, just before Trogir. From the road it doesn’t
look like much, but it’s a real insider tip. Down by the sea Seget has a
small old town with a wonderfully unforced atmosphere, few tourists, and
very cheap cafés and konobas. So close to the tourist bustle of Trogir
and Marina, you could still watch unfiltered Dalmatian life.

We also ate very well at the friendly Konoba Rico with a large grill:
delicious ćevapčići (32 K) and a whole grilled fish (70 K). Cheap and
good, and in a great atmosphere.

## Day 9 — 9 Aug — 98 km

*Seget Donji – Kaštela – Omiš – Makarska*

The first 20-odd kilometres to Split’s multi-lane bypass were flat and
uneventful on a rather bumpy road. We left Split to one side and
continued south along the Adriatic Highway.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0192.jpg" alt="Split">
  <em>Split</em>
</figure>

The roughly 30 kilometres to Omiš would have been a relaxed ride, 
mostly right by the sea, only gently hilly. Unfortunately the traffic
was so heavy that it often backed up; on a narrow road it isn’t always
easy to find a gap on a bicycle. It’s about time work started on a
motorway to Dubrovnik; in summer the highway is hopelessly overloaded.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0197.jpg" alt="Dalmatia - Adria Magistrale">

The coast here is pretty, but heavily built up. Omiš, despite some ugly
holiday complexes and prefab blocks, is a very attractive place, with an
old-town core at the dramatic mouth of the Cetina where the gorge

Given the fierce competition (there must be hundreds of cafés and
restaurants, often very tastefully done), Makarska’s prices are
surprisingly low. Even on the harbour promenade you pay only 6 Kuna for
a macchiato, including at stylish, comfortable Pink Panther Café on the
main square.

Finding a room was, unsurprisingly, difficult, but via an agency we did
secure accommodation, high up on the slope, but only ten minutes from
the harbour: 200 Kuna for a decent room with a small bathroom.

We had another excellent dinner at Gostionica Mondo, a friendly grill
place near the beach: calf’s liver (45 K) and grilled calamari (60 K),
plus Šopska salad (20 K) and mussels (50 K).

## Day 10 — 10 Aug — 39 km

*Makarska – Sumartin – Bol – Jelsa*

A broken spoke stopped us from riding further down the highway to
Drvenik as planned and crossing to Hvar from there. Since we couldn’t
get the bike fixed in Makarska, we decided to minimise wear and tear and
just get to Split. At 10:30 we took the ferry to Sumartin on Brač (25 K
p.p., 29 K for the bike).

The 25 kilometres to Bol on the island’s south side were very demanding:
kilometres of climbing up to almost 500 metres, followed by a glorious
eight-kilometre serpentine descent down to the sea, with fantastic views
of Hvar opposite, the town of Bol and the famous “Golden Horn”.

This beach, one of Croatia’s most-postcarded motifs, was a slight
disappointment, though less because of the beach itself than because it
was so overcrowded you could hardly make it out, let alone see anything
“golden”. In the off-season, as the postcards suggest, the Golden Horn
must be rather picturesque.

Bol itself is picturesque even in high season: one of those typically
secluded coastal towns that give Croatia its particular charm. Naturally
it also has plenty of street cafés along the waterfront. In the nicest
of them, the chic, extremely comfortable cocktail bar Varadero, I spent
almost the whole afternoon nursing a good but expensive cappuccino.

We had planned to take the daily Jadrolinija ferry to Jelsa on Hvar at
17:10, but discovered it didn’t take bicycles, despite our best efforts
to talk our way on. No chance. So we chartered a taxi boat to Jelsa
instead. Not cheap (600 K), but the hour-long private ride was one of
the highlights of the holiday, and worth the money.

Jelsa, too, is an incredibly picturesque little harbour town in a small
bay on the south of Hvar. With little hope of finding a free private
room, we went straight to the nearby campsite, also overflowing.
Luckily we still found a tiny spot for our tent behind the sanitary
block, right above the coast. Anything that qualified as somewhere to
sleep.

For dinner we cycled back into town and, for once, didn’t eat the usual
grilled fare: at Taverna Arsenal we had French cuisine with an Asian
twist. Fine frog’s legs Thai-style (48 K), a mixed fish platter (140 K),
and leg of lamb in rosemary sauce (110 K). Not cheap, but a welcome
change from Dalmatian standard cooking.

## Day 11 — 11 Aug — 50 km

*Jelsa – Stari Grad – Hvar – Split – Kaštela*

A fairly easy ride from Jelsa to pretty Stari Grad, Hvar’s main ferry
port, with a nice waterfront promenade and a labyrinthine old town of
stone houses and narrow lanes.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0274.jpg" alt="Hvar Island - Stari Grad">
  <em>Hvar Island - Stari Grad</em>
</figure>

From Stari Grad we climbed for a few kilometres to a 1,500-metre tunnel
that leads to the island’s south side. From there it was another 10
kilometres over a very hilly road to Hvar Town, with fine coastal views.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0276.jpg" alt="Hvar Island">
  <em>Hvar Island</em>
</figure>

We had originally planned to stay the night, but found out the
Jadrolinija morning ferry didn’t take bicycles, so we were forced to
rely on today’s afternoon sailing. Not a great tragedy, chances are we
wouldn’t have found a room anyway.

Hvar may be Croatia’s prettiest island town: the fortress above the old
town, the marble-paved piazza, the palm-lined waterfront with chic cafés
and millionaire yachts, the views of the little islands offshore… but in
August it’s also a magnet for every type of tourist, from young
backpackers to the jet set. The place was bursting at the seams, and the
crowds were at times almost stressful. A shame, because I’ve seen Hvar

## Day 12 — 12 Aug — 37 km

*Kaštela – Trogir – Marina*

In charming touristy Trogir (UNESCO World Heritage, after all) we

In the afternoon we cycled back into town and managed to finish dinner

## Day 13 — 26 Aug — 50 km

*Split – Vela Luka – Korčula*

Today was the hardest day of the tour so far. Whether that was due to
the two-week holiday break in Sevid, the blazing sunshine at just under
30°C, or the route itself, I’m not entirely sure.

After an excellent cappuccino at cosy F2 on Split’s harbour promenade,
we took the 10 o’clock ferry to Vela Luka on the island of Korčula. The
crossing (29 Kuna p.p. + 31 Kuna for the bike) took 3.5 hours, with a
short stop in Hvar.

Vela Luka is a pleasant, unspectacular place on Korčula’s beautiful west
coast. The first 20 kilometres through the island’s interior were more
rolling than mountainous, but after the little village of Blato things
got serious: kilometres of often steep climbing with little shade, up to
almost 600 metres above sea level.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0458.jpg" alt="Korcula Island">
  <em>Korcula Island</em>
</figure>

The descent down to Korčula Town was, despite the poor road, pure joy, 
and well earned. Korčula is one of the most famous and prettiest
Dalmatian towns, with its medieval core dramatically set on a rocky
promontory: watchtowers, palazzi and narrow lanes packed with charming
little konobas and cafés.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0466.jpg" alt="Korcula Town">
  <em>Korcula Town</em>
</figure>

Tonight’s sunset was a dream. Croatian sunsets over the Adriatic islands
are among the most beautiful in Europe, and since the summer has been a
near disaster weather-wise, today was the first completely cloudless
day, which also meant the first unspoilt sunset.

Overnight in a very central private room, too expensive at 300 Kuna,
but with a splendid view of the old-town core and the Pelješac peninsula
opposite.

Aside from the topography it was pleasant enough: hardly any traffic and

## Day 15 — 28 Aug — 70 km

*Babino Polje – Sobra – Dubrovnik – Cavtat – Dubrovnik*

Since I’d slept up in the mountains, I assumed it would be all downhill
to the ferry port, but this wouldn’t be Mljet if there wasn’t one more
climb before the final descent. The ferry port is about two kilometres
outside Sobra, and at 9 a.m. the ferry arrived on time with Achim from
Korčula.

The crossing to Dubrovnik took just under two hours and was very scenic,
right through the Elaphiti islands. Less enjoyable was the outrageously
overpriced fare: the bicycle alone cost 155 Kuna and the person 57 Kuna.
International prices, since the ferry continued to Bari.

At the harbour you were almost attacked by sobe touts, it seems the
season was already winding down. We took a room a little above the port
and then cycled into the old town to drink an expensive macchiato (12
Kuna) at Café Hemingway in a prime spot.

along the way.

Scenically the coast is gorgeous here as well: lonely pebble bays, views
of the Elaphiti and Pelješac. The road, as expected, was hilly

skewers with chard and black risotto (15 KM).

## Day 19 — 1 Sep — Rest day

*Sarajevo*

We spent the day in Sarajevo, meaning Achim spent most of it ill in the
room, and I spent it wandering the streets, drinking coffee and
marvelling at how many traces of the war had been cleared since my last
visit three years ago.

No more burnt-out high-rise shells on the way into the centre; and even
in the old town itself, apart from the pockmarked façades, there’s
little visible destruction left. Sarajevo is almost a normal, very
attractive big city again, architecturally and culturally shaped by the
Ottomans, the Habsburgs and Yugoslav socialism.

*Jakeš – Modriča – Bosanska Šamac – Vinkovci – Vukovar*

After about 20 kilometres along the Bosna, Bosanska Šamac, the border
town to Croatia, appeared. It at least has a pleasant little pedestrian
zone with lots of street cafés.

On the Croatian side we followed a flat, easy back road through farmland
to Vinkovci, a rather pretty Baroque town where we stopped for lunch.
After Bosnia everything suddenly felt very Central European and tidy.

After a good lunch (grilled chicken, mushrooms and Šopska salad for 62
Kuna) at Pizzeria Lamut, we continued to the Danube and our destination
for the day: Vukovar.

Once a pretty Baroque town on the mighty Danube, Vukovar today is a
war-destroyed city where, even 14 years after independence, hardly a
house stands unscathed. Between the ruins of once-very-beautiful
buildings, the odd new building has popped up, and the ground floors of
some ruins are often used as cafés.

excursion restaurant.

We stayed in the brand-new 4-star Hotel Luv right next to a burnt-out

We didn’t go out to eat, but café-wise Vukovar is arguably the cheapest

## Day 23 — 5 Sep — 92 km

*Vukovar – Ilok – Bačka Palanka – Novi Sad*

The first 40 kilometres to the Serbian border roughly followed the
Danube, but a bit inland and quite hilly, an unspectacular, lightly
trafficked stretch. The border crossing into Serbia was completely
hassle-free. Not long ago we’d have needed visas; now it’s all easy.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0763.jpg" alt="Along the Danube">

The tourist infrastructure in this region is surprisingly good: we got
our Bosnian marks exchanged, and in and around Bačka Palanka there were
plenty of Sobe/rooms signs. In a motel on the road to Novi Sad we could
immediately familiarise ourselves with Serbian prices, and, above all,
portions. For 950 dinars we were served a ludicrously large “house
specialities for two” platter: meat plain, breaded, stuffed, spicy
paprika sausage, and potatoes. Tasty, but appalling.

We took a simple, decent room at Hotel Vojvodina right in the centre

very pleasant, attractive city on the Danube, with a nice city park, the
massive Petrovaradin fortress on the opposite bank, and a freshly

## Day 24 — 6 Sep — 84 km

*Novi Sad – Petrovaradin – Sremski Karlovci – Inđija – Belgrade*

The first 30 kilometres to Inđija ran over the foothills of Fruška Gora
and were very hilly, including a kilometre-long climb after the pretty
town of Karlovci, once one of Serbia’s most important places. The road
surface was mostly catastrophic, it’s a wonder no spoke broke.

Today, for the first time since the Croatian coast, we met other cycle
tourists: two Austrians on their way to India. Otherwise Serbia seems to
see hardly any tourists.

After Inđija it flattened out again, but the narrow, poor road and heavy
traffic made progress laborious. We first reached the pretty Danube
suburb of Zemun, once an independent town, now swallowed by Belgrade.
The main attraction is the many nice fish restaurants on the Danube
bank, but Zemun’s centre is also quite appealing.

## Day 25 — 7 Sep — 37 km

*Belgrade (city day)*

After a well-earned “people’s breakfast” (a drink and a small
“sandwich”) at the hotel, we explored Belgrade. We cycled some of the
nice paths along the Danube and Sava, especially on the Danube side,
further south, there are many more attractive boat cafés and
restaurants, such as the comfortable Wooloomooloo, where we had our
morning cappuccino.

Afterwards we circled Belgrade’s city beaches at Ada Ciganlija, the
large artificial lake on the Sava. This is Belgrade’s leisure paradise:
kilometres of pebble beaches, lots of beach bars, and cycle paths
through the adjoining park.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0813.jpg" alt="Belgrade - Ada Ciganlija">
  <em>Belgrade - Ada Ciganlija</em>
</figure>

And it’s precisely this location (where Danube and Sava meet, with all
those excursion cafés on the rivers, parks and swimming options) that
gives Belgrade its appeal. The city itself is hectic and chaotic, and
has been damaged in so many wars that you can’t really call it beautiful
as a whole. That doesn’t mean there aren’t pretty corners in the centre.

Knez Mihailova really is lovely, with handsome façades and dozens of
street cafés. Also very nice is the large city park with the medieval
citadel on the hill above the rivers, offering fine views. The old
bohemian quarter Skadarlija is pleasant too, with cobbled lanes and many
restaurants and cafés.

In terms of bars and cafés, Belgrade lives up to its status as a
metropolis: you’re spoilt for choice among hundreds of options, from
cosy and rustic to big-city chic, everything exists. Culinarily,
though, there’s still a lot of catching up to do. Apart from a few
Italians and the usual suspects (a handful of relatively expensive
Chinese places, French bistros, tapas bars), there are few options for
creative or exotic meals.

We did, however, eat Serbian food very well at Tri Šešira in Skadarlija:
slightly more upmarket cooking: mućkalica (450 dinars), tasty Šopska
salad (170 dinars), grilled mushrooms (330 dinars) and stuffed peppers
(240 dinars), at fair prices.

In the evening we shared a very fine Andalusian salad with marinated
veal at Tribeca. This extremely chic, not exactly cheap bar-café came
closest, menu-wise, to modern fusion cuisine. The portion was
Serbian-generous and thus at 450 dinars absolutely reasonable.

Then the evening got properly fun. I popped into Central Bar, a fairly
smart cocktail bar opposite our hotel, where apparently everyone knew
everyone and it felt almost like a private party. I was soon chatting
away and even got a few cocktails on the house, but even the paid
cocktails were very cheap (195 dinars) and competently mixed. Very nice
people, these Serbs.

to be the only larger settlement in the region. It even had a chic club

*Timişoara – Sânnicolau Mare – Szeged*

Another flat stage through lonely agricultural country, with a tailwind
and very good roads, at least up to the border. On the Hungarian side
it was less pleasant.

Szeged is a splendid little town with many architecturally impressive

tranquil, it lacks, perhaps, the slightly shabby charm of Eastern

## Day 29 — 11 Sep — 108 km

*Szeged – Baja*

After properly fortifying ourselves at our hotel’s generous breakfast
buffet, we left Szeged westwards on Route 55. Once again the surface
wasn’t great, with frequent ruts, but at least the road wasn’t banned
for bicycles, which in Hungary counts as a small miracle.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBalkans2005-0894.jpg" alt="Szeged">
  <em>Szeged</em>
</figure>

We didn’t set off particularly early today, because it rained in the
morning for the first time in almost three weeks. Still, we managed the
100-odd kilometres to Baja without trouble, since the route was mostly
flat, becoming only gently rolling towards the end.

Once again we rode through thinly populated, not very interesting
agricultural country. In Melykút, one of the few villages en route, we
ate a quick lunch in simple Taverne Magellan: good and cheap.

Our destination, Baja, is a pleasant little provincial town on the
Danube, with a small pedestrian zone, a fairly grand old-town square
that is unfortunately still partly used as a car park, and a few nice
cafés and bars down by the river, with slightly lower prices than
Szeged.

At Daniels Caffè an excellent cappuccino cost 255 Ft; at Serrano Saloon
a half-litre of beer was 250 Ft, plus a generous portion of tasty,
viciously hot tacos for 1,290 Ft. We had a nice modern room at Pension
Kaiser for 9,000 Ft.

## Day 31 — 13 Sep — 83 km

*Pécs – Barcs – Koprivnica*

Pressed for time (and because the road to the Croatian border was, yet
again, closed to bicycles) we took the train from Pécs to Barcs. Taking
the bikes was straightforward, and the roughly 90-minute ride cost only
140 forints.

We crossed the border and first rode on quiet back roads; from Stari
Gradec onwards we continued on the busier N2, through many roadworks, to
Koprivnica, a quiet but quite pretty provincial town with a car-free
centre, lots of cafés and a large city park.

There was really nothing to do here, but the town has a good rail

## Days 31–32 — 14–15 Sep — 25 km

*Koprivnica – Zagreb – Stuttgart*

The train ride (44 Kuna) from Koprivnica to Zagreb took about 90
minutes. Taking the bikes was no problem and cost only a small tip for
the conductor.

In Zagreb there was a trade fair and most hotels were fully booked, bad
timing. But Zagreb is a catastrophe for accommodation anyway: too few
hotels, and the ones that exist are either luxurious and exorbitantly
expensive, or bad and still overpriced.

We were lucky nonetheless. The receptionist at fully booked Hotel Jadra
organised a private room for us. It was a bit outside the centre, up on
a hill, and at €50 too expensive, but that’s Zagreb for you.

Otherwise the city really is beautiful. And one of Europe’s most
underrated cities, without the tourist crowds that descend on the
Croatian coast or other Central European capitals like Budapest or
Prague. Zagreb offers everything that makes a city break worthwhile.

restaurant with sushi and fusion, what a delight after weeks of solid
home cooking. The food was of course very expensive, but worth every
cent: flawless nigiri, creative futomaki and hosomaki, delicious foie
gras, wok beef, seafood salad… a genuine feast without a single misstep,
for a good €100 with a bottle of Rosemount.
