# Burma / Myanmar 2008
**August 2008**

A journey through Burma / Myanmar in the difficult months after Cyclone Nargis, from Rangoon and the Golden Rock to Naypyidaw, Inle Lake and Bagan.

---

## 2 August 2008
### Rangoon
The conditions for my second visit to the Land of the Golden Pagodas couldn’t have been much worse. Tourism had already dropped sharply after last September’s unrest, and then in May this year the devastating cyclone Nargis dealt the industry another blow. Even online there was hardly any post-Nargis information about travelling in the country, because apparently almost no tourists were daring to come at the moment. I’d also heard a few stories about problems with visa applications, even refusals, but I had no trouble getting a 28-day visa at the embassy in Bangkok.

After the dramatic fall in visitor numbers, AirAsia now flies from Bangkok to Rangoon only three times a week. The flight (3,800 baht return) was still fairly full, with at most a dozen Westerners, and everything went smoothly.

We arrived in Rangoon around eight in the morning and took a taxi into town ($6) to Okinawa Guesthouse, very centrally located on 32nd Street near the Sule Pagoda. A fairly spacious, comfortably furnished room with a fridge, a chest of drawers, two decent beds and a mosquito net cost $15 for two people. There was air con but no hot water, and in the rainy season everything feels a bit damp. Thanks to its location, Okinawa seems to have replaced the Motherland Inn as the most popular budget place in town. The only travellers we met during our two days in Rangoon were all staying there too.

Rangoon hasn’t really changed over the last five years. One of the most exhausting, and at the same time most fascinating, cities in Asia, it’s a wild mix of subcontinental chaos and grime, Southeast Asian temples, and picturesque colonial architecture: some nicely restored, but many are dilapidated, crumbling away.

The city centre around Sule Paya is loud and hectic. The pavements are overcrowded obstacle courses, the streets are full of potholes, and unbelievably ancient buses belch exhaust into the air… This is the pulsating heart of a five-million-strong metropolis that is already asleep by 11 p.m., where hours-long power cuts are normal, and yet it has other sides, too.

The neighbourhood around the legendary Shwedagon Pagoda is much calmer, with stylish cafés and crumbling colonial villas under coconut palms. Just east of that, Kandawgyi Lake is another welcome change: plenty of open-air bars and restaurants along the green shore, great views of the elegant (though currently eerily deserted) Palace Hotel and the Shwedagon, and a lovely walking path along the southern edge, part of it on wooden planks out over the water.

Because we arrived on a Friday, we ended up at the Strand Hotel in the afternoon. This old grand place, set in a colonial building on Strand Road, is one of Asia’s most famous hotels and offers a generous happy hour every Friday in its classically elegant Strand Bar. From 5 to 11 p.m. all drinks are half price, which is an excellent deal because the drink prices (despite the outrageous room rates) are fairly moderate to begin with. During happy hour a cocktail costs $3 and a draught beer $1. Later we had dinner at Golden City Chetty, a very basic, popular Indian place on Sule Paya Road: cheap but perfectly solid thalis in a frantic, zero-comfort atmosphere.

We spent the second day at Kandawgyi Lake, then visited the Shwedagon Pagoda, which is still, on a second visit too, one of the highlights of Southeast Asia.

We ended the day with dinner at the atmospheric Sandy’s, a slightly more upmarket but completely unpretentious restaurant with attentive service, traditional Burmese food, and a very nice location with a lakeside terrace at Kandawgyi. The place is clearly suffering from the lack of tourists and didn’t have every dish from its long menu available, but what did arrive was good and not overpriced. I ordered duck eggs stuffed with minced prawns (3,000k++) and a duck curry (4,000k++). Burmese cuisine, unlike that of its neighbours, definitely isn’t one of the world’s great cuisines, but Sandy’s is probably rightly considered one of the best Burmese restaurants in the city.

You can get a perfectly decent cappuccino for 1,500 kyat at Café Aroma on Sule Paya Road. This Burmese take on a café chain would pass for a canteen elsewhere, but in Rangoon city centre it’s genuinely one of the smarter options.

## 4 August 2008
### Golden Rock
From Rangoon city centre it’s a 45-minute taxi ride to Aung Mingalar bus terminal (5,000k), where buses leave for almost every destination in the country. We got there around 9:30 a.m. and were able to catch a bus to Kyaikto straight away, though we overpaid for the tickets (7,000k) because otherwise we’d have had to stand, or wait for the next bus. The trip took a bit over four hours and by Burmese standards the bus was acceptable. From Kyaikto it was another half hour in a pick-up truck to Kinpun, a small village with two guesthouses and the starting point for the Golden Rock trucks.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/Burma2008-0019.jpg" alt="Rangoon Bus Terminal">
  <em>Rangoon Bus Terminal</em>
</figure>

Around 3:30 we were able to check in at the Pann Myo Thu Inn. It’s the worse of the two, but the Sea Sar, where I stayed last time, seemed to be closed at the moment. Our $7 room was fine: fairly spacious and clean, air con that worked unreliably (largely because of the frequent power cuts), no hot water, but two showers in the bathroom.

The plan had been to visit the Golden Rock that afternoon, watch the sunset up there, and then take the last truck back down to Kinpun. But my last visit was in the dry season during peak pilgrimage time, and things are nowhere near as smooth right now. In the rainy season, visiting the Golden Rock comes with a lot of waiting around. Yesterday there was apparently only one truck going up at 6 p.m., and we would have had to spend the night in one of the expensive hotels on the mountain. So we decided to spend a quiet evening in Kinpun instead.

This morning we had to wait for several hours again until the first truck was full enough to set off (1,300k). The half-hour ride up the mountain in tight hairpin bends is very uncomfortable, but in the rainy season it’s also very beautiful. From the terminal it’s then another good half hour on a steep uphill climb to the small pagoda on the balanced golden boulder. Visiting in the rainy season is a completely different experience to during pilgrimage season. Back then we were the only Western tourists too, but the place was swarming with pilgrims, souvenir stalls were open and busy, and there was a fascinating festive atmosphere. Today we were almost the only people up there; it was quiet, the shops were shut, and everything felt a bit lifeless. On the other hand, the climb was far more pleasant than under the merciless blazing sun of the dry season.

But since we didn’t get back to Kinpun until three in the afternoon, we decided to stay another night.

## 6 August 2008
### Moulmein
Yesterday we took the train to Moulmein. It was a spontaneous decision: the pick-up truck from Kinpun dropped us at the station in Kyaikto, and five minutes later the train to Moulmein rolled in.

Train travel in Burma is usually slower than the bus, and also much more expensive, because foreigners have to pay a higher fare (in US dollars); but travelling in Upper Class is probably the most comfortable way to get around the country. You get better views and can actually enjoy the scenery, unlike on the uncomfortable buses. The carriages are easily fifty years old and have clearly never been refurbished, but the seats are still very comfortable, there’s loads of legroom, and the windows are huge. It’s a wonderfully atmospheric, nostalgic way to travel: the train rattles along at a gentle pace on narrow tracks, and some stretches are barely less bumpy than a horse cart. The trip to Moulmein cost $10, took just over four hours (this was the express), and the scenery was excellent. Again and again you pass simple villages of bamboo huts, where ox carts and horse carts wait for customers at tiny stations, and children wave at the passing train. Before Moulmein the train crosses a three-kilometre bridge over the Salween River, and as you approach the new station you get wonderful views of the tropical port city and the pagodas on the hills above it.

Unfortunately our time in Moulmein was more or less washed out: it’s been raining almost non-stop since we arrived.

We stayed at Breeze Guesthouse, set in a pretty colonial villa on Strand Road. It’s the only hotel in town that’s even vaguely geared towards foreign travellers. The small windowless rooms aren’t particularly well kept and at $10 they’re too expensive. The bathroom is perfectly fine and private, but it’s down the hall. There’s also one really beautiful, spacious room with river views, four-poster beds and its own bathroom, but at $20 it was slightly over our budget. On the other hand, if we’d known we’d be spending so much time in the room, it would probably have been worth it.

Moulmein, or Mawlamyine, is a city that even in better times doesn’t see many travellers. South of here is a no-go zone, so in practice you have to go back to Bago or Rangoon to continue travelling. The boat trip from Moulmein to Hpa-An on the Salween is supposed to be very beautiful, but the boat only runs on Mondays and Fridays, and in this weather it would have been pointless anyway.

The city itself is certainly not unattractive, and for the country’s third-largest city it’s remarkably relaxed and undeveloped. The locals are very friendly; old colonial buildings and mosques slowly crumble away; and the setting on the wide Salween, with pagoda-topped hills in the background and a palm-lined riverside promenade, is very appealing. Some truly ugly newer buildings, concrete blocks that already look as rundown as the rest of the city, especially along Strand Road, keep Moulmein from being a colonial jewel, a second Luang Prabang. But still, for its original, authentic atmosphere alone, it’s worth a detour.

In an old dock warehouse by the river, Moulmein also has what may be one of the best restaurants in the country: Mya Thanlwyn. Not only did they have cheap draught beer, lager (500k) and stout (650k), they also served excellent seafood at moderate prices. The butterfish Yunnan-style (2,500k) was outstanding; the Szechuan squid (3,000k) was very good too.

## 11 August 2008
### Back to Rangoon
We took the train back to Rangoon ($17, 9 hours) and checked into Okinawa again for another two nights. We definitely wanted to be in Rangoon on 8 August, because it was the twentieth anniversary of the uprisings and we’d heard from several sources that something special might happen that day. But the junta would have been ready for any demonstrations by the democracy movement with a heavy military presence, and in the end nothing happened at all, not even on this symbolic date. It seems that last September’s bloody crackdown has broken people’s morale for a long time.

We spent a few hours at the Say Teing Kya teashop in the north of the city, with wonderful tea snacks, and bought bus tickets to Taungoo for the next day at the offices by Aung San Stadium. In the evening we took a taxi to Dhamma Zedi Road, one of the smarter areas of Rangoon, with a few mid-range hotels and a cluster of more upmarket cafés and restaurants. We had dinner at Dibar Café, a pleasant Italian trattoria where, for around 5,000k, you can get decent Mediterranean dishes like mussel risotto or paella.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/Burma2008-0037.jpg" alt="Rangoon - Say Teing Kya Teashop">
  <em>Rangoon - Say Teing Kya Teashop</em>
</figure>

The bus ride to Taungoo took about seven hours (6,000k) and by Burmese standards was entirely bearable. Halfway between Rangoon and Mandalay, Taungoo is the ideal stop on the long, gruelling journey north to the main tourist sights. The town has no real attractions, but it’s an authentic, non-touristy place, and it also has one of the most beautiful guesthouses in the country: Myanmar Beauty. The wonderful spacious rooms with verandas and views over rice fields are stylishly furnished, with four-poster beds and heavy wooden furniture. At $20, breakfast is included, and it’s fantastic, and all day you can help yourself to fresh fruit straight from the trees in the orchard. It’s the perfect place to do nothing, read a thick book, and get through three days of almost uninterrupted rain without losing your mind.

## 14 August 2008
### Naypyidaw
Since 2005, Myanmar’s capital is no longer Rangoon but an enigmatic place called Naypyidaw, somewhere out in the middle of nowhere near the small town of Pyinmana. We’d asked plenty of locals whether it was even possible for tourists to visit the new capital, but nobody really knew the rules. Answers ranged from “absolutely not” to “maybe with a guide” to “yes, shouldn’t be a problem.” Which, of course, piqued my curiosity, and we decided to try it on our own.

So in Taungoo we got on the bus to Pyinmana (4 hours, 1,900k). We’d actually planned to spend the night there and do a day trip to Naypyidaw from Pyinmana. At first glance Pyinmana seemed like a pleasant, friendly provincial town. We let rickshaw drivers take us to the only hotel in town, where we got a nasty surprise: foreigners are no longer allowed to stay overnight in Pyinmana. But, we were told, there was a hotel zone in Naypyidaw, 15 kilometres to the west, where we could try our luck.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/Burma2008-0040.jpg" alt="Pyinmana">
  <em>Pyinmana</em>
</figure>

So we took a motorbike taxi to that hotel zone, only to discover it consisted exclusively of four- and five-star hotels for state guests and business people, decidedly not our budget. We wanted to leave as quickly as possible, but at the bus terminal we were told all buses to Mandalay and Taunggyi were already “full” (as we found out the next day, foreigners officially aren’t allowed to leave Naypyidaw by public transport). Since it was already fairly late, we had no choice but to take a room for $80 at Aureum Palace Resort, forget about the budget for once, and simply enjoy the wonderful room and the jacuzzi until midday the next day. We could still worry later about how to get out. It was such a bizarre situation you could only laugh. We were almost certainly the first backpackers ever to stay in this hotel, and probably in Naypyidaw at all, and I felt like I’d wandered onto the wrong set.

We made the most of the room and didn’t check out until noon. Back at the bus terminal, after the buses were “full” again, we were finally told outright that we weren’t allowed to leave the city by bus. Since we didn’t want to spend another night at luxury-hotel prices, and the bus station in Pyinmana seemed deserted, we kept trying until one bus company finally took pity on us and agreed to take us to Taunggyi.

Interestingly, this night bus was not only ridiculously cheap (5,000k), it was also by far the most comfortable form of transport I’d seen in Burma: a super-modern minibus, less than half full, with comfortable seats and distinguished passengers. Anyone who knows this country will understand why the whole situation felt as surreal to us as it did.

After the last two days, though, nothing in this country really surprises me anymore, and Naypyidaw itself is the most bizarre part of the whole experience. There are a few larger government buildings, surprisingly modest, and beyond that the place consists of scattered residential quarters that, though brand new, look like European social housing and seem mostly empty. The whole city is lifeless: hardly any traffic on the wide boulevards, almost never any pedestrians, and no city centre. The liveliest place is the bus terminal, where there’s a market and a few teashops. You’d think the junta would have enough money to build a proper capital from scratch, but perhaps too much cash went into the brand-new four-lane highway connecting Naypyidaw to Rangoon, an utterly insane project, given that hardly anyone but government officials and the military is allowed to use it.

## 17 August 2008
### Inle Lake (Nyaungshwe)
We arrived in Shwenyaung at around five in the morning after a comfortable eight-hour ride and immediately took a taxi on to Nyaungshwe, the tourist centre for Inle Lake. For me this meant the end of unknown territory and a return to familiar ground, except that right now, unlike five years ago, there are hardly any tourists even here, at one of the country’s main destinations. The many guesthouses and restaurants are grateful for every guest, and the range of options has become genuinely wide, with plenty of competition.

We ended up at Aquarius Inn, a nice, friendly guesthouse with rooms that are a little dark but otherwise good, with bathroom and hot shower, for $12. There’s also a really pleasant lounge area and a good selection of books.

## 20 August 2008
### Bagan
Being the hardened travellers that we are, we took the local bus from Shwenyaung to Bagan, one of those epic Burmese bus journeys that are painful and interesting at the same time. An old rattling minibus that carried fifty passengers or more inside and on the roof needed almost twelve hours for this spectacular route. The views on the mostly unpaved road from Kalaw down into the plains towards Meiktila are stunning.

Bagan lies in the driest zone of the country, and for the first time in three weeks we finally had good weather. But in terms of tourism it’s also completely dead at the moment. The restaurants on Nyaung-U’s “Food Street” are happy when a few tables are occupied in the evening, and even the most popular hotels are largely empty.

We stayed at the New Park Hotel, centrally located in Nyaung-U. The motel-style rooms here, clean and modern, with air con and a spacious bathroom, are more than worth their $10. Because I had a painful inflammation in my leg, I couldn’t do the temple tour by bicycle this time. Instead I hired a horse cart for the day: cheap (9,000k), entertaining, and at least it gives the poor people here something to do until the tour groups return.

Today we flew from Bagan back to Rangoon on Yangon Airways, an interesting flight via Mandalay and Heho ($75). I would have liked to stay longer in the north, but with the inflammation making walking difficult, it’s better to spend the last week in Rangoon at my pleasant Okinawa Guesthouse.

## 29 August 2008
### Rangoon
Because my range of movement was limited, I spent most of the last week in Rangoon in my guesthouse. But at least I still had time for a few interesting culinary discoveries, and even went to a nightclub once, which, apart from the Naypyidaw episode, was probably the strangest experience of this trip.

I witnessed a fashion show that consisted of dozens of young women parading various outfits to unspeakably bad dance music. If one of these young ladies particularly appeals to an older Chinese businessman, he can pay a few thousand kyat to have a garland of flowers hung around her neck, after which she comes to his table (and, according to my local companion, later also to his room, for rather more money). This particular Asian form of prostitution wouldn’t surprise me in itself; what’s troubling is that, according to my local companion, every club in the city works on this principle. There don’t seem to be any discos where you can just go dancing.

A far nicer discovery was the rooftop bar at Sakura Tower. A cappuccino (or a beer) costs 2,500k, but you get absolutely magnificent 360-degree views over the entire city, and of course the Shwedagon Pagoda as well. An absolute must on any Rangoon visit, and worth a whole afternoon.

Rangoon’s smartest café, with excellent cappuccino and delicious cakes, is quite a way out of the centre, behind Shwedagon Paya on Zedi Dhamma Road. But the taxi ride to Coffee Circles is worth it for the people-watching alone: Rangoon high society in its natural habitat.

My favourite restaurant, however, was Monsoon, in a colonial building not far from the Strand. You can enjoy excellent cocktails in a wonderful atmosphere and try food from all corners of Southeast Asia. Despite the stylish setting and excellent service, this luxury is very affordable: there’s a daily happy hour from five to seven, when cocktails cost just 1,500k, and main dishes are only 3,000–6,000k.
