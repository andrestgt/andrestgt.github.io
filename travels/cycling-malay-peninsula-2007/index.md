# Cycling the Malay Peninsula

**14 February – 30 March 2007**

A bicycle journey down the Malay Peninsula, from Bangkok through southern Thailand and Malaysia to Singapore, then north again along the west coast.

---

## Days 1–3 · 14–17 Feb · 30 km

The flight with Mahan Air from Düsseldorf via Tehran went smoothly. We took off on time and reached Imam Khomeini Airport after five hours, where we had two hours to stretch our legs; they even handed out meal vouchers. After another five and a half hours in the air we arrived in Bangkok ahead of schedule, at half past five in the morning. Mahan Air offers excellent value: the food and service are decent, and the planes are comfortable.

After all these years, it felt strange not to arrive at good old Don Muang but at the new Suvarnabhumi Airport, which had something slightly claustrophobic and unfinished about it. At least it’s fairly straightforward to get into the centre from here, even if it’s a bit further than from the old airport. First we had to ride 20 km west along On Nut Road — quite relaxed at the start, though the closer we got to the centre, the heavier the traffic became, as expected. Still, if you’ve got good nerves, cycling in Bangkok can be fun, and you often make faster progress than the cars.

On Nut Road eventually runs straight into Sukhumvit, which brought us into the Pratunam area, where we’d booked accommodation this time. Rajthewi Residence had two things going for it. First, I’d never stayed in this part of Bangkok before; second, 950 baht for a 30 m² room including breakfast is very good value. Rajthewi feels a bit like a budget resort: the rooms are somewhat worn, and the air-con is positively archaic. The location, however, is excellent — quiet yet central, a stone’s throw from the Skytrain and Pratunam Market. Either way, it’s a good-value option if you don’t want to stay in Banglamphu.

Once we reached the room, I promptly fell asleep, slept through the entire day, and only woke up when it was time for dinner. We ate at Once Upon a Time, one of Bangkok’s most atmospheric restaurants, just two blocks from our hotel. It’s a minor miracle that those pretty old wooden buildings have survived the high-rise jungle; by candlelight, the ambience is hard to beat. The food isn’t outstanding, but it’s still good: classic Thai cooking at fairly moderate prices. Starters were 100–150 baht, mains 200–300 baht. The salads were very good — beef laab and spicy duck — while the red beef curry was more average, as were the katé leaves with tuna.

My second day in Bangkok was about nothing more than shaking off the jet lag with the usual “how do you spend a relaxed day in Bangkok?” routine: a cappuccino at the Starbucks on Sunset Street, sushi at Taketei, cooling off in the shopping malls around Siam Square and Pratunam, and so on…

## Day 4 · 17 Feb · 111 km — Bangkok to Ayutthaya

We left Bangkok heading north, staying close to the river along Samsen Road. From Nonthaburi we spent a few kilometres on busy Highway 306, then on the little-used six- to eight-lane Highway 347, which somehow felt absurdly oversized. After about 60 kilometres on multi-lane outbound roads, we turned right onto a country road that followed the Chao Phraya via Bang Pa-In to Ayutthaya.

We took a room at Ayodthaya Hotel, centrally located opposite the market, in the cheaper rear annexe for 550 baht. It was a bit dark, but clean and fairly spacious, with air-con and a good hot shower — decent value for money. In the afternoon I rode another 20 kilometres or so between the scattered temple sites of the former royal city, because I remembered surprisingly little from my last visit here many years ago. What is clear, though, is that the redesign of the park between the central temples — with its ponds and bridges — has added a great deal to the town’s appeal. And even if the temple ruins are pretty rather than spectacular, Ayutthaya has a certain picturesque charm that makes a visit well worth it.

## Day 5 · 18 Feb · 153 km — Ayutthaya – Sena – U Thong – Kanchanaburi

We left Ayutthaya heading west on the initially four-lane and not particularly interesting Highway 3263. After about 40 kilometres we reached Highway 340, which we followed for ten kilometres towards Suphanburi before continuing west on the pleasant, lightly trafficked rural roads 3351 and 3318, the first stretch through fairly attractive scenery with plenty of rice fields. After a total of about 70 kilometres we hit a main road again, which took us via U-Thong and Phanom Thuan to Kanchanaburi.

We spent the night at Pong Pehn GH in the tourist quarter on the Kwai. A huge, brand-new and spotless air-con room with an excellent hot shower and a veranda cost 900 baht — not cheap, but worth it. My memories of my last visit to Kanchanaburi are vague, because then, as now, we only used the place as an overnight stop, but I’m pretty sure the riverside area wasn’t yet such a backpacker convenience centre, with odd bars like Ugly Sisters or No Name Bar and its unmissable motto: “Get Shitfaced On A Shoestring”. That said, there are also nice beer gardens and even a shisha café, plus the lovely riverside setting and, at least for now, a very relaxed atmosphere. You could absolutely settle in here for a while, and the countryside west of Kanchanaburi is beautiful anyway.

## Day 6 · 19 Feb · 110 km — Kanburi – Dan Makham Tia – Chom Bung – Ratchaburi

At sunrise we crossed the Mae Klang a few kilometres south of Kanburi and at first rode through pretty scenery, partly along the Kwai, on the pleasant Road 3209. Beyond Dan Makham Tia it became even more remote and rural. We took an unnamed little country lane, many of these in Thailand are paved and in good condition, to the small town of Tha Maka, where we rejoined a main road. We followed it via Chom Bung to Ratchaburi. The last 30 km were gently hilly and busier.

In Ratchaburi we checked straight into Namsi Hotel right in the centre: a large, typical provincial hotel with simple, slightly worn but clean air-con rooms and such pathetic water pressure that I had to resort to the bucket-shower method. Still, fine for 480 baht.

I liked Ratchaburi a lot. Not because the town is beautiful, architecturally, most Thai provincial towns are an eyesore, but with its riverside setting, very friendly locals, a magnificent food market, and the absence of Western tourists, Ratchaburi is an excellent stopover. On top of that, today was the final day of Chinese New Year, and half the town was out celebrating in Chinatown, which had been turned into a pedestrian zone, complete with countless food stalls and stage entertainment. All the lanterns, the delicious-smelling smoke from the barbecues, and the hand-pulled rickshaws weaving through the crowds made for a wonderfully atmospheric scene.

## Day 7 · 20 Feb · 137 km — Ratchaburi – Phetchaburi – Cha-Am – Hua Hin

In the morning, for lack of any obvious alternative, we spent sixty kilometres on the heavily trafficked Highway 4 to Phetchaburi. It wasn’t exactly a pleasure ride, with all the roaring buses and trucks, but on the wide shoulder we made quick progress and were largely left alone. Phetchaburi is another fairly pleasant provincial town, with the Phetchaburi River running through it and a very photogenic limestone hill covered in temples west of the centre. There’s a guesthouse, Rabiang, in a lovely traditional wooden house by the river, and in its atmospheric restaurant I had an excellent banana-blossom salad with seafood and chicken for lunch (60 baht). A perfect lunch stop.

Less pleasant was the rest of the route to Hua Hin. Fighting a strong headwind, we first went about 15 km along Road 3177 to the sea, where a monotonous, shade-free and not particularly attractive road ran down the coast to Cha-Am. The stiff breeze and relentless sun really made for a rough day of riding. Cha-Am is one of the country’s most popular resorts with Thai tourists, with countless hotels and restaurants spread over many kilometres, but the beach isn’t especially appealing and is completely covered in sun loungers. Only the quieter southern part is rather pretty, but it doesn’t exactly put you in a tropical-holiday mood.

  <img src="photos/CyclingMalay2007-0015.jpg" alt="Cha-Am">

For the final 25 km on the four-lane coastal highway to Hua Hin, it was a case of grit your teeth and get on with it — no shade, very few places to buy food, no nice views; at least there was barely any traffic and the road surface was excellent.

In Hua Hin it was still high season, and there seemed to be more farangs, especially pensioners and families, than Thais. On both the beautiful beach and the high-quality night market you can hardly move for tourists; the many pubs and restaurants are all packed, and room rates reflect that. Even so, we found a room for a reasonable 750 baht at Subhamitra Hotel. Aesthetically it did nothing for me, very institutional, populated by the same clientele as the budget hotels on Bulgaria’s Golden Beach, but the functional room was perfectly fine for the price, and there was even a pool. More important, though, was the location: just a five-minute walk from the night market, Hua Hin’s highlight. Yes, it’s very touristy and pricier than night markets elsewhere in the country, but the range of high-quality seafood is unique: huge langoustines, king prawns, scallops — everything you could wish for, all fresh from the grill.

## Day 8 · 21 Feb · 122 km — Hua Hin – Khao Sam Roi Yod NP – Prachuap Khiri Khan

It was roughly 20 gently hilly kilometres south before, just before Pranburi, a country road branched off left towards the coast and Khao Sam Roi Yod National Park, rejoining Highway 4 after about 50 km. Ironically, this detour on excellent, almost traffic-free back roads was at its most scenic before you even reached the park entrance: imposing limestone formations rise behind the coast, and the road passes through lush tropical vegetation. Inside the park itself, the road runs flat and shade-free through abandoned shrimp farms and was far less appealing.

The entrance fee for foreigners is a completely inflated 400 baht, which I avoided by simply turning left before the checkpoint onto a small laterite track that rejoined the main road 2 km further on. A nice, short detour (800 m) inside the national park led to Sam Phraya Beach, shallow and unspectacular, yet quite picturesque, and I had it entirely to myself at lunchtime.

Back on Highway 4 it was another 40 km or so to the provincial capital, Prachuap Khiri Khan. Parts of this stretch were a little hilly, but it was quite pleasant riding, because the hard shoulder was often in the shade. Prachuap is an exceptionally friendly, laid-back town on a very picturesque bay framed by limestone outcrops, with colourful fishing boats bobbing offshore. To my mind, that makes Prachuap one of the most enjoyable and worthwhile Thai provincial towns, and it’s hardly surprising that it seems to have become something of a well-kept secret among farangs as well, mostly of the older variety.

Most of them stay, as we did, at Had Thong, the large two-star place on the promenade, the prime spot in town, with very decent, simple mid-range rooms at sensible prices. The cheapest room without a sea view cost us 675 baht and was good value. Along the waterfront road there are a handful of seafood restaurants with lovely views and English menus. We had a good, inexpensive dinner at Krithong: three grilled squid, prawn sashimi, and a mussel salad for 380 baht. The dipping sauces were tasty, too.

## Day 9 · 22 Feb · 104 km — Prachuap – Ban Krut – Bang Saphan

We left Prachuap heading south through military grounds, passed the very pretty Ao Manao bay, and after about 15 km of country road we were back on Highway 4. For roughly 35 km there was no alternative to the main north–south route, but this gently hilly section was quite pleasant because of a shaded hard shoulder. At kilometre 373 we turned off onto a quiet country road to the coast, and after about 15 km we reached Ban Krut, a fishing village where the most beautiful, and most pleasant to ride, section of the tour so far began.

A laterite lane lined with coconut palms ran for several kilometres along the stunning white-sand beach of Ban Krut Bay, until crossing a lagoon which we could follow for a few more kilometres. At the end of the lagoon we continued on a tarmac road along an empty, wide beach almost all the way to Bang Saphan, a small town a few kilometres inland. The idyllic bay of Ban Krut is, understandably, very popular with Thai weekend visitors. The resulting tourist infrastructure, with a few lovely resorts, has now started to attract a small number of adventurous farangs as well (again mostly pensioners and families), for whom the classic tourist hotspots are presumably too loud and too commercial. During the week you have Ban Krut’s miles of postcard-perfect beach practically to yourself.

From Bang Saphan we rode about five kilometres south on Road 3374 and stopped at Suan Luang Resort on the spur road to the beach of the same name. A very pretty, friendly place, with small but spotless, well-equipped bungalows with air-con, satellite TV, fridge and hot shower. At 600 baht it was a very good choice, especially because the resort’s Franco-Thai restaurant had an excellent menu, with properly spicy Thai dishes at fair prices. The seafood curry (90 baht) was superb, and the fish carpaccio “Tahiti Style” (120 baht) was just as good.

## Day 10 · 23 Feb · 140 km — Bang Saphan – Ban Bird – Pathiu – Chumphon

A fairly hilly day, entirely on back roads near the coast, heading south. Signposting here was often poor to non-existent, but the roads were mostly good and pleasant to ride. The most beautiful stretch was the section right along the coast around Ban Bird Bay. The beach, which runs for miles, is a favourite with Thai tourists; unfortunately, compared with Ban Krut Beach, the otherwise lovely and quite picturesque shore here looks a bit neglected.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingMalay2007-0028.jpg" alt="Ban Bird">

Further south towards Chumphon, the roads mostly ran quite a long way inland through gently rolling countryside. The stretch between Amarit and Pathiu was very enjoyable and led through shaded woodland. We didn’t take a room in Chumphon town centre, but at Tanyung Pier about ten kilometres to the east, where the ferries to Ko Tao depart. There’s a simple hotel here with very cheap but perfectly adequate air-con rooms for 350 baht. A few hundred metres upriver there’s a wonderful seafood restaurant where you can eat lying down in private Thai pavilions on the riverbank. For such an atmospheric place, the prices were quite moderate: a whole medium-sized fish of excellent quality cost 250 baht; cashew nuts with seafood 90 baht.

Freshly showered and rested, we decided to take the midnight ferry to Ko Tao (200 baht) and from there continue to Ko Samui the following morning. After my positive experience with the Surat–Phangan night ferry, very spacious, with mattresses laid out, I was surprised that on this night ferry there were only woven mats and barely enough space to lie down, as an unexpectedly large number of farang travellers had to share the limited deck space. Even so, I slept reasonably well under the circumstances, and when we arrived on Ko Tao at six in the morning I felt rested enough.

## Days 11–12 · 24–25 Feb · 109 km — Ko Tao – Ko Samui

Since the catamaran onward to Ko Samui didn’t leave until 9.30 a.m., we set off as soon as it was light to explore Ko Tao a little. With only a few kilometres of paved roads and lots of brutally steep sections, the small island isn’t exactly ideal for cycling, and the beaches can’t really compete with Ko Phangan either. The whole west coast, including the hinterland, is already heavily built up. Ko Tao is simply too small, its beaches too narrow, to cope with the large tourist crowds. With the larger neighbouring islands of Phangan and Samui nearby, if you’re not here for diving or snorkelling there’s really no reason to spend your holiday on Ko Tao. That’s not to say the island isn’t beautiful. You can still shoot glossy-magazine tropical clichés by the bucketload, and especially at Sairee Beach there are some impressively well-designed cafés, bars and restaurants where the atmosphere in the evening must be wonderful. It’s just that all of this exists on Ko Phangan too but the beaches are simply better, and the coastline less built up.

The catamaran via Thong Sala took less than two hours. It didn’t go to the island capital, Nathon, but to Mae Nam Beach on the north coast, where we rented a big new bungalow for 400 baht, less than a two-minute walk from a really nice stretch of sand. I was honestly surprised that you can get accommodation that good for so little money on Samui , with air-con, a fridge and even a gas hob. This part of the beach by the pier, where the boats to Hat Rin and Thong Nai Pan leave, was very pleasant: fairly quiet, but with everything you need for a beach holiday all the same: seafood restaurants on the sand, bars, internet cafés, 7-Eleven. Achim will spend the rest of the time here, and I’ll continue on my own to Singapore and then come back to Samui again — or at least that’s the plan. For dinner, after a small afternoon ride in the north-east of the island between Big Buddha Beach and Bophut, we ate at My Friend Restaurant, a simple beach place where you could sit comfortably and get fresh seafood at reasonable prices.

I stayed another day on Samui and we did an island circuit on the 50 km ring road. Cycling on Samui, despite the traffic, the so-so road surface and a few passes, isn’t as bad as it sounds. Large sections are fairly flat, and the island is beautiful anyway, mass tourism or not. There are still no high-rise hotels, condo blocks or similar eyesores. The stretch between the island’s best beach, Lamai, and the famous, but over-commercialised, Chaweng Beach is magnificent. It’s the hilliest part of the ring road, but viewpoints with spectacular panoramas over picture-perfect tropical scenery make up for the effort.

The northern beaches, Bophut and Mae Nam, are also very picturesque, and development still feels just about tolerable. What did surprise me, though, is that for such a famous, established "dream holiday destination" Samui often looks oddly unfinished. Bad roads and building sites in prime locations weren’t what I’d expected. Even so, apart from Chaweng, Samui still isn’t quite as over-commercialised and spoilt, and above all not as expensive, as to be avoided. Personally, though (without a bicycle), I definitely prefer Ko Phangan.

In the evening we had a seafood barbecue in wonderful surroundings, with tables on the sand at the restaurant by the pier just round the corner from us. Four enormous tiger prawns cost a whopping 675 baht (salad and baked potatoes were included), but they tasted fantastic. Grilled shark steak and octopus, by contrast, were much cheaper.

## Day 13 · 26 Feb · 137 km — Ko Samui – Donsak – Khanom – Tha Sala

In the morning I cycled the short 13 km to Nathon and took the big 8 a.m. car ferry (140 baht), which brought me back to the mainland at Donsak in 90 minutes. From the pier it was about 25 km through beautiful karst scenery on the slightly hilly but shady and lightly trafficked 4142 to Khanom. The coast south of the small town is absolutely stunning. In essence it looks like the most beautiful corners of Samui: white sandy beaches lined with coconut palms, small photogenic bays dotted with rocks, and a hilly, jungle-covered hinterland. The concrete lane between the idyllic coves of Nai Plao and Thongyu offers, at times, fantastic views, but the gradients are no joke. This wonderful little corner of the world is just beginning to be developed for tourism. There are already a few resorts that look quite smart, and a handful of farangs are holidaying here, but overall the area is still a real hidden gem. Unfortunately, the continuous coastal road between Khanom and Sichon shown on some maps doesn’t exist. At Thongyu Bay, that was the end of the line, so I had to head back to Khanom and then return to the main road. I didn’t regret this involuntary detour in the slightest, though. It wasn’t only the beauty of the coast that impressed me, I even had a picture-perfect tropical bay entirely to myself for my lunchtime swim.

Cutting through the interior, I reached Highway 401 after about 15 km, the road linking Surat Thani with Nakhon Si Thammarat. It’s a very pleasant road to ride: good surface, mostly flat, not too much traffic, and a wide shoulder.


I’d planned to stay overnight in Tha Sala, but there didn’t seem to be a hotel in town. So I continued a few kilometres south. Here Highway 401 meets the coast for the first time and runs almost right along the beach. Just under 10 km beyond Tha Sala, after the 401 had moved a few hundred metres inland again, there were a few simple bungalow places on a very unappealing, muddy beach; though judging by the countless picnic pavilions it must be hugely popular at weekends. For 200 baht I got a solid, very spacious bungalow with a squat toilet and cockroaches in the bathroom; otherwise it was fairly clean. For one night, something like this does the job just fine.

## Day 14 · 27 Feb · 143 km — Tha Sala – Nakhon Si Thammarat – Phatthalung

## Day 18 · 3 Mar · 74 km — Narathiwat – Kota Baru

From Narathiwat it was a relaxed 40 km down the coast to the Malaysian border. A small car ferry crosses the river here, and the immigration formalities were dealt with quickly and without fuss. The region’s biggest city, Kota Baru, lies only 25 km from the border and was easy to reach thanks to good signposting.

Funnily enough, this supposedly very traditionally Muslim area looks more Thai than the countryside north of the border, because there are a few pretty Buddhist temple complexes along the roadside. Otherwise there isn’t much difference from southern Thailand, and the price level is similar too.

Kota Baru is the first place since Samui where you also run into a few Western tourists again, and there are plenty of guesthouses. The pleasantly unhurried city doesn’t have a huge amount to offer for an extended stay, but there’s authentic Malay urban life to watch, good food at the night market, and in places even some fairly attractive architecture amid countless eyesores, and even more building sites.

## Day 20 · 5 Mar · 123 km — Perhentian – Kuala Terengganu – Pulau Kapas

## Day 23 · 8 Mar · 162 km — Pekan – Mersing

## Day 24 · 9 Mar · 145 km — Mersing – Johor Baru

JB isn’t Georgetown, but there are still surprisingly many well-preserved rows of old Chinese shophouses. Most of them have had a fresh coat of paint recently and they really make the city look attractive.

But back to food, the main reason to come to Singapore at all. Eating is a central part of the culture here, and for variety and quality the city isn’t world-famous for nothing.

I started with Yum Cha, a classic Chinese dim sum place right in the heart of Chinatown. Huge choice, and both quality and prices were great. It’s one of the best-known restaurants of its kind and has won multiple awards, so it’s certainly not the cheapest dim sum in Singapore, but with prices between 1.80 and 4.00 S$ per basket, it’s still far cheaper than Europe. Which is something you can say about eating in Singapore in general: alcohol is relatively expensive (even with happy hours, at least at Western European levels), but you can eat surprisingly cheaply even in good restaurants, and in the many excellent food courts, prices are only a little above the usual South-East Asian standard. For under five euros you can stuff yourself silly with Indian, Chinese, Malay and Japanese dishes.

## Day 26 · 12 Mar · 186 km — Singapore – JB – Batu Pahat

I made up for what I’d missed the day before and had a tasty dumpling soup for breakfast at Lau Pa Sat before leaving the city and heading back to Malaysia via the Second Link. At least, that was the idea. At the border I was politely but firmly told that the regulations explicitly forbid bicycles from using this crossing. I lodged an equally polite official complaint about the lack of warning signs, but still had to ride back to the Causeway and cross via JB, so I’d already done 60 km before my tyres touched Malaysian soil again. At least the border was quiet this time.

Highway 1 northbound isn’t pleasant: hilly, heavy traffic, usually no proper shoulder, and in places in very poor condition. After nearly 95 km the misery finally ended and at Air Hitam I turned onto the far better Highway 5 towards the coast. That way I still made it to Batu Pahat before sunset, where there’s a rest house, though not one of Malaysia’s better ones. The room (52.50 RM) was fairly spacious, with a big bed and air-con, but a bit neglected, and the shower was a mere trickle, so I had to fall back on the tried-and-tested bucket method.

The food in the attached Chinese restaurant was much better. I had meltingly tender beef on a hot plate with fresh chillies for 8.50 ringgit; the steamboat looked very tempting too, but solo it isn’t really worth it.

## Day 27 · 13 Mar · 101 km — BP – Muar – Melaka

Although Highway 5 runs close to the coast all the way, you never actually see the sea on today’s route. This road is much more pleasant to ride than Highway 1, but you do have to admit: road conditions in Thailand are noticeably better than here in Malaysia. A wide shoulder exists only sporadically, and all the unevenness along the edge makes riding harder.

Even so, today’s stage was quite enjoyable: mostly flat, with lots of fruit and drink vendors along the road. The only larger town before Melaka, Muar, was also rather pretty and laid-back.

In Melaka I managed to get the last room at the deservedly popular Sama Sama Guesthouse, a traveller hangout ideally located in the middle of Chinatown. This is what a budget guesthouse should be like: a large, clean room with mosquito net and a hammock outside the door (25 RM), proper water pressure in the showers, and a relaxed, friendly atmosphere.

## Day 28 · 14 Mar · 145 km — Melaka – Selangor – Kuala Lumpur

Highway 19, the feeder road to Highway 1, had been resurfaced for nearly 40 km and was easy to ride thanks to a broad shoulder. The entire day’s route went through hilly inland terrain; the only properly "mountainous” bit didn’t come until after Selangor, where I had to cross a small pass. Highway 1 was, in parts, unpleasant again with heavy traffic, roadworks, a bumpy surface, and no shoulder, though the landscape was quite pretty.

## Day 29 · 15 Mar — Kuala Lumpur

Today, for the first time, I left the bike completely alone: KL is relatively compact, and it’s anything but bike-friendly, with heavy traffic and hills.

You can explore a lot of the interesting neighbourhoods on foot, and if a multi-lane expressway blocks the way there’s still the metro and the monorail to get you where you need to go quickly and cheaply.

Since my last visit to KL was more than ten years ago, which in fast-changing Asian cities is basically an eternity, it felt like entering new territory. I first walked to the impressive Petronas Towers, then strolled at a leisurely pace through Kampung Baru, a still fairly traditional, cosy Malay neighbourhood where you can have a very cheap breakfast and get lovely skyline views. Around midday I took a longer walk through Little India to Merdeka Square, the centre of the old colonial quarter, lined with many architecturally beautiful buildings. Cross the river and you’re in Chinatown with its markets and old shophouses; the distances here are quite tolerable for a city of this size.

In the afternoon I went up to the observation deck of the KL Tower, one of the tallest buildings in the world (though not the tallest in KL), with a 360° view over the city and the surrounding area. It isn’t cheap, but it’s worth every ringgit. The tower sits on a wooded hill, and on the way down I took the less obvious route: a path right through dense jungle. Being able to walk alone on slippery trails through jungle right in the city centre is something only a handful of cities on earth can offer. A definite highlight.

Otherwise, it feels as though KL is doing everything it can to step out of the shadow of the region’s more famous metropolises, where it has traditionally played second fiddle, even in tourism. Everything in KL seems oversized: some of the biggest shopping malls in the world, some of the tallest buildings in the world, and a gastronomic diversity that is on a par with any Asian metropolis. Maybe that’s necessary, KL doesn’t have Hong Kong’s spectacular setting, Bangkok’s grand temples, or Singapore’s harmonious cityscape. Unlike those places, KL still lacks that sense of a single, coherent whole, the kind of cohesive flair that truly great cities have. Either way, KL is absolutely worth a visit, especially since it’s still much cheaper than Hong Kong and Singapore.

Food-wise, as mentioned, the city is world-class. For lunch I had Hokkien mee and very cheap sushi in the food court at Lot 10 in the Golden Triangle. They served excellent, thick, juicy gourmet burgers in every imaginable variation, with fresh ingredients in crisp buns, for around 20 ringgit. ## Day 30 · 16 Mar · 107 km — KL – Kuala Selangor

I decided to spend the morning in KL and only ride back to the coast after lunch, because the previous day I hadn’t managed to visit the city’s two big parks outside the centre, Lake Gardens and Titiwangsa. That morning confirmed I’d been right to leave the bike alone the day before, because cycling in KL is a complete nightmare. The heavy traffic isn’t even the worst of it; it’s the countless one-way streets and expressways with nowhere to turn off that drive you to despair. My little outing to the two parks, really not that far from the centre, ended up being almost 40 km. In the parks themselves, though, you can cycle quite pleasantly, and the Lake Gardens in particular are a green oasis in an otherwise frantic city.

## Day 31 · 17 Mar · 150 km — Kuala Selangor – Lumut

I didn’t get going until about ten, because it had been raining cats and dogs all morning. For the first 130 km I stuck to Highway 5 which was a flat and, apart from a stretch of about 20 km in the middle, a fairly smooth, wide road, though the scenery was uninspiring.

On excellent, brand-new roads I then rolled into Lumut, the ferry terminal for boats to Pangkor. If it hadn’t been Saturday, I’d have crossed over today, but I figured it would be more relaxed (and cheaper) to stay on the mainland, because Pangkor is always packed at weekends.

Lumut is quite a nice place for an overnight stop: pleasant, with a well-kept, modern riverside promenade, and a decent backpacker guesthouse, Era, where a clean, brightly painted and fairly spacious room with a fan and shared bathroom cost 30 ringgit.

## Day 32 · 18 Mar · 119 km — Lumut – Pangkor – Lumut – Taiping

Even though it was Sunday and the island is far from an untouched paradise at weekends, I decided to spend the morning on Pangkor and do a full lap of the island. Ferries ran regularly every half hour; the crossing took just 40 minutes (10 RM return), and taking the bike on board was easy and free of charge.

The paved ring road on Pangkor is only 20 km long, but in places, especially in the north-east, it’s very steep indeed. That said, it’s also the prettiest stretch, where the quiet little road winds through dense jungle. Along the east coast it’s village after village, with houses perched on stilts over the water. There aren’t any beaches here worth writing home about, but the kampung atmosphere feels fairly authentic.

## Day 35 · 21 Mar · 131 km — Georgetown – Butterworth – Kuala Kedah – Langkawi

The ferry back to Butterworth was free. From there it was around 70 km north on Highway 1 towards Alor Setar, mostly flat, pleasant to ride, with a wide shoulder, before I turned left and covered the remaining 30 km to Kuala Kedah on quiet country roads near the coast. This is where the ferries to Langkawi depart.

The crossing on fairly modern, air-conditioned ferries took 90 minutes and cost 18 ringgit (plus another 5 ringgit for the bicycle).

I arrived at the ferry terminal in Kuah at five o’clock and at first felt like I’d walked onto the wrong set. The terminal here is basically a shopping mall which, given the island’s duty-free status, shouldn’t be surprising, but if you remember Kuah as the small sleepy place it used to be, it still comes as a shock, even though my last visit was more than ten years ago.

The centre of Kuah was hardly recognisable either. I vaguely remembered eating at street stalls along the coastal road. That same road no longer runs along the coast at all, thanks to land-reclamation projects; entire new districts have emerged, with countless malls and duty-free shops.

Since the town still has very little else to offer, I cycled the roughly 20 km along the quiet, hilly south-coast road to Langkawi’s main beaches, Tengah and Cenang, separated only by a rocky headland and a duty-free shopping complex.

Tengah is the prettier and quieter of the two: just a handful of nice beach restaurants and a few mid-range resorts, mostly popular with Europeans. The beach is gorgeous, good for swimming, and very clean.

## Day 36 · 22 Mar · 98 km — Langkawi – Satun

I’d originally planned to spend another night on Langkawi, but when I heard there was also an afternoon ferry to Thailand, I changed my plans. That way I still had enough time for a loop ride around the island and could continue to Satun the same day — where it’s far cheaper to stay than in Kuah.

Even though Langkawi is very mountainous, it’s brilliant for cycling. Most roads are only gently hilly (often almost flat for kilometres), in excellent condition and with little traffic. Despite Kuah’s shopping malls, most of the island, including the villages, still feels fairly unspoilt and is very scenic. Riding through the jungle you’re accompanied by brightly coloured birds, and along the roadside monkeys do monkey things. Tourism outside Cenang is largely limited to a few mid-range and luxury resorts, most of which sit quite harmoniously in the landscape.

## Day 38 · 24 Mar · 111 km — Pakbara – La Ngu – Trang

I took the 9 o’clock ferry back to the mainland and rode again to La Ngu on Highway 416. Even though it’s the main connection between Trang and Satun, it feels more like a pleasant, quiet country road with little traffic. For almost 50 km it runs through some very pretty karst-hill scenery, with a small pass to climb as well.

The remaining stretch into Trang on Highway 404 was less interesting and only gently rolling, but it was absolutely hammering down anyway, so I just wanted to reach the provincial capital as quickly as possible.

Trang is very tidy but not particularly pretty. Still, quite a lot of tourists spend the night here because the surrounding area — with its many offshore islands — has plenty to offer. Most of them, for good reason, stay at Koh Teng, an old, simple Chinese hotel that calls itself “Five Star Backpackers” and has been renting rooms since 1948. And even if the building and corridors still look very 1948, the rooms offer excellent value: spacious, clean, with a relatively newly tiled bathroom and a proper toilet — 180 baht is a bargain.

## Day 39 · 25 Mar · 160 km — Trang – Sikao – Krabi – Ao Nang

The whole region is quite hilly, but the climbs are generally not very long or steep. Given the once again poor weather, I decided against a detour to Ko Lanta and rode straight through to Krabi instead; a wise decision, because it started raining again around midday. ## Day 41 · 27 Mar · 123 km — Phuket island loop

I spent today doing a loop around the island, though naturally the main focus was the west-coast road with its glorious beaches and views.

I first cycled back north on Highway 402 and chose Nai Yang Beach near the airport as my starting point. We’d stayed here many years ago at Pearl Village, but today I found the beach comparatively unappealing. This was also the only place on Phuket where you could even begin to sense that a natural disaster had struck two years ago; elsewhere there were absolutely no traces left.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingMalay2007-0194.jpg" alt="Phuket - West Coast - Nai Yang Beach">
  <em>Phuket - West Coast - Nai Yang Beach</em>
</figure>

Across the island, especially away from the beaches, there’s an enormous building boom, almost exclusively luxury apartment resorts, and some places may well be unrecognisable in a year or two.

The west-coast road isn’t really cycling terrain. There are countless gradients well over 20% where even mopeds struggle. After Nai Yang you go over a short, steep pass to Nai Thon, a very pretty bay that’s still relatively quiet. From there the road climbs properly into the mountains, kilometres of steep ascent, before winding back down into flatter terrain. Back on the coast you hit the island’s most touristy strip: Surin, Kamala, Patong, Karon… all picture-perfect beaches, but so overdeveloped you can barely see the sand for sunloungers.

Scenically, the mountainous road down the coast is spectacular; again and again you get postcard views, because besides the big main beaches there are also countless smaller coves often with nothing more than a discreet luxury resort. These are the only places on Phuket where you can still have a halfway relaxed high-season beach holiday with a bit of privacy. Which, naturally, costs serious money.

In Patong I wouldn’t spend my holidays even if you paid me. This huge place is a resort nightmare that makes even some of the Mediterranean coast’s worst excesses look modest. It’s not that there are endless high-rise blocks; it’s the sheer concentration of cheap package-tourism culture that made me flee.

Karon, further south, has a very wide, long, white beach and felt almost cosy by comparison.

The southern end of the mass-tourism zone is Kata: a coconut-fringed double bay that probably ranks among the most beautiful in the world (something like Ao Thong Nai Pan in XL format). It’s much quieter here than Patong too, but unfortunately a big chunk of the beach is Club Med territory. Not my idea of a good time!

After a glorious descent I ended my beach run in the far south at the stunning Nai Harn Bay, where I finally went for a swim as well. Given how extraordinarily beautiful it is, Nai Harn felt, by Phuket standards, still fairly undeveloped and relaxed, so it’s probably my favourite beach on the island.

Conclusion: there can’t be many more spectacular coastal roads in the world than the one that runs down Phuket’s west coast. For a proper beach holiday, though, the main beaches are unsuitable thanks to total over-commercialisation, and the alternatives cost a fortune. In other words: Phuket is absolutely worth a visit; I just wouldn’t come here for a holiday.

Dinner was excellent again. I went to Dibuk, in a restored Sino-Portuguese old-town house on the street of the same name that had caught my eye yesterday. It’s an upmarket but by no means pretentious Franco-Thai restaurant where you can dine in a wonderful atmosphere with wicker chairs and candlelight. Prices are fairly moderate for Phuket, and both the moules sautées à l’ail with French baguette (95 baht) and the Thai yam nǔa (100 baht) were outstanding. I’d almost say it was the best yam nǔa I’ve ever had: spicy, with generous coriander, and wonderfully tender strips of pink beef.

## Day 42 · 28 Mar — Ko Phi Phi day trip

I’d sworn never to visit Phi Phi again in high season, but my curiosity about what the island looks like after the tsunami was simply too strong. So I bought an open return ticket (750 baht) and took the big ferry at 8:30, which took about 90 minutes. I wanted to keep my options open — either stay overnight or return to Phuket in the afternoon — but given the room prices the decision was easy.

Right now is not a good time to visit Phi Phi. Prices are as high as ever and tourist numbers are back to (high) normal, but the island currently resembles a building site — hardly conducive to a holiday mood. Not a tragedy; I’ve been here often enough. This trip really was just about satisfying my curiosity.

At first I was surprised. The front part of the village looked exactly as it had on my last visit two and a half years ago: the same restaurants, dive shops, cafés… Carlito’s, Hippies, HC Andersen, the Rolling Stoned… Petcharee Bakery still does an excellent pad thai goong for 70 baht… so everything unchanged, then? Not at all.

It was predictable that the rebuilding wouldn’t prioritise nature and atmosphere but economic interests. Still, I hadn’t expected it to be quite this extreme. On the land strip between the two gorgeous bays — where there used to be coconut palms, wooden huts, stinking drains and corrugated iron hut slums — new buildings are going up everywhere, some of them multi-storey, and they’ll fundamentally change the face of the place. Phi Phi had already been on the brink of tourist collapse before the tsunami; I really wonder how the island is supposed to cope with the new apartments and resorts and the further increases in bed capacity that come with them.

With those thoughts I climbed up to the viewpoint, where the full scale of the disaster only really hits you. More than half the land bridge is one big construction site. Where huts once stood in coconut groves, multi-storey shop rows with air-conditioned rooms are now being built. There’s already a 7-Eleven too — only Starbucks is missing.

Phi Phi Don is still one of the most beautiful islands in the world, one of those places you simply have to see — but if I ever spend a few days here again, it’ll be in September or October, and only once the construction sites are gone.

I took the afternoon ferry back to Phuket and, thanks to an engine problem, didn’t arrive until around half past five. Since I also needed to have my inner tube patched, I decided to spend another night in Phuket Town and took a room at On On Hotel, where I’d booked my tickets and left the bike.

The On On, with its majestic but crumbling colonial façade, is one of the most famous budget hotels in the world since it was used as a filming location for The Beach. A real old-school hotel, with dark corridors and rooms (250 baht) that are little more than wooden cubicles, though not small, and not grubby either, even if the bathroom has clearly seen better days. I love the vibe of places like this. You’re meant to lie naked under the fan with a cold bottle of beer and read Dostoevsky (or something similar). And that’s exactly what I did until dinner, when I treated myself to something a bit fancier again.

Khana Sutra isn’t your standard Indian place but a chic, stylish lounge where you can lounge on cushions and eat at low tables. Exactly my thing. Since the quality of food was more than convincing, I didn’t mind that the bill was a bit higher, the value still felt right. I had three chunky samosas (88 baht) as a starter, exactly as they should be: crisp outside, juicy inside, served with a fine dip. For the main, a fiercely hot vindaloo with a generous number of king prawns (240 baht), plus crisp garlic naan (66 baht). They also did a good mango lassi (100 baht).

For the 40 km back to the bridge I took the relatively flat Highway 402 again. At the northern end of Phuket, just before the bridge, there are a few simple seafood places right on a nice beach, where I had a small breakfast: squid stir-fried with cashews (80 baht) — very tasty.

Immediately past the cape, the town’s mid-range and luxury resorts sit between road and beach; most shops, restaurants and simpler accommodation are a kilometre further along the highway.

I found a very nice, modern, large room for an extremely reasonable 300 baht at Sri Guesthouse on the inland side of the road. With veranda, good bathroom and a king-size bed it was the best value of the entire trip — though being far from the beach wasn’t ideal.

In the afternoon I cycled around the nearby area a bit. Khao Lak, where the tsunami hit worst, and where you can still see scars in the form of patches of barren land, is an unspectacular but very pleasant holiday town, popular mainly with families and slightly more independent package tourists. The setting below jungle-clad hills is as attractive as the long golden beaches, which are perfect for extended walks. There are no noisy water-sports activities, no rows of deckchairs on the sand, and no giant holiday complexes. Everything feels relaxed and low-key, and prices are comparatively reasonable: even at Mukdara Resort’s smart beach bar, a draught beer cost just 50 baht. The only drawback is that Highway 4 runs right through the place, which doesn’t exactly scream “summer, palms, sunshine” once you step away from the beachfront resorts.

I had dinner at Khao Lak Seafood Restaurant, also on the highway. There was a thick menu of the usual suspects at fair prices, but also a few dishes you don’t see quite so often — like the delicious barracuda fillet steamed in banana leaf (100 baht), a real hit. The “Yam Three Musketeers” with prawns, fish and octopus (80 baht) wasn’t bad either, but it had been toned down too much for tourist palates.

## Day 44 · 30 Mar · 197 km — Khao Lak – Khao Sok – Surat Thani

A grand final stage: straight across the peninsula back to the Gulf coast on the consistently hilly Highway 401. After Takua Pa the road first undulated steadily, overall tending uphill, through a broad, scenic valley, before a long, steep climb up to a pass began after about 30 km.

After the descent from the pass, Khao Sok National Park lies to the left, where I stopped for lunch. Near the headquarters you can have a lovely swim in the river, and along the access road there are a few pleasant restaurants with pretty views. Either way, it’s a very beautiful area.
