# Cycling the Baltic
**24 July – 4 September 2004**

A bicycle loop through Finland, Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania, from Tampere to the Baltic coast and back.

---


## Day 1 — 24 July 2004 — 64 km

### Tampere Airport – Lempälää – Toijala – Iitala

Flight with Ryanair from Hahn to Tampere (€22 + €25 for the bike).
Arrival at 18:00.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0001.jpg" alt="Tampere Airport">
  <em>Tampere Airport</em>
</figure>

We rode through the gently rolling foothills of the Finnish Lakeland on
quiet roads, often with a cycle path alongside. There were virtually no
places to stop, and the small towns that cropped up now and then along
the way were not exactly enticing. Still: clean air, and nature, at
least.

We spent the night for free outside Iitala in a simple wooden hut that
is actually used as a changing cabin for a lakeside bathing spot.

## Day 2 — 25 July 2004 — 139 km

### Iitala – Häämenlinna – Helsinki

Today’s ride was quiet and hilly again. We stayed on road 130 until just
before Helsinki and, although it’s a main road, traffic was very light.
Apart from petrol-station cafeterias there were no places to stop, but
there were once again plenty of cycle paths running alongside the road.

## Day 3 — 26 July 2004 — 35 km

### Helsinki (cycling around the city)

After a generous Scandinavian breakfast at the hotel, we set off to
explore Helsinki by bike. We picked up a free cycling map at the tourist
office and followed both the coastal route and the city route.

Our first stop was the harbour market, where fishing boats were selling
their catch and, alongside the fruit stalls, there were plenty of food
stands. We then rode down the coast to Kaivopuisto Park and had the best
cappuccino in town at lovely Café Ursula, on a terrace overlooking the
little offshore islands.

### Tallinn – Palmse – Võsu

In a fine drizzle, which was to last for hours, we left Tallinn on
signposted cycle route 4 along the coast towards Lahemaa National Park.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0032.jpg" alt="Around Tallinn">

After about 50 kilometres on lonely but mostly decent roads, we reached
the park.

Inside the national park the terrain became a bit hillier, and there
were a few unpaved stretches. Because of the awful weather, we couldn’t
really enjoy the park’s attractions, Baltic coastal scenery with small
beaches, Ice Age boulders, and dense green fairytale forest.

## Day 6 — 29 July 2004 — 55 km

### Võsu – Rakvere

We rode in the rain again today, still on long-distance cycle route 4,
to the small town of Rakvere. Because of the weather we decided to
settle in already in the afternoon.

We had an excellent and cheap lunch at “Berlin” on the market square: a
pleasant place with international cuisine (homemade spring rolls
Thai-style (24 kr), mushroom couscous (37 kr), and beef fillet with
Mediterranean vegetables and mushroom sauce (97 kr)).

We had to take a room in the town’s best hotel because all guesthouses
were full. Wesenbergh not only had an elegant façade but also very
decent, large rooms for 800 kroons including breakfast, perhaps a bit
expensive.

In the afternoon we went for a walk around town. Rakvere may not be a
tourist hotspot, but it’s a nice, interesting place where you can
happily spend a few hours. Majestic castle ruins loom over the town, and
the centre is a strange mix of old, colourful wooden huts and fully
fledged socialist prefab blocks.

### Rakvere – Mustvee – Kodavere

For the first seventy kilometres we cycled on barely used roads across
flat, uninhabited terrain to Mustvee on the huge Lake Peipsi. From there
we took the coastal road south, the day’s most scenic stretch.

For several kilometres, colourful wooden houses lined both sides of the
road: a very unspoilt area. Pure Baltic romance.

## Day 9 — 1 August 2004 — 124 km

### Tartu – Otepää – Sangaste – Sõmerpalu – Nursi

By Estonian standards, today was a proper mountain day. The area around
the winter-sports and hiking centre of Otepää is not only one of the
country’s prettiest regions, with forests and lakes, but also the
hilliest.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0051.jpg" alt="Around Otepää">
  <em>Around Otepää</em>
</figure>

At first we rode fairly flat on long-distance cycle route 3 to Otepää
itself, a pleasant little tourist hub.

<div class="photo-row">

Then we followed cycle route 5
along the wonderfully pretty Puha Lake; there was even a large beach
here.

This section, in particular, was tough: countless short but very steep
pitches. Above the lakeshore we had lunch on the fantastic viewpoint
terrace of the Irish pub Setanta. The food was decent: seafood salad
(50 kr), chicken-and-shrimp paella (80 kr), but the view was the real
highlight.

Near Sangaste we left cycle route 5 and took the very undulating road
69, trending uphill, to Sõmerpalu. From there a small lane led through
insect-infested forest and swamp to today’s destination, Nursi.

Our accommodation, Rohtlätte, was a little outside the hamlet in the
woods and ticked every Baltic-holiday box. We had a small wooden cabin
with a simple but pleasant attic room, plus a sauna and a fireplace room
on the ground floor.

After the sauna we could even jump straight into our own small private
lake in front of the cabin to cool off. The whole lot cost 400 kroons
for two!

## Day 10 — 2 August 2004 — 122 km

### Nursi – Rõuge – Krabi – Ape – Alūksne – Balvi

Today was hilly throughout again, especially the section to Alūksne. We
first rode to the village of Rõuge, which Lonely Planet describes as the
prettiest village in the country. That must refer to its lovely setting
in a green valley by a lake with a beach, otherwise it offered only the
usual wooden houses, a café and two shops.

From here we followed cycle route 4 to the Latvian border. After Krabi
the road was unpaved and bumpy for 15 kilometres. This area is lonely
and remote even by Estonian standards.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0055.jpg" alt="Around Krabi">
  <em>Around Krabi</em>
</figure>

From the border it was another 30 hilly kilometres to the first larger
town, Alūksne, where we had an excellent and astonishingly cheap late
lunch in the town’s only proper restaurant.

At Pajumte there was a large portion of chicken fillet with peanut sauce
for 2.20 lats, chicken breast baked with onions and cheese for 1.35
lats, and potatoes with chanterelle sauce (1.50 lats).

The town itself was quite pleasant: an old castle in the park and a
large lake with little islands.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0056.jpg" alt="Alūksne">
  <em>Alūksne</em>
</figure>

From Alūksne it was another 40
kilometres or so, pleasant cycling, to Balvi, our destination for the
day.

Balvi is also set on a pretty lake, but it consists largely of prefab
blocks; not exactly a lovely townscape, though it’s softened by parks
and green areas. Our hotel, Viesnīca Balvi, the only one in town, was
also in a prefab block, a relic of socialism, but the rooms were
reasonably acceptable and spacious in Soviet chic. A cultural
experience, and at 18 lats including breakfast, affordable.

Around Rēzekne
the terrain became much hillier, which, together with the headwind and
the poor road surface, made for a fairly demanding day’s ride.

Rēzekne is the largest town in the whole region and at first glance it
looked anything but inviting: prefab blocks, potholes, no real centre, 
let alone any café or restaurant culture.

Because of a serious mechanical problem (a broken spoke), we were forced
to take a second look: the repair took so long that we decided not to
ride on.

In the end I didn’t find Rēzekne quite so terrible. The parks are very
pleasant, as is the district of colourful wooden houses on the road to
Krāslava. There’s even an Italian restaurant here, Little Italy, where
we had a snack in the afternoon.

The main dishes, especially the chicken fillet in gorgonzola sauce for
2.40 lats, were okay, while the Patatas Bravas suspiciously tasted like
chips with ketchup. Well, you shouldn’t expect too much.

Down by the river there was a nice beer garden with cheap beer (from
0.39 lats for 0.5 l). And a large part of Rēzekne’s young female
population looked like supermodels, almost eerie.

We even found a beautiful café, Mols, which didn’t quite fit the place:
a really cosy, arty café with tasty, cheap cocktails (Piña Colada, 1.45
lats), cherry beer (0.45 lats for 0.5 l), and good food, also very
cheap.

The town’s only hotel, Latgale, was another Soviet throwback: an ugly
prefab block with simple but acceptable rooms for 18 lats.

## Day 22 — 14 August 2004 — 37 km

### Klaipėda – Palanga

There are countless beer gardens, restaurants and cafés everywhere in

## Day 25 — 17 August 2004 — 70 km

## Day 32 — 24 August 2004 — 145 km

### Bauska – Riga – Sigulda

Then to Sigulda on the four-lane motorway, far nicer than it sounds,

bothered. Sigulda is the centre of Gauja National Park: a somewhat

On the way to Tallinn we made a detour to the coast, to Paldiski, in
Soviet times a submarine port and restricted area, today a vivid
illustration that something went wrong in ‘actually existing socialism’.
Paldiski consists largely of crumbling prefab blocks. A truly depressing
place, especially under grey skies and drizzle.

We drove into Tallinn along the north coast. Outside Keila-Joa there was
a nicely overgrown park with a suspension bridge and a small waterfall.

  <em>Keila-Joa</em>

We dropped the car at Tallinn’s small, airport close to town and cycled
5 km into the Old Town. Lunch was at Olde Hansa, Tallinn’s essential
culinary institution. You dine by candlelight in a dark room, the staff
wear medieval costumes, and everything is cooked strictly to surviving
medieval recipes.

Tourist trap or not, the quality was excellent, even if the prices were
a bit much. Tasty forest-mushroom soup with fennel rolls (98 kr) and, of
course, the non plus ultra of the menu: bear with lingonberries and
pearl barley, absolutely delicious and absurdly expensive (580 kr). But
how often do you get to eat bear, the finest game meat of all?

  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0241.jpg" alt="Tallinn - Olde Hansa">

After lunch we moved into our apartment, which we’d booked online. The
location was perfect, right in the middle of the Old Town (Pikk 37), and
it was well equipped too: satellite TV, washing machine, even free
internet would have been available. Only the bathroom was a bit small, 
but for €50, the value for money was excellent.

In the afternoon we cycled 7 km along the coastal bike path to Pirita,
Tallinn’s beach area, to drop the bikes off for a quick check-up. While
we waited, we had cappuccino on the beautiful terrace of Café Bellevue
and then briefly went down to the beach.

  <em>Tallinn</em>

Back in town we had an amuse gueule at the posh Karl Friedrich, with a
view of Town Hall Square. Haute-cuisine portions, but the quality was
very good and the prices reasonable: salmon tartare for 79 kr and foie
gras poêlé with caramelised apples for 159 kr. Unfortunately they also
feed tour groups here, which hurts the atmosphere a bit.

After an excellent cappuccino at Café Kehrwieder on Town Hall Square, we
wrapped up today’s culinary itinerary with a few small things at
Möökkala, a very prettily furnished fish restaurant. The two starters, 
raw salted salmon (30 kr) and green mussels in garlic butter (23 kr), 
were very good, and the flounder with basil (44 kr) was perfectly decent
too.

## Day 41 — 2 September 2004 — 19 km

In the morning we got our bikes out again and rode to Nõmme, a nice,
quiet district where lots of old wooden villas still sit in greenery.
Here we found a great and popular Georgian place, Pirosmani, in the
woods, with outdoor tables.

The food was excellent and very cheap: mushrooms stuffed with cheese (60
kr), grilled mackerel with mashed potato (55 kr), and lamb stew (84 kr).
Back in town we had an excellent cappuccino at the prettiest Kehrwieder
branch, in a cellar off Town Hall Square.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingBaltics2004-0245.jpg" alt="Tallinn - Café Kehrwieder">
  <em>Tallinn - Café Kehrwieder</em>
</figure>

Then we explored Tallinn’s New Town, a fascinating mix of old wooden
houses and new high-rises, some of them architecturally genuinely
successful. There’s also a good selection of chic, stylish cafés here,
usually cheaper than in the Old Town.

One of the most stylish places in the New Town is City Gourmet, a café
with a delicatessen shop that wouldn’t look out of place in any capital.
Both the coffee and the Tahitian tuna tartare (150 kr) were excellent, 
if not exactly cheap. Very friendly service!

Another pleasant surprise in the afternoon was Golden Dragon, 
essentially our go-to Chinese, since it sits in a nice vaulted cellar in
the same building as our apartment. The dim sum (59 kr for six) were
excellent, and the Hong Kong duck with garlic-chilli sauce (179 kr) was
very good, generous, and actually spicy.

Coffee afterwards at the pretty, classic Café Aroma: one of the best and
cheapest (20 kr) cappuccinos in the Old Town.

Dinner was an absolute hit. The unimaginatively named Sushi House is
almost certainly among the very best Tallinn has to offer, food-wise.
Nicely decorated in simple chic, with friendly, competent service, it
isn’t a classic Japanese restaurant, but a modern, creative fusion place
with high-quality treats at civilised prices for what you get: yakitori
(30 kr), veal fillet with chanterelles and wasabi (45 kr), and the sushi
were perfect too, from classic tuna-and-avocado temaki (45 kr) to the
deliciously creative scallop-and-Roquefort fusion nigiri (35 kr).

## Day 42 — 3 September 2004

Our last day in Tallinn. After buying our tickets at the harbour for
tomorrow’s crossing to Helsinki, we went for lunch at Pegasus, a
minimalist, fashionable spot with creative fusion cooking that is among
the very best Tallinn has to offer.

Thai soup with truffle foie gras (115 kr) and fried scallops (195 kr).
Gourmet portions, but absolutely delicious. Cappuccino afterwards at a
nice Kehrwieder branch inside Apollo bookshop.
