# Cycling Southern Italy
**25 March – 16 April 2004**

A bicycle tour from Naples through the south of Italy.

---

## Day 1 (25 March) – 58 km

### Naples – Pompeii – Sorrento

Riding out of Naples along the seafront was easy enough to navigate, and
mostly flat as far as Torre del Greco.

Even so, it really wore us down:
the road surface was dreadful, often cobbled stone with potholes, and
the traffic was pure chaos. The first long, drawn-out climb up the
slopes of Vesuvius came between Torre del Greco and Torre Annunziata.

Scenery-wise the stretch as far as Pompeii wasn’t particularly
appealing, apart from the view of Vesuvius, but the coastal towns on
Naples’ periphery were all quite interesting. Since the city of Pompeii,
buried in AD 79, lay only about a kilometre off our route, we didn’t
miss the chance to detour to this magnificent site. We spent a
full three hours wandering the lanes of what must be one of the
best-preserved ancient sites anywhere.

Camping Nubo d’Argento lies beautifully in a valley section west of the

cyclist-friendly, and both the pitches and the sanitary facilities were
rather basic (€19).

Outstanding dinner in the simple, reasonably priced Trattoria da Emilia
at the old fishing harbour, Marina Grande: delicious antipasti (€7) and
grilled squid (€9).

## Day 2 (26 March) – 18 km

### Capri

In the morning we cycled from the campsite to Sorrento harbour,
dramatically located below the cliffs on which the town sits.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0014.jpg" alt="Sorrento">
  <em>Sorrento</em>
</figure>

The ferry
crossing to Capri took about 40 minutes (€5.60 + €3 for the bike),
following the beautiful north coast of the Sorrento Peninsula. From
Marina Grande, where the boats dock, a road of about 2 km climbs in
hairpins up to Capri Town, 150 metres above sea level.

After a scandalously expensive cappuccino on the piazzale, we explored
the narrow, steep little lanes of this picturesque, if in places very
touristy, town. Time and again we were treated to glorious views along
the coast.

We had a very tasty lunch at the long-established Da Gemma in Capri. The
food was cheaper than the celebrity-heavy guest list might have
suggested: a superb antipasto buffet (€12) and a delicious grilled fish
(€30, but worth every cent).

Properly fortified, we tackled the second mountain stage: an adventurous
hairpin road with spectacular views up to Anacapri, the island’s
second-largest settlement, set at 275 metres. Up here it was cosier and
quieter, and with its whitewashed houses Anacapri reminded me a little
of Greek mountain villages.

12 minutes) up to the highest point of the island, a brilliant

The descent by bike down to the harbour was equally enjoyable; the
suffering on the way up had been absolutely worth it.

Back on the mainland, we went for a proper walk around Sorrento, a town

centre with its narrow alleyways there are countless inviting cafés and
restaurants, perfect for a relaxed evening. We ate delicious wood-fired
pizza, grilled fish and seafood salad at very reasonable prices in the
pretty Ristorante S. Antonio in the old town.

## Day 3 (27 March) – 34 km

### Sorrento – Termini – Sant’Agata – Positano

From Sorrento we continued along the lovely coastal road to Massa
Lubrense, where we had our morning caffè. After Massa, a climb of almost
5 km began, hard work, but softened by a beautiful view of sunlit
Capri.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0024.jpg" alt="Sorrento">
  <em>Sorrento</em>
</figure>

This part of the peninsula seems the most unspoilt and least
touristy, yet still very scenic.

From Termini we carried on, mostly uphill, into the interior of the
peninsula to Sant’Agata, where we rejoined the main road. It climbs in
hairpins up to the 470-metre Colli di Fontanelle, and from there we
caught our first glimpse of the famous Amalfitana.

The ensuing kilometres-long descent along the Amalfi Coast towards
Positano was, without exaggeration, the most glorious stretch I have
ever cycled.

most beautiful coastal roads. Deep-blue sea, high mountains behind, and

Positano is a jewel (not only for its setting, but in every respect)
and even tourism hasn’t managed to ruin it. We checked into our hotel
early in the afternoon and took full advantage of the sea-view balcony
at Hotel Il Gabbiano. The rooms in this 3-star place (€90), set quite
high up the hillside, were a little small, but the view from the balcony
was priceless.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0031.jpg" alt="Positano">
  <em>Positano</em>
</figure>

In the afternoon we went out exploring. Because of its extreme
mountainside location, Positano has hardly any streets; instead there
are mostly steep stairways. The old-town core, with its tight alleys,
almost feels like a North African medina, and between all the souvenir
stalls and palazzi there was a reassuring sense that people actually
live here. Most day-trippers arrive in the morning, and since we were
here out of season there was, thankfully, no sign of mass tourism.

Once again we had an excellent dinner at Saraceno d’Oro: delicious
antipasti, a perfect porcini pizza (€7) and grilled sea bream (€14). By
Positano standards, it felt like a bargain.

## Day 4 (28 March) – 48 km

### Positano – Amalfi – Salerno

Today we continued along the Amalfitana. After a longer climb, the first
town was Praiano, pleasant enough, though by local standards rather
unspectacular. The next 10 km to Amalfi offered plenty of gorgeous rocky
coves, and the road runs through several tunnels, so the climbs stayed
within reason. A fantastic stretch.

One of the coast’s best fish restaurants is also in Atrani, hidden in
the cellar of an old house. Even though it was packed at lunchtime, we
were the only non-Italians at A’ Paranza. The food, exclusively fish,
was superb: marinated anchovies, octopus, grilled fish… Not cheap, but
fairly priced.

  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0040.jpg" alt="Atrani">

We continued along the gorgeous, rolling coastal road, past the two
pleasant but less spectacular beach towns Minori and Maiori, to Cetara,
another jewel at the eastern end of the Amalfi Coast that has, happily,
escaped mass tourism. A wonderfully picturesque place with a romantic
black beach and a terrific atmosphere.

  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0041.jpg" alt="Cetara">

Cetara seems popular with
day-trippers from Salerno. Unfortunately there was nowhere to stay.

The last town before Salerno was Vietri, the ceramics town, set high
above the coast.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0042.jpg" alt="Vietri">
  <em>Vietri</em>
</figure>

We had one more caffè there, then enjoyed a long
descent down to our day’s destination.

Salerno, oddly, doesn’t have the best reputation and isn’t a classic
tourist stop, but it has two major upsides: a smart, car-free seafront
promenade, and an atmospheric old town with bars and trattorias in
winding lanes. The long Corso is, by southern Italian standards, an
unusually polished pedestrian street with outdoor cafés and shops, and
even the functional 1960s architecture didn’t bother me too much,
especially since we were lucky enough to be here on a Sunday evening. A
festival was in full swing, and the whole city seemed to be out and
about.

We spent the night at the Albergo d’Italia on the Corso. The roomy rooms
were rather plainly furnished, but at €70 very decent.

Dinner was at the venerable Trattoria S. Lucia. The food
(buffalo-mozzarella pizza, antipasto misto) was rather average, but at
least it was very cheap.

## Day 5 (29 March) – 70 km

### Salerno – Paestum – Agropoli – Santa Maria di Castellabate – San Marco

There isn’t much to say about the first 40 kilometres along the coast
between Salerno and Paestum. A flat, wide cycle path runs almost all the
way parallel to the road, which slightly makes up for the dull landscape
and the ugly, dirty beaches. Hard to believe this is supposed to be a
popular holiday destination.

The impressive Greek temples of Paestum lie a little inland and are
definitely worth a visit. The Temple of Poseidon, in particular, is
utterly imposing.

There are also two smaller temples and the ruins of a
small amphitheatre. Since you’re no longer allowed inside the temples,
it’s only worth paying to enter if you’re an amateur archaeologist or
a photographer: you can admire almost everything from the outside,
without missing much, free of charge.

A few kilometres on, we reached Agropoli, a larger town with a
wonderfully unspoilt old centre and a medieval castle on a hill above
the sea and the newer town.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0045.jpg" alt="Agropoli">
  <em>Agropoli</em>
</figure>

From up there you also get lovely views of
the harbour and the beginning of the Cilento, the coastal national park
we were about to tackle.

Before that, though, we ate at the simple, authentic Pizzeria Regina
Margherita on Agropoli’s Corso. It was really cheap, and the porcini
pizza was excellent, as was the seafood salad.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0046.jpg" alt="Agropoli - Pizzeria Regina Margherita">
  <em>Agropoli - Pizzeria Regina Margherita</em>
</figure>

After Agropoli we headed into the Cilento, where the scenery finally
became prettier again, though the road also turned hillier.
To reach Santa Maria we had to swing inland briefly, since there is no
continuous coastal road.

Santa Maria is the first highlight in the Cilento: a lovely little
seaside town whose entire old centre is a pedestrian zone running to a
pretty beach with a castle at the end. Out of season it’s so quiet you
could hear a pin drop, and the atmosphere feels unfiltered.

<div class="photo-row">

  <figure>

Hard to
believe southern Italy’s biggest club opens here in summer.

Since no hotel was open yet, we continued to the neighbouring village of
San Marco, also a very pretty, authentic fishing settlement. The
friendly \*\*\*Hotel Antonietta on the small village piazza was,
thankfully, open. The rooms weren’t big but were nicely furnished,
though at €65 not exactly cheap.

Once again we ate extremely well, and cheaply, at L’Aquario just above
the square: a family place with great pizza and an excellent seafood
risotto (€6.50).

## Day 6 (30 March) – 65 km

### San Marco – Acciaroli – Ascea-Velia – Pisciotta – Palinuro

After a generous hotel breakfast we continued along the Cilento coast on
a rolling road. The coastline may not be as dramatic as Amalfi’s, but it
is far less touristy.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0049.jpg" alt="Cilento">
  <em>Cilento</em>
</figure>

The first notable place was Acciaroli, which likes to boast that
Hemingway once stayed here. It has a pretty little old town and a
fishing harbour. We carried on along the coast via the pleasant small
town of Pioppi to Marina del Casal Velino, where we treated ourselves to
an espresso on the promenade.

Shortly afterwards the road left the shoreline and a 4 km climb began up
to Ascea-Velia at 230 metres. The following stretch to Palinuro offered,
alongside some adventurous climbs, stunning coastal scenery, and,
halfway along, the impressive medieval town of Pisciotta.

<div class="photo-row">

  <figure>

      <em>Cilento - Pisciotta</em>

  <figure>
      <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0051.jpg" alt="Cilento - Pisciotta">

    </figure>

</div>

Perched on a
hill high above the sea, it ticks every cliché box of southern Italy.

Palinuro, our destination for the day, lies on the north side of a
headland lined with nice sandy beaches.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0052.jpg" alt="Cilento - Palinuro">
  <em>Cilento - Palinuro</em>
</figure>

It seems to be the main resort
in the Cilento, but the season is short here too, and finding an open
hotel wasn’t easy.

In the end we ended up at the \*\*\*Miramare, where we got a lovely,
spacious room for €70, with splendid views from the balcony. Highly
recommended despite the small bathroom.

We were also lucky with dinner. The only restaurant open nearby, the
smart Luna Rossa, offered upmarket fish cooking at moderate prices. A
great antipasto plate, prawn carpaccio (€8.50) and grilled squid
(€6.50).

## Day 7 (31 March) – 40 km

### Palinuro – Marina di Camerota – Lentiscosa – San Giovanni – Scario

Leaving Palinuro, the road hugged the coastline for another ten
kilometres or so, a gorgeous stretch of secluded beaches and high cliffs,
punctuated by a handful of tunnels. Along the way we had views of
Spiaggia di Arco Naturale, a natural rock arch where the coast almost
felt like the Algarve.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0053.jpg" alt="Cilento - Spaggia di Arco Naturale">
  <em>Cilento - Spaggia di Arco Naturale</em>
</figure>

After Marina di Camerota the route turned into tight, steep hairpins for
a 6 km climb up to the little hill village of Lentiscosa. Not that this
marked the end of the hard work: the road continued upwards for
kilometres, crossing a pass through beautiful scenery that felt almost
alpine, before a long descent brought us to San Giovanni at around 450
metres above sea level.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0055.jpg" alt="Cilento - San Giovanni">
  <em>Cilento - San Giovanni</em>
</figure>

From there we had splendid views over the Gulf
of Policastro and the 1,200-metre Monte Bulgheria.

<figure>
  <img src="photos/CyclingSouthernItaly2004-0054.jpg" alt="Cilento">
  <em>Cilento</em>
</figure>

A 7 km descent with sea views dropped us back down to the coast and into
the pretty fishing village of Scario on the eastern edge of the Cilento,
our stop for the day.

We stayed for a very reasonable €40 at the
friendly Pensione Garagliano. The room was fairly basic but came with a
balcony and was well worth the money.

Finding something to eat was trickier, as almost everything along the
lovely harbour promenade was still shut. The only place open was the
Birreria “Incontro”. The menu was small, but the food was very decent
and surprisingly inexpensive: prawn carpaccio with Parmigiano (€8),
spaghetti alle vongole (€7) and bruschetta.

## Day 8 (1 April) – 57 km

### Scario – Sapri – Maratea – Praia a Mare

The first 15 km were fairly flat along the coast to Sapri, a larger town
whose main draw is its fantastic setting on the Gulf of Policastro. A
mechanical problem forced us into an unplanned long break here.

Beyond Sapri the coastal road turned hilly again, but it stayed right
above the sea: wonderful scenery of high mountains and secretive,
picturesque little bays of black sand.

the season) we were the only guests. Among other things we had excellent

## Day 9 (2 April) – 105 km

### Praia a Mare – Scalea – Diamante – Paola – Longobardi

In the morning we warmed up straight away by riding over a headland,
followed by a descent to Scalea on the SS18, the national road that
will accompany us all the way to Reggio.

## Day 10 (3 April) – 87 km

### Longobardi – Amantea – Gizzeria – Pizzo – Briatico

The first 40 km ran along the fairly busy SS18 by the sea, parallel to
the railway line. The scenery here is not exactly inspiring, and the
holiday resorts we passed, despite their long sandy beaches, felt
dull, in places even run-down. On the plus side the road was very flat
and we made quick progress.

Near Lamezia Terme airport we spent around 20 km inland, crossing a
bleak plain of industry and vegetable fields. Traffic was light here.
About 10 km before Pizzo we turned onto a quiet coastal road, and met
the first proper climb up into town.

Pizzo is a very pretty seaside place, its old town and castle sitting on
a rocky spur above the sea. On the main piazza a good dozen gelaterie
compete for customers. Pizzo’s signature seems to be tartufo, advertised
everywhere, and it really was sinfully good.

balcony at the Costa Azzurra on the lungomare above the water (€50). We
had a brief look around town, but it didn’t have all that much to offer.

A few steps from our hotel we found a nicely furnished wine bar with a
pizza oven, where we ate well and cheaply. The Diavolo was only €3.50,

## Day 17 (10 April) – 74 km

### Naxos – Fiumefreddo – Cottone – Riposto – Acireale – Catania

After a cappuccino in the pleasant (at least for the moment) beach
resort of Naxos, we took the main road, which rises gently inland, to
Fiumefreddo. There we turned off and dropped down to the coast at Marina
di Cottone. Here a kilometres-long stretch of undeveloped beach begins,
with a quiet road running through woodland alongside it. After the
little harbour town of Riposto we headed inland again, riding rolling
back roads through lemon and orange groves on the lower slopes of Etna.
It’s agricultural, but still a lovely area, and it deserves the name
Riviera di Limone.

Back at the coast there was a steep climb up to Acireale, the start of
the Riviera dei Ciclopi: a lava-rock coastline with bizarre offshore
formations.

## Day 20 (13 April) – 22 km

### Ischia – Procida

Because we’d slept in the tent and it had rained almost all night, we
couldn’t take the ferry to Procida until 11:20 (Caremar, €2.30). It was
still raining when we reached the harbour, so we went straight into a
travel agency (Graziano) to find a room quickly.

We booked an apartment at Le Grand Bleu, perfectly placed right in the
centre of the small island. We got a modern flat with a lovely bathroom,
a kitchen and a balcony for €60. There were also fantastic views from
the rooftop sun terrace across the island and the Gulf of Naples. A real
find.

Once we’d moved in and freshened up, the sun came out again and it
turned into a genuinely beautiful day. We got back on the bikes, because
the island is small enough to ride all the way round in half a day.

Although Procida is the closest of the three Gulf islands to Naples and
the mainland, it’s also the least known and least touristy, something I
still find completely baffling.

Via Toledo at nearby Piazza Dante, we began our tour of the Centro
Storico: first with a proper Neapolitan pizza and tasty antipasti at the
long-established Port'Alba, by the city gate of the same name.

We had our coffee in an expensive pavement café on Piazza Bellini, the
prettiest square in the old town just around the corner. When you wander
through UNESCO-listed Spaccanapoli today, it’s hard to believe this area
was supposedly pretty much a no-go area for tourists until the 1990s,
run-down and dangerous. It doesn’t feel threatening at all now. The
countless palazzi, houses and churches still haven’t really been spruced
up, though, and some of the narrow lanes probably looked exactly the
same decades ago, apart, perhaps, from the residents’ clothes hanging
from windows all over the place. But that’s exactly what gives the
Centro Storico its charm and its one-of-a-kind atmosphere; and as long
as people still live here, and a complete renovation is financially more
or less impossible, it will probably never turn into the glossy tourist
trap that a neighbourhood like this would be in other European cultural
cities.

It’s also interesting that modern Naples is, quite literally, built on
the ruins of ancient Neapolis, something you can see for yourself in
the excavations beneath Chiesa S. Lorenzo Maggiore (€4).

Dinner was at China Dragon near the station. After three weeks of
Italian food, it was time for a bit of variety: good dim sum (€2.60),
prawns and beef on the hot plate. Very cheap and tasty.

All in all, Naples, not only because of its one-of-a-kind setting, is
one of Europe’s most beautiful and most interesting cities.

I actually
liked it better than Rome or London. Naples still seems to suffer a
little from its grubby image: in other cities of this calibre, hordes of
tourists practically trample each other to death; in Naples, by
contrast, tourists mostly pass through, so the city is still firmly in
the hands of the locals, perhaps the most important reason for its
unique atmosphere.

I’d always assumed Naples would be interesting, but I was genuinely
surprised by how beautiful it is, both scenically and architecturally,
because I had associated Naples mainly with dirt and chaos. Dirt and chaos are part of the city, just like the gorgeous coastline
and grand palaces, wood-fired pizza and chic cafés, run-down residential
quarters, fishing boats, washing lines, and wildly gesticulating locals
in sunglasses. That’s Naples, and it’s a lot more besides.
