# Australia – Thailand – Hong Kong – Cambodia (2004–2005)

## 6 Oct 2004 — Sydney

I arrived in Sydney at 6:30 a.m. and took the shuttle bus (\$9) to
King’s Cross, Sydney’s red-light and backpacker district east of the
city centre. Of the many backpacker hostels in the area, I chose Eva’s —
not a bad decision: decent six-bed dorms, clean bathrooms, a friendly
atmosphere, free internet and breakfast. After claiming my bed, I slept
a few more hours, and by noon I was ready to start exploring the city.

I bought a weekly ticket for Sydney’s excellent bus/train/ferry network
(\$32) and took the train into town, getting off at Town Hall in the
heart of the city. I walked straight through the busy centre along
George Street all the way to The Rocks, an old harbour district with
restored buildings that now house cafés and restaurants. The area is
very touristy, but it offers great views of the world-famous Opera House
and Harbour Bridge.

From Circular Quay, the city’s ferry terminal, I took the next ferry I
could find just to enjoy Sydney’s layout and skyline from the water. And
that ferry ride left no doubt: Sydney is rightly considered one of the
most spectacular and beautiful cities in the world. The views were
incredible — the skyline, the Opera House, the countless quiet coves in
the huge fjord-like harbour.

Back at Circular Quay I ate a tasty chicken döner (\$6.50) and took the
bus to Little Italy in Darlinghurst, where I had a cappuccino in one of
the many cafés on Stanley Street. From Little Italy it was a 15‑minute
walk back to the hostel.

In the dorm I met an Austrian guy, and in the evening we went to
O’Malleys — a great pub in King’s Cross with excellent live guitar music
and an international party vibe. Five beers later (\$4.30 a pint) I
finally went to bed around 3 a.m.

## 7 Oct 2004 — Sydney

When I woke up the next morning and realised, to my surprise, that I
didn’t have a hangover, I got ready for another day of sightseeing. I
took the train to Central and first walked through Sydney’s busy
Chinatown. You’re tempted by countless restaurants, dirt-cheap food
courts and shops — a typical Chinatown. I saved the food for later,
though, as I wasn’t hungry at all.

My next destination was Darling Harbour, a huge, kitschy entertainment
complex with a marina. Along both sides of the waterfront promenade
there are lots of really nice restaurants and cafés — mostly not really
within my budget — but they make Darling Harbour much more interesting.
Just reading menus on your own is fun, too.

I wandered on through downtown, past The Rocks and Circular Quay, and
into the Botanic Garden — a fantastic, huge park right by the sea east
of the Opera House. I spent the whole afternoon here, walking along the
waterfront to Macquarie’s Point, enjoying the spectacular views, and
seeing cockatoos for the first time in my life.

I took the bus back to King’s Cross and had a plate of steamed chicken
dumplings (\$9) at the tiny hole-in-the-wall place Noodles & Dumplings.
Really good.

I decided not to go out tonight — it was about time to recover after the
intense last few days. Instead I went up to the hostel rooftop terrace,
enjoyed the night view of the illuminated skyline, and devoured Steve
Martin’s Shopgirl in one go.

## 8 Oct 2004 — Sydney

After muesli at the hostel and a cappuccino at my King’s Cross regular
café, I took the train to Newtown, one of Sydney’s hip student
neighbourhoods. The area’s main street, King Street, is an interesting
mix of ethnic restaurants — from cheap to stylish — espresso bars and
bookshops. Everything feels a bit cheap and run-down, but that is
exactly what gives the place its charm and appeal.

I had a very good falafel at the Lebanese place Al Bahsa and bought two
cheap books in a second-hand bookshop: Bonjour Tristesse by Françoise
Sagan and The Fox by D. H. Lawrence.

After that I walked from Newtown across the university grounds and
Victoria Park to Glebe, another student neighbourhood with a similar
selection of cafés and shops, but much prettier and better kept than
Newtown — a very nice area, also a good place to live. At the end of
Glebe Point Road, after a cappuccino at an Italian café, I took the bus
to Circular Quay because on this wonderful spring day with blue skies I
really wanted to take some photos from the ferry.

I took the boat from Circular Quay to Darling Harbour, passing all of
Sydney’s landmarks. A fantastic trip. From Darling Harbour I walked
through downtown past the grand old Victoria Mall to Hyde Park, where I
rested for a while before taking the train back to King’s Cross.

Before meeting Stefan at the station I went to Chinatown for dinner at
the Dixon Food Court. The food stalls will feed you well for very little
money. A huge portion of beef in hoisin sauce on a hot plate with rice
cost \$6.95 and was too much for me alone. Not a culinary highlight, but
tasty and cheap.

I went with Stefan to a café on Oxford Street and he told me his best
travel stories from Pakistan and Tibet. After we had said goodbye in
Istanbul back then, he had spent a few months in Lyon and then travelled
overland to India, and via Bangkok back to Australia, where he is now
studying in Sydney.

Around half past eleven I went with some people from the hostel — a
colourful mix of Brits, Germans and Dutch — to the 3 Wise Monkeys pub
downtown. It was Friday, the place was packed, and it’s a miracle that
we all got past the bouncers. Upstairs there was a very good live band
and a dance floor. Unfortunately the music played in between was boring
mainstream rubbish, but we were already so drunk that it didn’t matter,
and I didn’t get to bed till five.

## 9 Oct 2004 — Sydney

On this Saturday “morning” (12 o’clock) I took the bus to Coogee,
Sydney’s second most popular beach. It is much smaller than I had
expected and not particularly attractive. On the plus side, it is one of
the cheaper parts of Sydney and there are a few nice cafés and pubs. The
Sydney Oktoberfest will also take place here in two weeks.

From Coogee I did an almost three-hour walk along the coast over several
headlands to Bondi Beach. The most spectacular and most beautiful part
of the route lay between the huge Waverley Cemetery, which sits directly
on the coastal slopes, and Bondi Beach. This is also where the two
beautiful, less crowded bays of Bronte Beach and Tamarama lie. Well
worth doing — and a bit of exercise never hurts.

Bondi Beach is one of the most famous beaches in the world and the most
popular in Sydney. I don’t really know why. The beach is neither
spectacular nor particularly long — rather ordinary and overcrowded.
Bondi has the typical Sydney mix of cheap take-aways, chic stylish
cafés, vegan places, esoteric shops — the whole range. But it doesn’t
really suit my taste. The cool, laid-back surfer vibe feels somehow
forced here, and there was far too much hustle and bustle.

I had the first fish & chips of my life (\$7.40) at one of Sydney’s
supposedly best seafood shops, but if that was supposed to be high
quality, then I don’t even want to try any others. One thing is clear:
this greasy stuff will certainly not become my favourite food, even
though the Aussies love it.

In the evening, England–Wales match, lots of beer, and an 18-year-old
Dutch girl who tried to convince me that Pim Fortuyn was a misunderstood
benefactor with the right ideas. Oh dear.

## 10 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Today was one of those days when I didn’t get anything done — but that
suited this wonderfully lazy, sunny Sunday perfectly.

In the morning I took a short walk from King’s Cross to Rushcutters Bay,
a pretty, cosy bay with a marina and a park, where locals were
picnicking or enjoying the sun.

I spent the afternoon on the hostel rooftop terrace and read Bonjour
Tristesse, often interrupted by the social duties of a backpacker in
Australia: small talk. You could really just hand your conversation
partner a slip of paper with pre-printed answers to the same old
questions — it would be less tiring.

In the early evening I took another short walk through the pretty
nightlife districts of Darlinghurst and Paddington and had a leisurely
cappuccino. After three beers we — meaning people from the hostel — went
to O’Malleys, where there was great funky live music and even on Sundays
it was really happening. Still, I was in bed by half past two.

## 11 Oct 2004 — Sydney

The jackhammers started at 8 a.m. A pretty awful start to Monday, being
ripped out of last night’s sweet dreams so roughly — you simply gotta
luv them English gals. But since I was already awake so early, I decided
to do another proper day of sightseeing.

I took the bus towards Watson Bay via Double Bay and Vaucluse — a
wonderful ride with beautiful views of the harbour and the skyline. I
got off at The Gap, a lookout on rugged sea cliffs above Watson Bay.

I walked along the cliffs, enjoyed the views and the bright blue sky,
and followed the path to Camp Cove, a wonderful swimming bay with golden
sand on the gentler side of the peninsula.

After a detour to Lady Bay Beach, a beautiful, secluded nude-bathing
cove, I walked back to Watson Bay. Watson is one of Sydney’s most scenic
neighbourhoods: elegant villas on an idyllic bay, a few fish restaurants
and cafés — and still very relaxed.

I drank the most expensive cappuccino I’ve had in Sydney so far (\$3.50)
and watched the people at the tables around me feast on crayfish and
lobster claws. My budget would only have stretched to fish & chips, but
I could do without that.

I waited an hour in the park for the ferry to Circular Quay — another
highlight with great views. Back at the hostel I realised that I had
lost my key. Since it could only have been in Watson Bay Park, I took
the bus back there and actually found it.

On the way back I also got to enjoy one of the most impressive sunsets
I’d ever seen from the Vaucluse Hills. The sun sank like a red fireball
between the skyline and the Harbour Bridge on a pink horizon. I couldn’t
have been happier.

In the evening we went to the 3 Wise Monkeys, where a rock band was
playing — which I didn’t find particularly exciting — and I was also
dead tired.

## 12 Oct 2004 — Manly

32 degrees and sunshine — perfect weather for a relaxed day at Manly
Beach, a beach resort north of Sydney. We took the ferry, which crosses
the entire eastern part of the harbour, and all of us took a midday nap
at the pretty little Shelley Beach south of Manly. I even went for a
swim — wonderfully refreshing in those temperatures.

At sunset we took the ferry back to Circular Quay — magical and
indescribable. The evening turned into the wildest party since I’ve been
here. Half the hostel went to O’Malleys, got drunk and danced. The sense
of community among backpackers here in Australia is really fascinating,
and the way people treat each other is extremely relaxed — no matter
where you’re from, everyone talks to everyone, drinks with everyone,
flirts with everyone. I suppose that has to do with the fact that many
people are away from home for long stretches and really feel free.

## 13 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Despite a bad headache, I went to the Fish Market in the morning with
Matthias, a chef from Munich, and Wicke and Annet, two 18-year-old girls
from Germany. We had decided the day before in Manly to shop for a
proper BBQ. I had delicious oysters for breakfast, and we bought
barramundi, flounder, mussels, scallops and prawns — all for just \$30.

Today the temperature reached 37 degrees — the hottest October day in
more than 50 years — so everyone spent the afternoon doing nothing at
the hostel; it was simply too hot for anything else. When it cooled down
a little in the evening, we put on a wonderful BBQ on the rooftop
terrace with the seafood we had bought. Eating grilled scallops while
looking out over Sydney’s illuminated skyline really is pure luxury — a
fantastic experience.

Later we went to O’Malleys again, but it was surprisingly quiet and the
music was rather contemplative today, so the evening stayed fairly
relaxed.

## 14 Oct 2004 — Blue Mountains

It had cooled down by a good ten degrees — ideal weather for a trip to
the Blue Mountains, two hours west of Sydney. So early in the morning I
set off to the station together with Annet, Wicke and Bernadette. The
return ticket cost \$36 and included the train ride as well as the
hop-on/hop-off bus, which runs from Katoomba to various lookout points
from where you can do short hikes.

All in all, a very nice day trip away from the big city. The area really
is beautiful: densely forested mountain scenery as far as the eye can
see, green valleys, rock formations — and we even saw parrots glowing
red and blue along the way.

We did quite a bit of hiking, including a walk to the Katoomba Falls,
which unfortunately carried little water due to the dry days before,
down into the valley and then back up again via the 2,000 steps. We also
walked from Echo Point, the most beautiful viewpoint in the Blue
Mountains, along the clifftop track to Honeymoon Lookout and beyond.

After a cappuccino in the quite nice little town of Katoomba, we headed
back to Sydney in the evening. The evening at the hostel was very
pleasant. We sat on the rooftop terrace, drank wine, and I grilled the
barramundi that had been left over from yesterday. Elaine’s birthday was
tomorrow and we decided that, by Fiji time, we could already start
celebrating. Still, for once I did make it to bed at 1 a.m.

## 15 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Today I went to the bank to open an account, which was fairly
straightforward and necessary if you want to work here. I’ve now
basically seen everything in Sydney that you’re supposed to see as a
tourist and can start taking care of more practical things.

I’ve also decided to look for my own room next week. Hostel life, as
nice and sociable as it is, becomes quite exhausting over time, and if I
stayed here too long I’d likely be unable to get anything done at all.
On top of that, constantly going out costs money.

At midday I did a quick sightseeing circuit through Sydney with a few
very nice new arrivals from Germany — Botanic Garden, Opera House, The
Rocks — so I got some exercise and it was a pleasant outing. For fun we
went for a Radler at the Löwenbräu at The Rocks: a proper German beer
hall, with dirndls and lederhosen, and brass bands playing in the
evenings. Interestingly, this pub is one of the most popular places in
Sydney, and even I liked it a lot — the Radler is good, and the music,
at least in the afternoon, excellent: Grönemeyer, Die Ärzte,
2‑Raum‑Wohnung. Probably not the last time I’ll be there.

In the evening Annet and I went to Potts Point Lookout, not far from the
hostel, to say goodbye. It offers wonderful views of the nighttime
skyline and the Harbour Bridge. The night turned out to be intense
again: it was Friday, and everyone felt the need to really celebrate. We
first went to the Cargo Bar, a hip mainstream club with a fantastic
location at Darling Harbour. Then a short detour to the casino, which I
found absolutely awful — a real kitsch palace — and afterwards on to
Pier 36, an open-air lounge bar, also with an enviable location above
the marina. Bed at 5 a.m., and I wasn’t even drunk.

## 16 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Today was a really pleasant, relaxed day. It has turned fairly cold, but
at least the sun was out. I went to Newtown and Glebe with Elaine and
Andy to stroll around a bit and buy some books in second-hand bookshops.
In the evening, O’Malleys.

## 17 Oct 2004 — Sydney

The Australian winter is back — cold and rainy. I spent most of the day
at the hostel reading Bill Bryson’s Neither Here Nor There. In the
evening I met up with Stefan. We first went to the cinema — Collateral,
not exactly a masterpiece, but quite entertaining.

After that he took me to the club where he works. That was brilliant:
the club was still closed, we sat in the lounge, and I could drink
whatever I wanted for free. It was a welcome change from hostel life,
and it’s always a pleasure talking to an Aussie as well-travelled and
cool as Stefan.

Already nicely tipsy, I met the others at the World Bar, where we
carried on drinking for a while before ending the evening at O’Malleys.

## 18 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Today was a complete disaster: 10 degrees, wind and constant rain.
During the day I only left the hostel once to buy something to eat — I
really hadn’t expected weather like this here.

In the evening things were much better. A fantastic band was playing at
O’Malleys, and some of their versions of classics were even better than
the originals. We were all very drunk and could hardly believe that the
pub already closed at 3 a.m. on a Monday. After that I went with Nina,
Claudia and Julian to the Empire, the only place in the area (apart from
the strip clubs) that was still open, and we were perfectly capable of
having animated discussions about the education system and the EU’s
eastward expansion. That is what really distinguishes us Germans from
the English.

## 19 Oct 2004 — Sydney

The bad weather continued, but at least I managed to get out again
today. I went with Elaine to Manly and Freshwater Bay to look at the
apartment we might be moving into next week. Freshwater Bay has a
beautiful beach, and it would be great if the apartment worked out.

The evening was great again. We went out in a large group, got back to
the hostel around three and kept drinking until eventually only Chris,
Elaine and I were left. At some point Chris said goodbye as well, and I
spent the rest of the night with Elaine in front of the hostel. We
drank, smoked and philosophised about the meaning of life.

At some point one of those perverse freaks who populate King’s Cross
joined us — harmless, more like a failed philosopher who couldn’t really
copen’t cope with the world and went off the rails. An interesting
experience, and I felt like I was in a surreal film.

The best thing about this night, however, was that I really got to know
Elaine properly for the first time, and to my surprise realised that she
is anything but the simple-minded backpacker slag I had initially taken
her for. On the contrary — she is one of those people who hide their
true character, their dreams and their depth beneath a surface of
alcohol, sex and partying. We are all aware that this escapism is not a
solution, but it is arguably better to have fun than to be crushed by
melancholy.

I went to bed at six.

## 20 Oct 2004 — Sydney

I slept all day, and when I woke up in the afternoon it was still
pouring — no change from the days before. I’m really glad I got all my
sightseeing done last week while the weather was good, because right now
there’s genuinely nothing you can do here apart from hanging around,
going to a café now and then, reading (at the moment I’m reading Quiet
Days in Clichy by Henry Miller), and looking forward to the evening.

Today we broke the record: 25 (!) people from Eva’s went out together.
The night only lasted until half past three, but it was really nice and
very sociable.

## 21 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Today was finally a day when you could leave the house again, even
though the weather still wasn’t particularly good. I went with a few
Germans to the Chinese Garden in Darling Harbour. A worthwhile outing —
an oasis of calm surrounded by skyscrapers.

Afterwards we went to Chinatown to eat at the Dixon Food Court. I
started with delicious raw spring rolls at the Vietnamese place,
followed by lobster prawns with cashews on the hot plate. All for under
10 euros.

We spent the evening up on our rooftop terrace, and the guys all got
drunk very quickly. I only had three glasses of wine and I was dead
tired, so after a beer at O’Malleys I decided to call it an early night
for once.

## 22 Oct 2004 — Sydney

After eleven hours of sleep — finally — I was still pretty exhausted
from the last few days. We went to Newtown to do a bit of shopping. The
men found everything we wanted after the first round, and the girls were
frustrated because, as usual, they couldn’t make up their minds. So at
four o’clock we went to the pub first — “just for one”.

In the evening we first went to the Slip Inn, a nice club with a Thai
restaurant in the city. There was a lovely garden terrace and good
music. After that we went dancing at the Cargo Bar. Later the English
were so unbelievably drunk that I was genuinely worried Elaine might
throw up on me in the taxi.

This is all getting a bit extreme for me. I’m no saint either, but the
English are really wearing me out — you simply can’t drink that much.
Still, it’s another week at Eva’s until my tax file number arrives. I
hope my life in Australia will take on a more orderly shape then. This
can’t go on like this.

## 23 Oct 2004 — Sydney

It was the first sunny day in a long time, and when you’re in Sydney on
a day like that, you go to the beach. The stupid thing was that we only
managed to leave around half past one, because we were still waiting for
the last alcohol casualties. The trip to Bondi took more than an hour,
because apparently the whole city wanted to go there.

It felt really good to enjoy the sun again for a bit. In the evening I
went to O’Malleys with Mark and two girls from Israel, Yaara and Shira.
They were the kind of Israelis I really like: individual, open and
secular. They suffer most from the fact that their compatriots (quite
rightly) have a bad reputation in the traveller community. We drank beer
and exchanged German–Hebrew vocabulary. A very nice, funny evening.

## 24 Oct 2004 — Sydney

The weather is really starting to get on my nerves, and I’m beginning to
doubt whether it was the right decision to make Sydney my base. It
rained all day again.

We used lunchtime to say goodbye to Sebastian and Martin, two really
nice guys from Ravensburg — proper Swabians — whom I had often gone out
with. Just to show what a strong sense of community there is at Eva’s:
the whole procedure took more than two hours before all addresses were
exchanged and photos taken.

They bought a VW van and want to cruise around Australia for half a
year. For the first time since I’ve been here, the travel bug bit me
again — I really long to set off and travel myself, and I would have
loved to go along with them right away.

The life I’m leading at the moment, despite all the great people I’ve
met, despite all the entertainment and the feeling of freedom, has
become a bit monotonous. In the afternoon I wanted to be on my own, so I
went to Café Fellini in Darlinghurst, had a cappuccino, ate bruschetta
and stared out at the grey sky and the drizzle. Am I really in
Australia?

Looking back later, I’ll likely say that I had the time of my life here
— and that may well be true — but while it’s happening I often find it
hard to really enjoy “the time of my life”. I should be happy, and I
suppose I am, but there are phases when I just can’t take it anymore and
need a break. Just me and this city — is it even Sydney? Basically, this
city could be anywhere.

The night was quite nice again. We first went to the World Bar and sat
outside, which was great for talking, and then went dancing at the
Bourbon. I talked for a long time with a Brazilian girl from the hostel,
which meant I now have a free guide for Rio.

## 25 Oct 2004 — Sydney

Finally sun again — and warm, too. I went to The Rocks with Elaine and
Jules, an English girl who had previously spent six months in Asia,
because Elaine needed advice on buying a digital camera. On the way back
we did some shopping for a BBQ.

In the afternoon we lay in the sun on the rooftop terrace. In the
evening I was so tired that I went to bed already at half past eight
after the BBQ. Even though today was in fact very nice, I just don’t
feel comfortable in the hostel anymore. I think I’ve simply been here
too long — it’s time for a change.

## 26 Oct 2004 — Sydney

A perfect spring day — 26 degrees and sunshine. I went for a long walk
through one of Sydney’s prettiest neighbourhoods, Paddington, with its
rows of Victorian houses and lovely cafés. It felt good to consciously
realise I was in Sydney again — away from the hostel and all the other
backpackers.

On Friday I’m definitely leaving — hopefully into a place of my own. If
that doesn’t work out, I’ll probably go and work on a farm for a while,
just to get some distance. It’s strange how much the weather affects
your mood: all those days of rain brought me to a low point despite the
nice company; now I’m happy again and glad to be here.

At Obelisk Cafe I ran into Claudia by pure chance — a woman from Hamburg
I’d met last week at O’Malleys. We went together to the house in
Darlinghurst where she’s renting a room. I felt properly at ease there:
everything was a bit worn and run-down, but genuinely comfortable. We
spent the whole afternoon in old armchairs on the balcony, smoking and
chatting. I felt very “Bohemian like you”.

That evening I did another BBQ with Elaine on the roof terrace. Since
almost everyone I really liked has left by now, I decided to stay in —
but I couldn’t sleep properly, so at about three I went downstairs for a
cigarette.

Elaine was just coming back from the pub — drunk, naturally. She threw
her arms around me, snogged me senseless and tried to explain how much I
meant to her, and so on… The best thing about our relationship is that
we both give each other all the freedom in the world and don’t cling to
each other the whole time. For that reason alone — because she thinks
the same way I do — I didn’t hesitate for a second when she asked
whether we shouldn’t move in together; besides, I’m very fond of her. An
Englishwoman who wakes up in the dorm in the morning and realises she’s
completely naked — but can’t remember why — you just have to love.

## 27 Oct 2004 — Sydney

To my great delight, I discovered that Before Sunset was still playing
in cinemas here in Sydney, and since I’d been looking forward for years
to the sequel to my favourite film, there was no way I was going to miss
it — and what can I say: Before Sunset is the most beautiful, most
wonderful film I have ever seen. It managed to make me happy. Afterwards
I wandered through the streets of Sydney as if in a trance, smiling to
myself — and that despite the fact that, like its predecessor, the film
is basically “just” a dialogue between the two protagonists. But what is
said — the themes — is meaningful, and it spoke straight to my soul.

That same evening, Elaine and I spontaneously decided to turn our backs
on Sydney for a while, because the whole apartment situation was still
dragging on. We found a cheap flight to Fiji for the following Sunday
and went for it. At the moment the job market is pretty bad anyway; from
early December, when the summer season starts, it should be easier —
especially in hospitality — to find something. Until then, the money
will just have to last.

## 7 Nov 2004 — Sydney → Fiji

The next ten days were mostly fairly uneventful. We did meet a few more
nice people — Ty from London, Yael from Israel — but we didn’t go out
nearly as much anymore, mainly because of money.

In terms of sightseeing, Balmain is especially worth mentioning — a
pretty neighbourhood with lots of cafés, shops and nice architecture —
and Paddy’s Market, a big indoor market with clothes and food stalls
that brings a slice of Asia to Sydney.

I'm no longer sure whether the decision to go to Fiji with Elaine was
the right one, even though I really like her — financially the whole
thing is pretty risky. But above all it was about developing a plan to
get out of Eva’s and King’s Cross and to escape the big city for a
while.

The last evening was strange — depending on the moment I felt a mix of
wistfulness, indifference and excitement. Since the flight was early and
we had to be at the airport at 5 a.m., we stayed up all night: one last
time to O’Malleys, smoked some grass, and got the farewell ceremony over
with. I was completely finished and dead tired when I got on the plane.

Stupidly, it wasn’t a direct flight — we first had to go to Brisbane,
where we had a two-hour layover that we drifted through in a trance. But
the onward flight to Nadi (three hours) was fine, because we could
sleep.

## Fiji — Arrival

Because of the time difference, we didn’t arrive in Fiji until around 5
p.m., and spent the first night in a beach suburb of Nadi at the Tropic
of Capricorn — a simple but decent budget resort where a double room
with private bathroom and a shared kitchen cost \$45. The beach here
isn’t particularly attractive, but the atmosphere is incredibly relaxed
— exactly what we wanted.

In the evening we ran into Ty again, who’s on his way to South America
and is spending a week in Fiji. We went out for a small bite to eat and
decided to spend the next few days together.

## 15 Nov 2004 — Fiji

The week flew by. We took the bus (\$5) to the Coral Coast, where we
spent the first three days at the Tubakula Resort. We had an entire
house with kitchen and veranda for \$102 and spent the days playing
table tennis, lounging by the pool, and enjoying the gorgeous sunsets on
the beach.

We decided to rent a car for the next four days to explore the island,
so we took the bus to Suva (\$6.80), Fiji’s capital, and stayed about 10
kilometres outside in the laid-back Raintree Lodge surrounded by
rainforest. A dorm bed cost \$20 and the bathrooms were fairly basic,
but otherwise the place was really lovely — with a wonderful restaurant
by a small lake and cosy hammocks where you could listen to the sounds
of the jungle.

In the evening we drove into Suva in our rental car (\$90 a day) to run
a few errands, but the city was packed and very hectic because the
Indian population was celebrating Diwali the next day — a kind of New
Year festival. We had a great evening back at the lodge: a proper dinner
with wine, getting stoned in the hammock, and long conversations while
blissed out. We didn’t go to bed until 3:30 even though we had a lot
planned for the next day.

We drove back from Suva to the Coral Coast because we’d been invited to
Diwali by an Indian family we’d met shopping a few days earlier. On the
way we stopped for lunch at the Beachhouse, a very nice backpacker lodge
on one of the most beautiful stretches of the Coral Coast. Afterwards we
hiked to a gorgeous waterfall near the village of Biasevu — 45 minutes
through beautiful scenery. The waterfall was impressive and had a pool
for a refreshing swim.

In the afternoon we drove on to Natandola Beach, the only beach on the
Coral Coast that’s good for swimming regardless of the tides — a perfect
long sand beach in a sheltered bay without a reef. You could handle life
here!

We took a room at the Casablanca Hotel in Korotogo because it was close
to the family’s house. An excellent decision! We got a huge room with a
bathtub and balcony for \$70 and got ready to celebrate Diwali. We had
bought sweets and fireworks, and Elaine had even picked up a sari in
Suva. It was a great evening: we visited all kinds of relatives of the
family, drank kava, were fed Indian delicacies, and got a real insight
into local life.

The next day we took it easy. We spent a few hours in Sigatoka burning
photo CDs (not very easy here in Fiji) and then went back for a swim at
Natandola Beach.

Ty was flying to Auckland the next day, so we took a room near Nadi at
the Beach Escape Villas (\$75 for a simple but very spacious bungalow).
We treated ourselves to a delicious dinner at the beach restaurant of
the Travelers Beach Resort (grilled snapper in lolo sauce for \$14.90),
then went back to the bungalow, drank wine and smoked grass until we
were all stoned.

The next day we took Ty to the airport and drove further along King’s
Road on Fiji’s north coast. This area is very untouristy and
traditional, and the landscape is dreamlike. We had originally wanted to
go to a resort on the offshore island of Nananu i Ra, but we didn’t feel
like waiting that long for the boat, so we decided to continue to
Korovou.

What we hadn’t reckoned with was the state of the road between Viti Levu
Bay and Korovou: a track with adventurous wooden bridges and potholes —
you’d have wanted a 4WD for it.

At first it was still quite amusing, because the area is very
traditional and picturesque and we were greeted very warmly in all the
villages. After dark, however, the whole thing became pretty adventurous
and exhausting. The track seemed to have no end, and we even managed to
get lost because it was completely dark and we couldn’t see any signs,
and our petrol was also starting to run low.

But around 10 p.m. we finally reached the Tailevu Hotel in Korovou,
where we got a roomy but somewhat neglected room for \$60. We didn’t
care in the slightest — all we wanted was a bed and something to eat.
The hotel food was cheap and excellent, and they even brought it to our
room, together with a cold bottle of beer. I don’t think I’ve ever
enjoyed a beer that much.

After that we collapsed into bed and slept for eleven hours straight.

Today we had to go back to Suva to return the car. Luckily the road here
was paved again, and we were back at the Raintree Lodge in less than 45
minutes, where we checked in for two days — this time not in the dorm,
but in a pretty little double room with a balcony by the lake (\$50).

## 25 Nov 2004 — Levuka (Ovalau)

We spent another two nights at the Raintree Lodge and didn’t even make
it to the nature reserve on the other side of the road. One day
disappeared in the hammock with a book, and on the last day there was
only time for a bit of culture in Suva. We visited the Fiji Museum with
its interesting cannibalism section, drank cappuccino, and booked a
ticket to Levuka for the next day.

The trip to Levuka took five hours: first by bus to Natovi, then a
fairly spectacular 45-minute ferry crossing, followed by another bus
ride on the island of Ovalau.

Levuka is Fiji’s old capital, with an unbelievably relaxed atmosphere,
colonial-style wooden houses, and a wonderful location by the sea
beneath jungle-covered hills. It’s one of those places where time seems
to stand still, and we stayed a few days longer than planned.

There were several reasons for this. For one thing, you can stay
incredibly cheaply in a wonderfully atmospheric colonial hotel, the
Royal — long past its best days, but still almost unbeatable value for
money. Our double room, simple but stylish, cost under \$30. The pool —
which we usually had to ourselves — was absolutely brilliant, set in a
garden with views of the green mountains inland, and the bar and lounges
radiated the charm of a long-gone era. I loved this place.

Another reason we stayed so long was simply that we couldn’t leave.
Bobo’s Farm in the north of Ovalau was fully booked, and the inland tour
we wanted to do didn’t run again until the following week because of
deaths in the village.

So we spent a few cosy days in Levuka, swam in the pool, and enjoyed the
good food in the town’s two restaurants — the pretty Whale’s Tale and
the Chinese‑influenced Kim’s.

There is no nightlife in Levuka; even the hotel bar closed at 9:30.
Still, on Friday we managed to stay out until half past twelve and had a
wonderful evening in the colonial Ovalau Club with lots of beer and
interesting conversations with the club chairman, his wife and a few
local regulars. Another great experience — and a cultural insight you
only get as an open-minded independent traveller.

On Monday we also did the Highland Tour with Epi, a local from the
inland village of Lovoni. It was a nice hike through beautiful scenery
to the village, and at the same time an insight into the island’s
history and the medicinal properties of jungle plants. Epi is a great
storyteller, even if probably only half of what he says is even remotely
well founded.

In the village we got a tasty traditional lunch — taro, cassava, tuna —
and we visited the chief’s house. The chief wasn’t at home, but his used
underwear was lying scattered around the room. A very nice day for \$30.

On Tuesday afternoon we took a small speedboat (\$20) to the coral
island of Caqelai, just off Ovalau — a little paradise and the
fulfilment of South Seas clichés. The island is tiny, you can walk
around it in 15 minutes: white beaches with coconut palms, reefs
offshore, wonderful snorkelling with intact coral and brightly coloured
fish.

The resort here is very basic — no running water, no electricity, none
of that unnecessary luxury. Just a few simple bures under palms, little
toilet huts, bucket showers and a common room where three meals a day
are served. In the evening there’s guitar, singing, dancing and kava.

The people working here are all very nice, too, and you don’t feel like
a tourist being fed an entertainment programme. It’s more like being a
guest spending an authentic evening with locals.

A wonderful place to relax and forget. We paid \$45 per person for the
bure and three meals — a fair price for the right to spend a few days in
paradise.

## 3 Dec 2004 — Fiji (end)

Today is our last day in Fiji. The last week wasn’t particularly
eventful because Elaine got sick shortly after we left our paradise and
headed back towards Viti Levu. We spent two nights at the Casablanca on
the Coral Coast, mostly in the room, since the weather wasn’t great
either. On Saturday it rained for the first time after three weeks of
dream weather.

Our plan was to spend the last few days on an island in the Mamanucas,
so we spent another night in Nadi — this time at the Hotel Kennedy. Not
the most attractive hotel on Queens Road, but the rooms (\$50) were
excellent: a huge bathroom with a jacuzzi (!) and 24-hour room service
at no extra charge, which was very convenient given Elaine’s condition.

We went with the cheapest island option, Mana Island — a one-hour boat
ride from Nadi — a mistake, as it turned out. The boat ride (\$70
return) was very beautiful; some of the islands we passed were the
ultimate South Seas dream.

But our resort on Mana was an absolute dump with zero character, full of
party backpackers. The rooms were grubby and too expensive (\$45 per
person including food). The island itself is really quite pretty. I
explored it thoroughly with two Germans who arrived on the boat with us,
and some of the beaches were gorgeous; the views of the surrounding
islands absolutely grand.

We still went back the very next day, because it didn’t make much sense
to spend the last days in Fiji in a resort we both hated. And apart from
beaches and views, the island had nothing else to offer. The village was
unattractive, and all the hills had been cleared.

This part of Fiji — Nadi and the backpacker resorts on the surrounding
islands — is, despite all the natural beauty, the least attractive area
in the country: too touristy, too loud, and everything is overpriced.
The worst part is that most backpackers only ever see this part of Fiji.

I’m so glad we saw so much more, and I regret a bit that we didn’t stay
longer in Caqelai, or do the trip to Taveuni.

Since Elaine’s health hadn’t improved much — which made travelling
impossible — we spent another two days in Nadi, in a nice mid-range room
(\$75) at the West Motor Inn, and enjoyed a bit of luxury: the pool and
the good food in the restaurant.

Every day I walked into town to shop and discovered The Chef’s
Restaurant, where I had lunch twice. The “Chef’s” is one of the smartest
restaurants in town, but the prices were very fair and the food
absolutely delicious. For \$10.50 I got a mixed plate (Mezze of 8) with
raw tuna, aubergine, tabbouleh, hummus, tender chicken kebabs and kofta
— a real gourmet meal in a stylish setting, and the best thing Nadi has
to offer touristically.

For the last night we switched hotels again because we wanted to spend
an evening with Abi, a Scot from Eva’s. She had booked a package tour
and — like all package-backpackers before they are shipped off to the
islands — was staying at the Nadi Bay Resort.

The hotel itself is quite nice, with two pools, good food and clean
rooms. Still, the whole thing is basically a rip-off designed to take
money from inexperienced backpackers. Drinks and food were overpriced,
as was the room — \$56 without a private bathroom — especially if you
compare it with the less backpacker-oriented hotels nearby.

And the atmosphere didn’t do much for me either. Package-backpackers
generally aren’t my favourite people: they think they’re especially cool
and individual because they have a backpack and are far away from home,
but basically they don’t do anything different from your average
Mallorca package tourist.

## 15 Dec 2004 — Back in Sydney → Perth

The plan after returning to Sydney was to find a job and a flat within
the next two weeks — which turned out to be harder than expected, even
though we really made an effort. We went through letting agencies and
cafés, read the newspaper ads and made phone calls. The flats we looked
at were overpriced dives; all the good ones are only rented out for six
months or more.

Jobs weren’t going well either, because basically every backpacker in
Australia is in Sydney over New Year’s, so there weren’t many jobs
going. I did a trial shift at the Löwenbräu Keller — an absolute
nightmare. I had to wear lederhosen and was responsible for 150 tables;
it was like a factory and everyone was unfriendly. I worked one day on a
building site for \$100 cash-in-hand, which was okay despite the
exhausting nine-hour shift.

Life at Eva’s was mostly quiet. Some of the “original crew” were still
around, and now and then we went out or did a BBQ, but it wasn’t
comparable to the first stay and I wasn’t exactly sad to leave.

On Saturday we finally bailed and booked a flight to Perth (\$269). It
was the most sensible decision: we were pretty broke, Christmas was
coming up, the hostels in Sydney had been fully booked for months, and
there were no jobs.

Since we were bored and frustrated with Sydney anyway, Perth seemed like
the perfect alternative — without all the NYE hype and the packed
hostels. And it would be stupid to spend the whole Australia stay in the
same city. You can look for work somewhere else just as well, and you
get to see another side of Australia too.

On Tuesday we flew to Perth — 4h30 and still the same country — and took
the shuttle to Fremantle, a beach suburb of Perth, just 25 minutes south
by train, and our home for the next few weeks. That was yesterday, and
although I haven’t seen that much of the place yet, I’m sure I’m going
to like it here. It’s a wonderfully relaxed little town with a historic
centre, the “Cappuccino Strip” with countless street cafés, and glorious
summer weather — 41 degrees yesterday. A welcome change from Sydney’s
weather, where it rained almost every day.

## Fremantle (Dec 2004)

We booked a week at Sundancer Backpackers in the centre of Freo. A
beautiful historic building that used to be a real hotel, with
atmospheric rooms, a big kitchen, a cheap bar and a jacuzzi in the back
courtyard. Unfortunately the shared bathrooms were very basic and not
particularly clean, and the dorms had no shelves, which led to chaotic
mess. On the other hand, the week here only cost \$112, whereas at Eva’s
we paid \$25 a night.

Our first evening in Freo was perfect. We had a cappuccino on the Strip,
Elaine cooked a delicious lamb roast, and afterwards we sat in the
jacuzzi under the starry sky with a glass of wine. What more do you
want?

## 19 Dec 2004 — Fremantle / Perth

On the second day we went into Perth to meet Chris, an old acquaintance
from Eva’s — and like every evening (or in this case afternoon) with
Chris, it ended in a drinking session. We drank rounds of pints in the
nice beer garden at the Brass Monkey in Northbridge, followed by a
bottle of wine at sunset on Freo beach. Intoxicated, Elaine and I got it
on in the bunk bed of a fully occupied eight-bed dorm — worse still:
seven women and me — and luckily there were no complaints.

The next day I got a text from Annet asking whether I wanted to meet her
in Perth. So I got on the train and we spent a relaxed day sightseeing
in Perth — a city which, despite lacking standout landmarks, is
extremely attractive and laid-back. Even with 1.4 million inhabitants it
is anything but a hectic metropolis; rather, it really is the most
remote big city in the world — which in itself is an attraction.

A large part of the centre is pedestrian-only, which I liked a lot and
which also helps to disguise the lack of historic buildings. We took the
ferry across the Swan River, where we had a cappuccino with wonderful
views over the river and Perth’s skyline.

Back on the other side we took the bus — and inner-city routes are free
in Perth, how brilliant is that? — to King’s Park on a hill west of the
centre. This wonderful large park had the smell of the Mediterranean and
with green lawns to lie on, also offers great views of the city and the
surroundings.

I’m very glad we decided to turn our backs on Sydney. Much as I like the
city, everything here is far more relaxed and cosy.

Over the next few days I didn’t really leave Freo. I worked one day on a
building site for \$100 and was so exhausted that I fell into bed dead.
On Saturday we strolled around Freo’s pretty historic market, bought
fruit (you can get magnificent mangoes here for throwaway prices) and
drank cappuccino.

Freo is simply wonderful — a mix of European and Australian flair, a
feel-good little town with a fantastic climate and high quality of life,
at least if you have money. Finding a job isn’t that easy here either,
and I also have to look after Elaine, who is sick again. But as long as
I can work on building sites now and then, the financial situation isn’t
quite so acute.

## 22 Dec 2004 — Fremantle

After a week we moved into a house outside the centre of Fremantle,
which we shared with the owner and other backpackers. It’s nice and
quiet here, and we have our own spacious room in the garden. Much more
pleasant than the messy dorm at the Sundancer, and hardly more expensive
(\$140 per person per week).

We had a small garden party on the first evening, and, cliché as it
sounds, every conversation revolved around surfing, sharks and
crocodiles. Very amusing — Australians really are special.

The last few days weren’t particularly eventful; the only thing worth
mentioning is a hospital visit, which was an impressive demonstration of
the shortcomings of the Australian health system. We spent hours in the
waiting room of the “Emergency Department”(!) just to see a GP. Around
us were people who were genuinely in a terrible state, writhing in pain,
collapsing, unable to sit… but nobody really seemed to care — neither
the nurses nor the other patients. If you’re not literally on the
threshold of death, you have to wait to be treated — hours in an
uncomfortable hall — and nobody seems to think much about whether it’s
right; it’s simply accepted. Strange customs.

Today I rented a bike and cycled up the coast to Cottesloe Beach. There
are lots of good bike paths in the region, but it’s usually very windy.
“Cotto” is a beautiful wide beach with white sand and blue water. Above
the coast is a picturesque historic tea house that contains a café and a
stylish restaurant. It’s no surprise this is one of Perth’s most popular
beaches.

## 31 Dec 2004 — Perth / Fremantle

The last days of the year were perfect — glorious, hot weather over
Christmas, cinema visits and a few trips into Perth, everything very
relaxed and harmonious. On Christmas Eve we started drinking cold beer
in the garden in front of our room early in the afternoon, got properly
drunk, listened to good music and slept with each other. Christmas
doesn’t get better than that!

On Boxing Day we went to Subiaco, one of Perth’s most attractive
neighbourhoods, with some pretty cafés and a great, cheap market. In the
middle of the market grounds there’s a big shady courtyard, surrounded
by food and drink stalls and even a small stage with live jazz — not a
bad place to spend the afternoon.

The next two days, because of the heat, we went to the cinema at midday
(The Motorcycle Diaries and I Heart Huckabees, both excellent films),
and afterwards for a cappuccino at Toscanini — anything else would have
been too exhausting.

On the 29th we made another trip into Perth, first for a cappuccino on
Oxford Street in Leederville, one of Perth’s trendiest neighbourhoods.
Here it’s one café after the next, but the most atmospheric — very
bohemian — was the 130: excellent cappuccino and sinfully good
banana–pecan–ricotta cake.

We walked from Leederville to Northbridge, Perth’s backpacker and
nightlife district, and went for a beer at the Brass Monkey. Northbridge
is an insanely attractive neighbourhood — I can’t wait to spend a few
nights here. In the afternoon it’s very relaxed, there are countless
good-looking restaurants, cafés and pubs, and despite its wild
reputation (90 arrests for antisocial behaviour on an ordinary Friday),
the area is very well kept and pretty to look at.

## 7 Jan 2005 — Perth / Fremantle

Elaine is gone, and saying goodbye was a lot harder than I’d expected.
The last two days were very strange. Even though we did plenty, the mood
was heavy because both of us had it in the back of our minds that it
would be over soon. Three months is a long time — especially when you’re
travelling and together every day. Elaine cried every day for the last
three days, and I feel oddly empty, as if I have to start from scratch
again here.

We spent the last two nights in Northbridge at the Britannia Hostel — a
bad choice: big and impersonal, and overpriced for what you get. We paid
\$66 for a small, spartan double room with an uncomfortable bed, and the
bathrooms were more like a mediocre campsite. We'd wanted to go out
properly on Tuesday, but Northbridge isn’t King’s Cross, and during the
week there isn’t all that much happening here. So we got drunk in the
room and listened to music until someone told us to please behave
decently because other guests had complained. What a prissy hostel.

On Wednesday we walked to Lake Monger to admire the black swans —
Perth’s emblem. On the way we had cappuccinos in Leederville. A very
nice little outing in glorious weather. In the evening we did a tour to
the Swan Valley, a wine region east of Perth. We visited three wineries,
did tastings, got cheese and olives, and had an excellent dinner at the
beautiful restaurant of Black Swan Winery. When we were already nicely
drunk we went for one more pint in the beer garden of the oldest pub in
WA.

What I liked about the tour was how unpretentious and relaxed the
tastings were — Aussie style. The wine from this region isn’t really my
thing, though: mostly fruity, sometimes sweet white wine, and even the
Shiraz here is only so-so. And for \$75 the tour was a bit too
expensive.

The last days in Fremantle were very nice. On New Year’s Eve we went to
the Mexican place on the Strip, had a huge, tasty meal with a jug of
sangria, and then went to the park with a box of Shiraz. The following
days were very hot. We went to the cinema in the afternoons, drank
cappuccino, and did a trip to the wonderfully white beach of Scarborough
— where we got blown away by the wind and therefore spent most of the
afternoon in a pretty pub with live music.

We hardly ever went out at night, because in the extended garage of the
backpacker home we had our own little kingdom: we could sit outside the
room, play our own music, which was cheaper and nicer than running to
the pub every night. It’s strange how neither of us felt any real need
to go out and meet other people. I never would have thought I could live
with someone for that long without being afraid of missing out or
getting bored.

Yesterday I checked into the One World Hostel in Northbridge. It feels
like a restart — sociable hostel life and dorms again after all that
time with privacy. Luckily the hostel is genuinely pleasant: new, very
clean, and in my six‑bed dorm only two beds are taken. The people here
seem very nice. So last night I was sitting in the courtyard again,
drinking beer and having backpacker conversations. Despite the
adjustment — and even though I miss Elaine — I don’t feel uncomfortable
here.

## 10 Jan 2005 — Perth / Northbridge

Already my second day at the hostel ended with me absolutely drunk at
five in the morning after a long pub crawl in Northbridge. Even though
One World isn’t a party hostel, you can always find people to go out
with. The urge to get drunk and have fun is perhaps the one thing that
unites all backpackers in Australia most.

It was Friday night and Northbridge was properly alive: a mild summer
night, the street cafés and restaurants were packed, a wonderful evening
atmosphere. We started at Novak’s Inn, a pub with a rock’n’roll live
band. After a pint we moved on to the Elephant & Wheelbarrow, a huge
English pub with a dancefloor. Here, too, a live band played party
classics, and you could either sit outside or dance inside.

Our third stop was The Deen, a mix of pub and club — also enormous, with
several floors and a funky live band. We ended the night at the Bog, a
slightly shabby but very popular Irish pub after 3 a.m., open every day
until 6 — even the band played until 4.

I was out with Andrew, a nice English guy from Scunthorpe, and two girls
from the hostel. Although going out in Perth is generally quite a bit
more expensive than in Sydney, the night was pretty cheap for me because
I only had to buy one round. You can say what you like about the
English, but they’re fantastic to go out with because they’re always
ready to buy rounds. Needless to say, the next day was more or less a
write‑off. I slept until late and fought a hangover for the rest of the
day.

On Sunday I worked ten hours for the Greek community in Northbridge, who
were holding their annual festival in the park. A very pleasant job: set
up a few tables and chairs, grill a bit of souvlaki… very relaxed for
\$120 and free food. In the evening I met Dave, who drives pedicabs here
in Northbridge, and he introduced me to the quirks of the rickshaws and
the general pitfalls of the job. It took me about 20 minutes before the
bike did what I wanted, but I think being a rickshaw driver could be a
lot of fun. We’ll see — my first shift is on Wednesday.

This morning I worked a few more hours for the Greeks, took care of
laundry, and slept the rest of the day. Working and sleeping will be the
two activities that dominate my life over the next few weeks. I’m too
broke to go out — my Visa card is maxed out and my bank card has
expired… I have to earn money. There’s no way around it.

## 7 Feb 2005 — Perth → Melbourne

I arrived at One World in a state of emptiness — completely broke and
alone — and now I’m sitting at the airport on my way to Melbourne and
I’m genuinely sad to leave Perth. The last month was wonderful and very
educational.

For the first ten days I didn’t even have money to buy food, so I had to
live exclusively off the not‑exactly‑uplifting contents of the free-food
shelf, and I was hardly able to take part in the hostel’s social life.
During that time I read a lot, slept a lot, and worked to pay rent and
tobacco.

My job was great — unusual and exciting. I didn’t earn all that much at
first, but who can say they’ve been a rickshaw driver in Perth? It’s
hard work, but I wouldn’t want to miss a single minute. Every night
funny things happened: drunk girls stripping on the back seat or trying
to pay with a blowjob, people paying me \$10 just to go across the
street, and during the waiting times I could watch the wild nightlife of
Northbridge and Fremantle.

On Australia Day I worked fourteen hours straight. The city of Perth had
spared no effort to celebrate, and half a million people watched a
magnificent 45‑minute fireworks show over the river — and I was right in
the middle of it all as a rickshaw driver.

Once my credit card was working again, I could also take an active part
in hostel life and enjoyed my free evenings to the full. Especially the
last two weeks we had a great group. We did day trips, or went for
cappuccino at Vulture’s, cooked together almost every day, went out
properly in the evenings or played cards all night, drank and listened
to music. The last few days I never got to bed before sunrise.

It was almost like the beginning at Eva’s again. I felt unbelievably
comfortable in the hostel — but at least I had a fixed date when I had
to leave Perth. Otherwise I could easily have imagined sinking into it
again, because I really had very nice people around me. It was genuinely
hard to say goodbye today.

I spent the afternoon with Steffi, a Swiss girl I got on brilliantly
with, in Kings Park. Before I had to leave, Will — Dutch and basically
part of the furniture at OWB, and an all‑round great person — and Steffi
decided to play a prank on me. Everyone in the hostel knew about it
except me: they cancelled the shuttle bus to the airport that I’d
booked.

After I waited and waited, I decided to call a taxi — but Sally, the
night manager, was in on it too, so of course no taxi came either. When
I’d almost accepted that I was going to miss the flight and opened my
third beer, Chris suddenly drove up in his car. Steffi and Will had
organised the whole thing and accompanied me to the airport to say
goodbye. I was really touched, and I won’t forget my time in Perth and
the people from OWB anytime soon.

## 9 Feb 2005 — Melbourne

When I arrived in Melbourne early in the morning, in the dark, it was
raining. I took the airport bus, the Skybus (\$13), into the city. Dawn
was grey, wet and cold; I had to run through the rain to the tram stop,
froze miserably, and cursed Melbourne.

When my hostel — the much‑praised “Nunnery” — also turned out to be
absurdly overpriced (A\$25 for a ten‑bed dorm with one (!) bathroom for
everyone), all I wanted was to go back to Perth. But since that was
unrealistic, and it was still pouring, I lay down for a bit — and when I
woke up it was five in the afternoon, the sun was shining, and the world
looked a lot better.

The hostel wasn't too bad: the dorm was fairly clean and spacious, and
the interior had atmosphere. Still, after OWB — especially for that kind
of money — it was a disappointment. The kitchen was small and messy, and
there was neither a nice courtyard nor a rooftop terrace.

I took a long walk through Fitzroy and Carlton, two of Melbourne’s most
popular going‑out districts, and, against all odds, fell in love with
the city immediately. Fitzroy alone has such an abundance of cafés and
bars — bohemian, stylish, cosy — wine bars, pubs, cocktail bars,
restaurants of every kind… I could hardly believe my eyes. Everything
I’d missed, as a European, in terms of going‑out culture in Sydney and
Perth, Melbourne has in abundance. At Volare on Brunswick Street I had
the best cappuccino I’d had in weeks, and afterwards I went for a
delicious malai kofte (A\$9.50) at Fitz Curry, which, despite its modest
interior and low prices, is apparently the first Indian restaurant in
Australia to offer organic food.

I also liked Carlton a lot, even if it doesn’t have Fitzroy’s slightly
run‑down charm. Lygon Street is Melbourne’s Italian quarter: one Italian
restaurant after another, all with outdoor tables, and almost all of
them busy even though it was a Tuesday. Melburnians definitely have a
Southern‑European style going‑out culture, which I really like. At night
I took another walk through downtown and, to my surprise, realised that
Melbourne’s CBD doesn’t empty out after dark — there are plenty of
places to go out here, too.

Today was exhausting. Since I’m not planning to spend several weeks in
Melbourne but am here as a tourist, I’d given myself a strict
sightseeing programme. I bought a day ticket (A\$5.90) for Melbourne’s
excellent public transport and took the tram to St Kilda, the seaside
suburb about 8 km south of downtown. The beach itself isn’t anything
special — but who comes to Melbourne for the beach? — yet there are
cafés and restaurants everywhere, plus nice views of the coast and the
CBD skyline from the pier. Since the nightlife is supposed to be very
good here and many backpackers base themselves in St Kilda, I might
spend a few nights here before I fly back.

I had a very good cappuccino at the cosy Veludo on the café strip, then
took tram 16 back towards the centre. Halfway there I got off to visit
Prahran and South Yarra, two more attractive inner‑city districts where
you could easily spend a whole day shopping, eating and drinking, given
all the options. If only one had the money.

I walked kilometres back towards the city through the beautiful Botanic
Gardens — one of the nicest city parks I’ve ever seen — to Southgate, an
impressive shopping and entertainment mecca with a promenade on the
south bank of the Yarra. The views across the river to the skyline and
Federation Square, a masterpiece of modern architecture, are
breathtaking, and for the first time I found myself thinking that
Melbourne isn’t just attractive, cultured and liveable — it's genuinely
beautiful. Not as spectacular as Sydney, but beautiful in its own way.

Since I was at Flinders Street Station anyway, I decided to do the
“Walking Tour” described in Lonely Planet, which starts there. Even
though I usually prefer exploring cities on my own, without a fixed
plan, this was an excellent decision: some of the narrow laneways
mentioned would probably have remained sheer luck for me — and they’re
easily an absolute highlight, especially Degraves Street and Block
Place.

I could hardly believe my eyes: right in the middle of the modern
high‑rise canyons of the CBD, these tight little lanes with pretty
street cafés and restaurants — more like a Southern‑European old town
than Australia, and with far more charm than Starbucks & Co.

I finished the walk with a yum‑cha lunch in Chinatown. For A\$14.50 I
got spring roll, wontons, soup, fried rice with shrimp, and mixed dim
sum. The owner of the Post Mao Café supposedly cooked the Chairman’s
favourite dishes personally — whether that’s true or not, the food was
very good.

## 13 Feb 2005 — Melbourne

On Thursday morning I picked Mona up from the airport bus terminal and
we moved into the Hotel Bakpak. The day before I’d booked a twin room
there — why, I honestly don’t know — because it has to be the worst dive
in Australia: a huge run‑down hostel full of loud, brainless party
backpackers; completely overpriced, foul‑smelling rooms (A\$75); and
bathrooms like an Eastern‑European campsite. Unbelievable that this dump
is insanely popular — it must be the two bars, and the fact that there
don’t seem to be any rules of behaviour.

In the afternoon we did a crash tour of Melbourne: cappuccino in Carlton
and at Segovia in Block Place, a walk along the Southgate river
promenade, and Fitzroy’s Brunswick Street, where we had an aperitif in
the wonderfully atmospheric, cosy Bar With No Name. Afterwards we had an
excellent dinner at Afghan Village — especially the mixed starters
(A\$13), which were really delicious.

On Friday we had breakfast under the arcades of the Victoria Market and
then took the City Circle Tram, a lovely old tram that’s free and runs a
fixed loop around the city centre. We got off first at the Docklands. A
modern entertainment complex on the water is being built here —
Melbourne’s version of Darling Harbour. Once it’s finished it could
become an attractive place to go out, but at the moment what’s already
done still feels very sterile and underused.

We continued on the City Circle Tram as far as Parliament and went for
lunch at Ants in Chinatown — an excellent choice: a chic, appealing
little bistro with fresh Szechuan food and tasty dim sum; the Tasty
Chicken was also great. Very cheap lunch specials — highly recommended.

In the evening we had a few pints in the pub of the James Squire
Brewery. The beer is very expensive (A\$7.60 a pint), but the Amber Ale
really is something special — absolutely delicious.

On Saturday we went to the Victoria Market again to shop for a picnic.
The market in the historic halls is enormous and is considered the best
in Australia. I’m not surprised — you can get almost everything: deli
stuff, fish, vegetables, fruit, clothes, souvenirs — and at very
reasonable prices. We bought ciabatta, olives, stuffed chillies and
cheese, took the tram to St Kilda, and from there the weekend ferry from
the pier to Williamstown (A\$6.50). With stormy, cold weather it was a
pretty wobbly affair — more like a winter day on the Mediterranean than
the Australian high summer — but the views across the bay to Melbourne’s
skyline were very appealing, and nobody got seasick.

Williamstown is a well‑preserved 19th‑century harbour town that reminded
me a bit of Fremantle. As a suburb of Melbourne it’s a popular day trip
destination, full of pretty cafés and restaurants. But we’d brought
provisions, so we had our picnic and watched black swans and pelicans
foraging for food.

Back in the city we took the train to Flinders Street Station and — now
in sunshine — had a glass of Chardonnay at the stylish Arintji on
Federation Square. Then we went on a long walk through the Botanic
Gardens and back into town along the river. We finished the day
relatively early with a cappuccino in Block Place, my favourite lane in
Melbourne.

Today, as every day, we started with a delicious croissant and
cappuccino under the arcades of the Victoria Market and took the train
to South Yarra, where we strolled along Chapel Street, visited the
Prahran Market (small and nice, but rather expensive), and bought a few
bits and pieces at Aldi. Hard to believe, but the Aldis in Australia
are, in terms of look and layout, a one‑to‑one copy of the German
originals.

Today was the final day of the St Kilda Festival, Melbourne’s biggest
street festival, and of course we weren’t going to miss it. For the
first time: shiny summer weather. Huge crowds pushed through the streets
and along St Kilda’s beach promenade; there were markets, food stalls,
concert stages, street performers — the whole package.

I’m sure it would have been very interesting in the evening too, but we
were flying to Launceston at 9 p.m. So after a delicious Vietnamese
lunch at Café Dang on Fitzroy Street (spring rolls, rice‑paper rolls,
beef in vine leaves — all A\$6.50; vermicelli with lemongrass and fat
king prawns, A\$9), we went back into the city. One last cappuccino at
Segovia — and then it was time to say goodbye, for now, to Australia’s
most cultured and stylish metropolis.

Three days earlier we’d found such a cheap deal for flights to Tasmania
(A\$65 with Jetstar) that we grabbed it on the spot, and we’ll be
spending the next two weeks on this enigmatic island.

## 14 Feb 2005 — Launceston (Tasmania)

The flight to Launceston only took 45 minutes and since we didn’t arrive
until just before ten, we took a taxi to the Launceston Backpacker
(A\$27). There we discovered they’d messed up our booking and were fully
booked. So at half past ten we were standing there with no room.

Luckily they were understanding and admitted it was their mistake, so
they not only found us a room somewhere else and drove us there, but
also paid us the price difference — A\$21.

Thanks to this blunder we ended up at Lloyd’s Hotel and I stayed in an
Australian “hotel” for the first time (which here, as is well known,
usually means a pub that also rents out rooms). We got a small but clean
twin with fridge, private bathroom and TV. The actual price would have
been A\$65, but with the A\$21 from Launceston Backpackers this little
bit of luxury only cost us A\$44.

In the morning we checked in at the Launceston Backpackers — in terms of
value for money the best hostel in Australia so far. A very spacious,
bright twin room costs only A\$22 per person; the bathrooms are fine;
there’s a big kitchen; and everything is quite clean.

We spent the morning reserving a rental car for the next day and buying
camping equipment, which wasn’t a problem in Launceston.

In the afternoon we took a walk through Cataract Gorge, Launie’s main
attraction. There are trails on both sides of the impressive river
canyon near the centre: an easy stroll on the north side, and a steeper,
more strenuous path on the south. The views are beautiful; there’s a
restaurant, a suspension bridge over the river, and a free swimming pool
with lawns for sunbathing. Well worth it.

We spent the rest of the day in town and had pints of the local James
Boag beer at the nice Cock n Bull English pub. Launceston is a pretty
attractive little town with lots of historic buildings, enough good
cafés and restaurants, and an almost too‑relaxed atmosphere — after five
o’clock the pavements are rolled up; not a place for wild nightlife.

## 15 Feb 2005 — Tasmania: North Coast to Rocky Cape

In the morning we picked up our rental car and left Launie on small back
roads — some of them unsealed — heading west to Liffey Falls. The day
before we’d picked up the excellent free brochure “60 Great Short Walks
in Tasmania” and we’ll do quite a few of them during our stay.

The walk to Liffey Falls was short (45 minutes) but beautiful, through
fairy‑tale rainforest — and the waterfall itself was very picturesque.

After that we drove up onto the central plateau and took a short walk at
Pine Lake in this harsh, unreal landscape. The rare, ancient pencil
pines — found only in Tasmania — stand there like skeletons.

On the way to the north coast we stopped for lunch in Deloraine, a
pleasant little town by a river, and had a tasty Tasmanian cheese
platter (a Ploughman’s Lunch for A\$11.95) at the deli of the same name,
and a cappuccino.

We then followed the coast, which, at least until after Burnie, wasn’t
all that spectacular and, for Tasmania, fairly heavily populated. Only
the little town of Penguin, with its nice beach and a few cafés, was
worth a stop — we had coffee on the beach road.

It got properly beautiful around Wynyard: a cosy place at a stunning
river mouth, where we ate a seafood basket on the quay.

From there it was one highlight after another: gentle, unspoilt coastal
scenery; dramatic cliffs at Table Cape with wonderful views; the lovely
little bay of Boat Harbour with blazing white sand and crystal‑clear
water; the long white Sisters Beach with lagoon; and more impressive
cliffs at Rocky Cape with a lookout. A fantastic area, and anything but
crowded.

At Rocky Cape National Park I saw my first kangaroo after more than five
months in Australia — so that’s finally ticked off as well.

We spent the night at a small roadside camping ground with pub and
bottle shop near the turn‑off to Rocky Cape: lush green grass, a
barbecue area, and good, clean facilities — for just A\$8.

## 16 Feb 2005 — Stanley to Cradle Mountain

Today was fairly sunny, but cool and extremely windy. We continued along
the beautiful north coast past long white beaches to the historic town
of Stanley, with its neat old houses below the imposing rock “The Nut”,
beautifully set at the end of a peninsula. White sand beaches and
deep‑blue sea on all sides.

First we had coffee and I ate scallops at the lovely Swinging Anchor
Café, in a historic building on the main street. Then we climbed The Nut
— a steep 20‑minute walk; there’s a chairlift too, but the views made
the climb well worth it. We walked the 45‑minute circuit around the
massive rock and went back down into Stanley.

Via Wynyard we then took the scenic A10 inland to Cradle Mountain
National Park, Tasmania’s biggest tourist magnet: massive mountain
scenery, picturesque lakes, dense, fairy‑tale rainforest — no wonder
it’s pretty crowded (A\$50 for a two‑month pass for all Tasmanian
national parks).

We only arrived in the afternoon and up here it was bitterly cold (well
— around 10 degrees). We did two short walks near the visitor centre: to
Pencil Pine Falls and the Enchanted Walk, mostly through rainforest
along a mountain stream.

When we reached the camping ground in the tourist park it started to
rain and the temperature dropped further. I hope I survive the night —
camping in sub‑zero temperatures is anything but fun. The site isn’t
great (forest floor) and is outrageously overpriced (A\$30). On the plus
side, the facilities are high standard and there’s a big communal
kitchen (without equipment, but with a large fireplace that puts out a
nice heat).

## 17 Feb 2005 — Cradle Mountain to Strahan

We took the free shuttle from the visitor centre to Dove Lake and walked
the two‑hour Dove Lake Circuit around the lake. The weather was good and
the views of Cradle Mountain were fantastic. The path mostly ran through
rainforest past inviting little beaches — far too cold for swimming,
though. Still, a beautiful area and a very pleasant walk.

Around midday we drove on towards the west coast through scarcely
populated, mountainous forest country. We had lunch at the Tullah
Tavern, a simple country pub in a godforsaken spot. The food, however,
was excellent, with portions big enough for hungry truckers: a fantastic
salad with fat garlic king prawns (A\$18), and a big rump steak with
scallops and jumbo prawns (reef & beef, A\$22) — genuinely fine cooking
for a place like that.

To walk it off we did a three‑hour hike (return) to Montezuma Falls near
Zeehan. The trail followed an old railway line right through dense,
green rainforest. The walk itself was already lovely; the waterfall was
an absolute highlight: 100 metres high in a dream setting. A narrow,
adventurous suspension bridge spans the deep gorge in front of the
falls, with great views of the forested surroundings and the waterfall.
This hike was more than worth it.

Through pretty green hill country we continued to the coast to Strahan,
the only larger place (800 inhabitants) on Tasmania’s west coast. We
arrived so late that we didn’t even see the town in daylight, but we’ll
probably stay another day.

We snagged the last free pitch at the Strahan Tourist Park, about a
15‑minute walk from the centre, right on the water. Not cheap (A\$20),
but with decent — if slightly small — grassy pitches and good
facilities.

In the evening we wandered into town and I ate half a dozen wonderful
Tasmanian oysters at Hamer’s Hotel, with a glass of (less wonderful)
Tasmanian Chardonnay.

## 18 Feb 2005 — Strahan / Henty Dunes / Gordon River

Even though the weather didn’t exactly invite us to stay, we decided to
spend another day in Strahan — an excellent decision, because the area
has a lot to offer.

In the morning we drove 14 km back towards Zeehan to visit the Henty
Dunes. All that points to this grand dune landscape is a small wooden
sign to a “picnic area” in the forest, and even the Lonely Planet guide
mentions it with only a single sentence. Yet the high sand dunes are
absolutely spectacular, with views over the endless green West Tasmanian
rainforest on one side and the blue ocean on the other. Somewhere else
in the world, these dunes would be a major tourist attraction.

Back in Strahan we did a nice 45‑minute walk through dense rainforest,
which starts right behind the town, to Hogarth Falls.

After an excellent cappuccino at the Banjo Café on the Esplanade, our
six‑hour Gordon River boat tour, which we’d booked, started at 2 p.m.
The trip isn’t cheap (A\$65), but it’s one of the few ways to penetrate
the untouched wilderness of western Tasmania, one of the most pristine
regions on earth and a World Heritage area.

On a fairly luxurious boat we first cruised through Strahan’s huge
harbour — twice the size of Port Jackson — out to open sea. We passed
the narrow harbour entrance, Hell’s Gate, and had wonderful views of
Ocean Beach, a 33‑km‑long white dune beach; very impressive coastal
scenery.

Back in the harbour we stopped at Sarah Island, once one of the most
remote penal colonies of the Empire; there isn’t much left to see,
though.

Shortly afterwards we reached the mouth of the Gordon River, one of
Tasmania’s longest rivers, winding through the wilderness. As far as the
eye can see: green, dense rainforest — no roads, not even hiking trails
penetrate this impenetrable region. We cruised about an hour upriver to
Heritage Landing, where we took a short walk through the rainforest. The
forest itself wasn’t any more spectacular than on walks in less remote
areas, but the real attraction is the feeling of being at the end of the
world, in one of the most isolated places, surrounded by pure nature.

Dinner For dinner they served a good buffet on board, very reasonably
priced at \$12, among other things smoked salmon and brie, plus a beer
to wash it down, and the return trip flew by.

## 19 Feb 2005 — West to East: Queenstown / Mt Field / New Norfolk

We left Tasmania’s wild, rainy west coast and took the only road that
crosses the West Tasmanian wilderness, heading east. Our first coffee
stop was in a café at the railway station in Queenstown, a former
boomtown surrounded by hillsides cleared for mining — an unreal place,
but, despite the drizzle, not unattractive thanks to the many historic
buildings.

At the station we were lucky enough to watch the historic Wilderness
Railway depart — a beautifully restored tourist train that runs through
the rainforest and, for a lot of money (A\$90 one‑way), ferries
pensioner groups to Strahan. Ridiculously overpriced, but quite nice to
look at.

On the long, winding drive through uninhabited countryside towards the
east coast we did a few signposted walks to stretch our legs: first
through rainforest to the very pretty Nelson Falls; a bit further along
we hiked up to Donaghys Lookout (40 minutes), with wonderful views over
the endless wilderness; and we also enjoyed the short rainforest walk
along the Surprise and Franklin Rivers (25 minutes).

We had lunch at the Derwent Bridge Wilderness Hotel, a very nicely
decorated pub and restaurant with a big fireplace and views over the
greenery. Tour groups get fed here too, but the prices were reasonable
and there was nothing wrong with the focaccia stuffed with smoked
venison ham (A\$9.50).

For a digestion walk we went to Lake St Clair, the end point of the
Overland Track in Cradle Mountain National Park. It’s a beautiful spot:
a clear blue mountain lake surrounded by rainforest. We walked to the
very pretty Platypus Bay (45 minutes) with a sandy beach and great
views, although we didn’t see any platypuses in Platypus Bay.

We saved the best walk of the day — and an absolute Tasmanian highlight
— for last. The trail to Russell Falls and Horseshoe Falls (45 minutes)
in Mount Field National Park ran through magical, picture‑book
rainforest so perfect it could have been painted. Both waterfalls were
unbelievably picturesque, and along the way we saw lots of cute
wallabies hopping about. Completely beyond words!

In the soft early‑evening light it was a wonderful drive through the
gently rolling, lovely Derwent River Valley to New Norfolk, a small town
on the river set in beautiful scenery, where we spent the night at the
camping ground: good grassy pitches, decent facilities, reasonably
priced (A\$12) — and, above all, in the perfect spot right by the water.

## 20 Feb 2005 — Richmond / Apsley / Bicheno / Freycinet

We first drove through wine‑growing country to Richmond, Tasmania’s most
historic little town north of Hobart. Here you feel a bit as if you’re
in Europe: old buildings, orchards, geese and ducks by the pretty river
and its picturesque historic bridge… and bagpipe bands were marching
down the streets. We took a break with cappuccino and divine nut cake in
the garden of the Richmond Wine Centre, which, right down to the chairs,
looked like a summer wine garden in southern Germany. Very bizarre, but
still a charming place.

From there we followed the scenic east coast north via Orford and
Swansea to Bicheno. Unfortunately the weather deteriorated dramatically,
and with grey skies and rain we couldn’t really enjoy the scenery.

North of Bicheno we made a short detour inland to Apsley National Park,
which we reached after some time on a very rough track. The weather
improved again and we were able to take a nice walk through wonderfully
fragrant dry forest (rare in Tasmania) to the Apsley Waterhole: a large,
beautifully turquoise pool in the Apsley River, in the middle of
unspoilt nature.

Back on the coast we spent some time in Bicheno, a small holiday town
set in very attractive coastal scenery, with white sandy coves between
reddish rocks.

Then we headed south again to Coles Bay in Freycinet National Park, one
of Tasmania’s most‑praised tourist attractions. On the way we stopped at
the Friendly Beaches. By then the sun was out again, the sky was blue,
and the kilometres long, powdered‑sugar‑white sand beach with
crystal‑clear water and green hinterland looked absolutely wonderful — a
great teaser for the other highlights of the Freycinet Peninsula.

We drove to the camping ground in Coles Bay, the beautifully located
tourist hub of the national park. At Iluka Holiday Park we got a
wonderful pitch on a hill with sea views for A\$18, and dinner in the
tavern in the evening was quite decent too — even if the portions were a
bit stingy: reef & beef (A\$24) and pan‑fried blue‑eye fish of the day
(A\$15).

Before dinner I took a little trip into the national park, enjoyed the
wonderful views from Cape Tourville and walked to pretty little Sleepy
Bay, with its pink fine‑gravel beach between red rocks. On the way back
I stopped briefly at the equally picturesque Honeymoon Bay and ate half
a dozen delicious Tasmanian oysters at the lovely Oystercatcher in Coles
Bay, with views of the national park’s impressive rock formations. They
also serve what’s probably the best cappuccino south of Melbourne.

## 21 Feb 2005 — Freycinet National Park

Glorious summer weather today, and we spent most of the day in the
magnificent Freycinet National Park. We did a long loop: first up to the
fantastic Wineglass Lookout, with views on a Ko Phi Phi level, down to
the dazzling white beach of the same name, where we went for a swim.

Half an hour later, across a narrow neck of the peninsula, we made it to
the almost equally magnificent Hazards Beach. The sand wasn’t dazzling
white — it was more golden — but the calm, crystal-clear water, with a
small island just offshore, was not only incredibly picturesque, it was
perfect for swimming.

The following hour and a half back to the car park along the coast was a
wonderful walk in gorgeous scenery, with views of deep‑blue sea and
white sandy beaches. Freycinet National Park is without a doubt one of
the most beautiful coastal landscapes I’ve seen so far.

In the afternoon we drove back down the lovely east coast — this time in
bright sunshine — to Richmond, where we stayed overnight at a nice small
camping ground with very good facilities (A\$16). On the way we’d bought
rump steak and wallaby for a barbecue, which was a good idea because in
Richmond everything was already shut by 6 p.m. — and anyway, a proper
barbie at the campsite is simply part of the Aussie lifestyle.

## 22 Feb 2005 — Tasman Peninsula / Port Arthur area

We left our tent up at the campsite in Richmond today and, after a
smoked‑salmon pie and cappuccino at the Wine Centre, drove to the Tasman
Peninsula, another stunning coastal region with impressive scenery,
which we explored in depth.

Our first destination was the white sand of Fortescue Bay at the end of
a 12‑km gravel road. What I liked most there was the lagoon, with
wonderfully green water surrounded by dense forest.

Our next stop was Port Arthur. This very well‑preserved former convict
settlement, set in beautiful scenery, is considered one of Tasmania’s
biggest tourist attractions. We were content with the view from the
lookout, though, because the A\$24 entrance fee struck us as a bit much.

Instead we drove along the pretty coastline to the end of the road,
where Remarkable Cave was a real highlight: a rock opening shaped like
Tasmania in a beautiful bay. Truly remarkable!

We also drove around the rest of the peninsula — White Beach, Nubeena:
wonderful scenery, green meadows, forests, lonely beaches — and at
Eaglehawk Neck walked to the various viewpoints along the imposing wild
cliffs on the peninsula’s eastern side.

On the way back to the campsite we bought fresh seafood for a barbecue:
snapper and trevally (the local fish), plus a whole big lobster at the
bargain price of A\$8. Delicious.

## 23 Feb 2005 — Huon Valley / Cockle Creek / Snug

After a cappuccino at Maldini on Salamanca Square in Hobart we drove up
to the Mt Nelson Lookout high above the city to enjoy the view, then
continued through the beautiful Huon River Valley — with its orchards,
vineyards and historic little towns, like the charming Franklin —
towards the south coast.

Near Geeveston we took a detour into the Southern Forests and did a
short walk at Keoghs Creek. The main goal today, however, was Cockle
Creek at the end of the southernmost road in Australia. The last 20 km
were dirt road, but the trip down was absolutely worth it: wonderful
white sandy bays in beautiful coastal scenery with a lagoon — a remote,
unspoilt area with something magical about it.

We had a picnic and a swim before returning to civilisation — which in
Tasmania isn’t exactly loud or hectic anyway. The entire drive — first
along the Huon River, then along the coast almost to Hobart — was
gorgeous: great views, hardly any settlements.

We made just one stop in the nice historic little town of Cygnet, on a
picturesque bay, where we had cappuccino and delicious chocolate cake at
the Red Velvet Lounge, a funky, alternative eco‑café.

Our campsite in Snug, south of Hobart, was the best we’d had so far: a
grassy pitch right by the sea with views of Bruny Island, a cosy kitchen
where we could grill lamb fillet, and excellent modern facilities — all
for A\$16.

## 24 Feb 2005 — Bruny Island

As our last stop before Hobart we did a day trip to Bruny Island, which
we could already see from our campsite. The ferry crossing took only 15
minutes and cost A\$25 return.

Despite being so close to Hobart, Bruny is still considered something of
an insider tip: sparsely populated, beautifully scenic, with long white
sandy beaches, lovely bays, wooded hills, and that “end of the world”
landscape around the windy Cape Bruny lighthouse.

Most of the island’s roads are unsealed, but easy enough to drive, and
we drove almost all of them. After a delicious lunch at the wonderfully
pretty Penguin Café in Adventure Bay we hiked up to the 300‑metre Fluted
Cape (2 hours), and in the afternoon we went swimming at the
kilometres‑long beach on the narrow strip of land that connects North
and South Bruny.

In the evening we did another gourmet barbecue at the campsite. Tasmania
is truly blessed with fresh produce of the highest quality: we bought
fruit (strawberries, cherries, apricots), a piece of fish fillet, and
lots of scallops — not only delicious here, but also abundant and
therefore cheap.

## 26 Feb 2005 — Hobart

We spent the last two days in Hobart, Tasmania’s small, relaxed,
beautifully located capital, which has plenty to offer: lots of
well‑preserved historic buildings (even in the CBD); the cafés on
Salamanca Place with its colonial sandstone buildings; Battery Point,
the oldest part of Hobart — a genuinely picturesque village of
wonderfully photogenic terraced houses, cafés, restaurants and a quiet
atmosphere.

And from almost everywhere in the city you can see the majestic
1,300‑metre Mount Wellington towering above it all; from the summit you
get impressive views over Hobart and Tasmania’s entire south‑east coast.
Also well worth doing was a harbour cruise on an old tourist boat (A\$17
for 90 minutes) for a different perspective.

Every Saturday Hobart also has what’s arguably the biggest outdoor
market for a city this size: the fantastic Salamanca Market, with
hundreds of stalls where you can buy pretty much anything, from koala
plush toys to German currywurst.

Hobart has a lot to offer food‑wise too: countless seafood restaurants
and stalls at the harbour, and a big choice of ethnic food, especially
on the restaurant strip in North Hobart. On the first day we ate at
Curry & Tandoor House, a very authentic, cheap Indian. The prawn
vindaloo (A\$16) was devilishly hot — as it should be — and the chicken
korma (A\$13) was also very good.

On Saturday lunchtime we ate grilled fish (A\$20) and a calamari salad
(A\$12) at Fish Frenzy at the harbour, and in the evening we went for
Asian at Dede, a restaurant with Indonesian and Thai food — an unusual
combination, but the food was good, especially the Indonesian dishes,
and reasonably priced: chicken satay (A\$6.60), scallops in plum‑soy
sauce with garlic and basil (A\$18). The pad thai (A\$13.50) was rather
mediocre.

As far as cafés go, Queenshead in North Hobart had the best cappuccino;
the prettiest café was The Village Store in Battery Point. And when it
comes to pubs, Hobart feels very cosmopolitan too: and pretty much
everywhere they had a delicious liquorice-like stout on tap — Cascade
Special Stout. We went to Shipswrights Arms Hotel in Battery Point,
Irish Murphy’s at Salamanca Place, and Bells & Anchor, supposedly
Australia’s oldest pub.

We stayed at Narrara Backpackers, relatively central — about a 10‑minute
walk from the centre — a small hostel in a pretty old house and cheap
(A\$44 for a twin). That’s the good news. The room was very small, the
shared areas weren’t always clean, and a lot of things (showers,
internet…) could do with being fixed up.

## 1 Mar 2005 — Tasmania: North‑East Coast / Launceston / back to Melbourne

On Sunday morning, after a small breakfast and good coffee at Sal’s on
Salamanca Place, we left Hobart in bright sunshine heading north on the
Heritage Highway, which connects the capital with Launceston. It runs
through a landscape that, for Tasmania, is fairly unspectacular — very
dry, brown summer hills that feel almost Southern‑European.

To relieve the monotony, there are a few pretty historic little towns
along the way, like Oatlands or the picturesque village of Ross. North
of Ross we left the highway and headed for the north‑east coast. The
closer we got to the sea, the lusher and greener the vegetation became.

At the Mount Elephant Pass near the coast there’s a beautiful little
countryside café in the middle of nowhere with tasty crêpe specialities
— not cheap, though (A\$16.50 for a crêpe filled with chicken,
blueberries and camembert). After the pass we hit the coastal road at
the grand, long Lagoon Beach. As far as St Helens the road runs past
white, untouched beaches and beautiful lagoons.

One of Tasmania’s most beautiful coastal landscapes (and that’s saying
something) lies between St Helens and the cape of the same name on a
peninsula: the wonderful Peron sand dunes above an endless white beach
on the south side, and small coves in postcard scenery on the north
side, where we even dared a swim in the calm, crystal‑clear water.

After that we visited a cheese farm in Pyengana (they also made an
excellent cappuccino) and also did two short inland walks to waterfalls,
and the rainforest walk to the 90-metre-high St Columba Falls was
particularly wonderful

The road from there was a horrible gravel track through the forest and
we were lucky our car made it. Back on sealed roads we drove through
very picturesque, almost pre‑alpine rolling hills, past countless cow
pastures, to Scottsdale, where we could stay for free at the municipal
campsite. In itself it’s a lovely spot in a park with grass, barbecues
and a small pond. Unfortunately the facilities were old and grubby, but
you know what they say about gift horses…

We spent our last day in Launceston mostly doing chores: laundry,
packing, returning the car, etc. We treated ourselves to lunch at
Stillwater, considered one of Tasmania’s best restaurants — though with
all the great fresh produce here you don’t have to be a genius to put
good food on a plate. The fish fillet with ginger mash was tasty, as
were the scallops in soy‑mirin sauce, but I suspect I could have managed
to cook this just as well. With main courses at A\$20–25 it isn’t cheap,
but eating well in a nice setting is still nice.

We stayed again at the Launceston Backpackers and flew back to Melbourne
the next morning (Virgin Blue, A\$58).

## 3 Mar 2005 — Leaving Australia: Great Ocean Road / Ballarat / Geelong

I’m lying in a half‑empty plane (Thai Airways from Melbourne to
Bangkok), with a whole row of seats to myself. A long‑haul flight really
doesn’t get better than that. And still I’m a little sad to be leaving
Australia for good, much as I’m looking forward to Asia and to seeing
Elaine again. I had a bloody great time here — and I wish I had more
time and money to travel Australia properly.

After we arrived in Melbourne around noontime we rented a car for the
last two days to drive the Great Ocean Road — one of the most beautiful
coastal roads in the world — and from Melbourne it’s just a 700‑km loop,
which is nothing by Australian standards. The Great Ocean Road really is
well worth it and incredibly varied.

Over those two days we saw cute koalas in their natural habitat on Cape
Otway Road; impressive green coastline with lonely beaches between
Anglesea and Apollo Bay; the beautiful Erskine Falls in rainforest near
Lorne; dense eucalyptus forests; gentle green hill country that much
reminded me of the Wild Coast of South Africa’s Transkei; and, of
course, the famous sandstone formations around Port Campbell: the
postcard scenes of the Twelve Apostles, Gibson Beach, Loch Ard Gorge,
and so on.

We spent the first night at a wonderfully located small campsite (A\$20)
near Lorne, right by Cumberland Creek in a pretty little canyon where
ducks and kangaroos wandered around on the grass. We enjoyed a delicious
seafood dinner at the Pier Restaurant in Lorne right by the sea: tasty
grilled baby snapper (A\$28) and garlic scallops (A\$15), plus a
beautiful sunset over Lorne.

On the way back to Melbourne we made a detour to Ballarat, a former
gold‑rush boomtown whose centre still has magnificent 19th‑century
buildings — at a scale and harmony you rarely find in Australia. There’s
a reason Ballarat is one of Victoria’s biggest tourist attractions.

On the last evening we treated ourselves once more to a very good dinner
at the Gamekeepers Inn — despite the kitschy safari décor a rather
upmarket restaurant with typical Australian food: a grill platter with
buffalo, kangaroo and emu (A\$26), and a seafood plate with scallops,
prawns and squid (A\$25), plus crisp fresh vegetables. A nice culinary
finale.

We spent the night at the Formule 1 at the airport. The rooms (A\$69)
are all en‑suite here in Australia, but otherwise they’re no different
from the French standard.

After I drove Mona to Avalon airport this morning, I spent a few hours
in Geelong, Victoria’s second‑largest city, 75 km south of Melbourne.
Despite the big, ugly industrial plants around the city, Geelong is
worth a short detour. Even if Lonely Planet is probably right when they
say, “Geelong isn’t a city where you spend your honeymoon,” it still has
a very pretty green harbour promenade and enough street cafés to be
reasonably attractive — and more than that: right in the centre of
Geelong there was a Bretzel café that bakes possibly the best pretzels
east of Stuttgart. Good‑bye, Australia!

## 6 Mar 2005 — Bangkok

I didn’t get to Banglamphu until after midnight and had already resigned
myself to all the decent rooms being taken. But I was lucky: the first
room I looked at was perfectly fine. At Four Sons on Phra Arthit Road I
got a small but clean single for 350 baht — including luxuries like
air‑con, satellite TV, hot shower, a safe and 24‑hour room service. No
complaints, really.

It’s so wonderful to be back in Thailand — to be able to afford my own
room, to eat delicious food without watching every penny, and to let
myself be properly spoiled. On the first day I got a proper shave, a
haircut, and treated myself to a fantastic 90‑minute massage for 300
baht. I gorged on delicious mangoes and pineapple, had grilled red
snapper (100 baht) and som tam for dinner, and ended the day with a big
Chang.

Yesterday was the coolest day I’ve ever experienced in Bangkok, and I
took advantage of the pleasant temperature: I walked to Chinatown and
visited the markets for the first time without breaking into a sweat.
Then I took a tuk‑tuk to MBK, did a bit of shopping and went to the
cinema. Constantine has to be one of the worst films I’ve seen in years,
but with a ticket price of 120 baht that’s not too tragic.

I took the Skytrain and the boat back to Banglamphu, ate a good red
squid curry in my guesthouse and went to bed early.

Tonight I’m taking the tourist VIP bus to Khao Sok National Park (550
baht). What fascinates me again and again is the speed with which the
Khao San Road area transforms from a backpacker ghetto into a smart
nightlife district, with bars and restaurants that are also frequented
by plenty of locals.

There are fewer and fewer of the traditional dives; instead lots of new
guesthouses are opening, with rooms of almost mid‑range quality. And the
bar and restaurant scene is improving too: chic cocktail lounges and
restaurants keep popping up. The nicest new opening is Sunset Street, a
stylish oasis in a side lane off KSR: a beautiful courtyard flanked by
colonial‑style buildings. A morning in Bangkok can hardly start better
than with an excellent (and at 65 baht very expensive) cappuccino in the
beautiful Starbucks branch in Sunset Street.

## 8 Mar 2005 — Bangkok / Khao Sok National Park

I had another very nice day in Bangkok. It was a sunny but not too hot
Sunday — perfect for a long walk through the old town. I climbed the
Golden Mount, enjoyed the calm in the Royal Park, went to the fish
market at Tha Tien Pier, and walked across the university campus and
along the river promenade to Banglamphu Pier.

I had a small lunch at Som Tam Lamphu on Phra Arthit Road — a cute
little restaurant like many in the area. The food was only so‑so,
though: the spicy prawn salad (70 baht) was more salty than spicy. The
dim sum were very cheap and okay (15 baht for three ha kao), at least.

At around 9 a.m. I arrived in Khao Sok National Park. I had a very good
seat on the bus and was able to sleep most of the time. I moved into a
nice, secluded bungalow on stilts with private bathroom and a veranda at
Mountain View Lodge (200 baht) and enjoyed the privacy and the quiet
forest location for a while.

At midday I went on an almost three‑hour elephant trek (700 baht). I had
my own elephant, and although we didn't end up riding inside the
national park, the area was very beautiful — karst scenery like in Khao
Sok itself.

In the afternoon the friendly young owner of the resort drove us to a
nearby cave temple where lots of monkeys hang around and we could feed
them. I spent the evening with a Chang beer on my veranda, listened to
music and looked out over the jungle. A wonderful place — I felt very
comfortable.

Today was very tiring. I spent almost the entire day in the national
park, whose entrance is about a 15‑minute walk from my bungalow. Along
the road there are lots of resorts, restaurants and even internet cafés.
It’s very quiet here at the moment, because the coastal tourists haven’t
come back yet since the tsunami

For a few years now foreigners have had to pay 200 baht entry to all
Thai national parks, which I find a bit much. At least you get a
brochure with possible walks and the trails were fairly well signposted.
After Tasmania — where most paths are also suitable for people who
aren’t very fit — I first had to readjust to the Thai jungle.

I walked 7 km to Ton Glay waterfall. The last couple of kilometres were
very tough, partly on steep trails that are hard to walk even when dry.
I must have lost at least five litres of water — luckily there were
enough small waterfalls along the way, and even a nice canyon with pools
to cool off in.

The area is definitely pretty, especially the karst landscape, similar
to Krabi province, but at the moment everything is incredibly dry, so it
hardly feels like rainforest.

Since the food both at my lodge and at the Jungle Lodge next door wasn’t
great, I went to a proper restaurant after the hike, Thai Herb, hoping
to get something reasonably authentic. They also offer cooking classes,
and you dine with beautiful views over the surrounding landscape — so it
is worth it for that alone. I ordered a red Thai curry with tofu and
vegetables, and although the basil was missing it was quite tasty — and
no more expensive than at the lodges (55 baht). And the 20‑baht
pineapple shake even came garnished with an orchid. That’s what I call
culture.

## 15 Mar 2005 — Ko Phangan

I spent just under a week on Ko Phangan, as usual at my dream beach,
Thong Nai Pan Noi. Like everywhere in Thailand’s tourist areas, this
region is moving more and more towards comfort tourism: the cheap bamboo
huts are being replaced by comfortable air‑con bungalows; prices are
going up; and since my last stay six months ago several new bars have
opened — and even a Mexican restaurant.

But that’s happening all over the island now, and Ko Phangan hasn’t been
a destination for hardcore budget backpackers for a long time. Even
though the roads are still in bad shape, more and more families and
middle‑class tourists are escaping the hustle of Samui. because it’s
still quiet and relaxed here — and Nai Pan Noi is still the paradise I
had found five years ago, just no longer an unknown backpacker paradise.
Which, as I get older, doesn’t bother me as much anymore.

I spent the first three nights in one of the few remaining budget
bungalow resorts, Digree, whose simple but quite nice huts on stilts
stand above the lagoon. For 250 baht I had my own bathroom and a veranda
with Thai cushions. The bed was as hard as the floor, though — and in
the rainy season I wouldn’t stay there even for free because of the
mosquitoes.

For the last three nights I treated myself to more comfort and moved
into a very nice wooden bungalow at Thong Ta Pan Resort: with a big,
wonderfully comfortable bed; everything very clean and pretty; and the
bungalow stood on a small hill, in a quiet garden, with sea views from
the veranda — not bad for 500 baht.

Elaine came over from Samui with her mother, and as expected they loved
the beach just as much as I do. It’s the perfect place to do absolutely
nothing: sleep, sunbathe, read, swim, eat — and occasionally get a
massage on the beach.

I had absolutely no desire to leave, but I’d booked two flights to Macau
for tomorrow for me and Elaine, and since she’s turned into a real
traveller over the past months and wants to see the world, I couldn’t
persuade her to bin the tickets and stay longer.

## 20 March 2005

We took the ferry from Ko Phangan to Surat Thani and the night bus back
to Bangkok, getting in around five in the morning. Since we didn’t have
to be at the airport until around midday, we rented ourselves a 150‑baht
cell at the Euro Inn for the remaining hours — one of the last proper
dives left on Khao San Road. The flight to Macau (1,200 baht, AirAsia)
took just under three hours, and it was already almost dark when we
arrived.

We took the public bus from the airport into the centre (3.30 MOP) and
first managed to get ourselves a bit lost while looking for Hotel
Central, which, in the end, wasn't all that hard to find at all — just
west of Largo do Senado, which, with its restored Portuguese colonial
buildings, is probably one of the most beautiful squares in Asia,
especially at night when it’s lit up. We had a very spacious room with
an old‑fashioned bathroom; a little musty and without a window, but
otherwise perfectly fine for 160 MOP.

On the first evening we went out for a few beers in a bar in Nape, the
nightlife district, and although Macau is probably cheaper than Hong
Kong, the prices after Thailand were rather painful (88 MOP for six
bottles of beer of your choice). We spent the next day sightseeing on
the peninsula: we visited Monte Fort, spent some time in the
entertaining Macau Museum, saw the ruins of St Paul’s, and took a long
walk to the 12th‑century A‑Ma Temple and back along the Avenida da
República promenade.

Macau is an interesting and attractive city — Chinese, with a fairly
well‑preserved Portuguese legacy. But it hasn’t been a sleepy little
colonial town for a long time: high‑rise estates on reclaimed land and
kitschy casinos dominate Macau more than colonial buildings or the
original Chinese quarters.

On Friday we took the bus (5 MOP) out to Coloane, an island belonging to
Macau. Its only settlement, Vila do Coloane, has preserved a little of
its original atmosphere and feels very quiet and laid‑back. I had a
feijoada (25 MOP) for lunch at the Village Café — a not particularly
tasty bean‑and‑pork stew that nonetheless seems to be very popular in
Macau — and we walked along the quite pretty coast to Hac Sa beach. Back
in Macau we wandered through the very pleasant, authentic Chinese
quarter southwest of Avenida do Ribeiro, with lots of traditional shops
and seafood restaurants.

On Saturday at lunchtime we took the fast boat to Hong Kong. The
crossing (HK\$155) took only an hour, but it’s not recommended for
anyone with a sensitive stomach, even if the views are very nice at
times. We checked into the first guesthouse we came across in the
Mirador Mansions in Kowloon, with the nice but misleading name Garden
Hostel. For the painfully high price of \$200 we got a dark
five‑square‑metre cell with two narrow beds and a tiny bathroom.

Mirador Mansions is one of Kowloon’s notorious, filthy, run‑down
high‑rise blocks — budget guesthouses, travel agencies and shops where
you can buy everything from fake Rolexes to overpriced electronics, and
dark corridors full of people from all over the world. Definitely an
interesting, if not exactly cosy, place to spend the night.

In the afternoon we had a look around the chaos of Kowloon, but we both
agreed that Hong Kong, for all that, is a fascinating, very pleasant
city where you can really feel quite at home. I ate at the nice, busy
fusion restaurant Red Ants: “Shredded Duck and Jellyfish in Chili Sauce”
— very tasty and surprisingly cheap (\$30). The room was only bearable
when completely drunk, so we went for six beers (\$109) during happy
hour at the pretty 48th St Blues Bar and afterwards drank a bottle of
Sang Som with Coke back in the room — but what happened next?

Today we checked out and moved to the Cosmic Guesthouse on the 12th
floor of Mirador Mansions — a much better choice. The room here (\$200)
is tiny too, but bright, quiet, very clean and surprisingly quite
homely. Since Elaine had to sleep off her hangover, I went out around
lunchtime and had a look around on my own. I took the ferry to Wan Chai;
the views of Victoria’s skyline and the harbour are fantastic,
especially at night when everything’s lit up.

After an afternoon snack at the Aussie pub Ned Kelly’s Last Stand (a
delicious chilli burger with excellent chips for \$85), we went to the
R.E.M. concert at the Wan Chai Convention Center. As luck would have it
— and what a stroke of luck — R.E.M. were playing their only Asia show
in Hong Kong today, and of course we couldn’t miss it despite the steep
ticket prices. Even after all these years, R.E.M. are still one of the
best live bands in the world, and the concert was magical — one of the
absolute highlights of the last six months.

## 21 March 2005

Today was pretty eventful, which meant we scrapped our original plan to
travel to Vietnam via Guangzhou and instead booked a flight to Bangkok
for next Saturday. We trudged from one travel agency to the next trying
to get visas for Vietnam and China, but it would have cost a fortune and
we would have had to stay in Hong Kong for another whole week, which, at
these prices, would have been ruinous.

So, over a tasty lunch at the Anyway Café in Kowloon (turbot fillet on
lemongrass vermicelli, \$45 including soup and coffee), I suggested we
fly back to Thailand. After that decision we both had the idea of
properly enjoying Hong Kong over the next few days, and we went for
afternoon tea at the Peninsula, Hong Kong’s most famous hotel — a
magnificent colonial building with pompous interiors.

It cost a fortune (\$320), but when you’re living in a cell you should
allow yourself something nice every now and then — and the quality was
excellent. In the evening I went to the Temple Street Night Market. I
like Hong Kong best at night anyway, and this market confirmed that
impression. It’s nice to have the feeling of really being in China: all
the bustle, the crowds, neon lights and the smell of the food stalls …

## 23 March 2005

We spent most of yesterday in and around Central. In the morning it was
absolutely pouring, but luckily it isn’t hard to find a nice café here.
We took shelter in the chic Q8 Lounge, whose glass front looks out onto
Central’s street life. The cappuccino was excellent too — and very
expensive (\$40).

Once the rain stopped we took the escalator up to SoHo, Hong Kong’s
nicest restaurant district. In a relatively small area you find almost
everything, from pretty Mediterranean cafés to a Russian place and a
Mexican. Not surprisingly, I liked this neighbourhood a lot. I picked up
a tasty, generous sushi lunch box from a Japanese place for \$60, and we
spent a few hours at the wonderfully kitschy, comfortable Sen Deli Café,
which makes excellent sandwiches with indulgent things like Gruyère and
foie gras.

After that we took the escalator all the way up to Mid‑Levels, a
high‑rise residential district above the city, and walked back down
through the botanical gardens (with a small zoo) to Lan Kwai Fong, Hong
Kong’s most attractive nightlife district, whose narrow lanes offer an
excellent selection of bars and lounges. Given our financial situation,
our nightlife consisted of drinking canned beer in our cell — well, at
least that way we can choose the music ourselves.

Today we visited the Hong Kong Museum of History, because all museums
are free on Wednesdays. It’s well worth a visit that covers Hong Kong’s
history from the Stone Age to the present day, with short films, photos,
lots of exhibits and multimedia displays. We spent many hours there and
afterwards went to the pretty Café de Fontaine by the fountain on the
main square in Tsim Sha Tsui East — that modern, built‑from‑scratch
“sibling district” of Hong Kong’s chaotic but far more atmospheric
tourist area.

For dinner we went to the “Biergarten”, a German pub in Kowloon. I
haven’t eaten anything German in six months and suddenly felt a strange
craving for it, even though it isn’t very sensible to order outrageously
overpriced Käsespätzle (\$68) in a culinary paradise like Hong Kong.
Still, the food was quite good and I enjoyed the Hefeweizen too (\$36,
happy hour price).

Elaine enjoyed her meatloaf with fried potatoes. When it comes to
culinary pleasure she’s a hopeless case, and it’s very hard to find
common ground. But I can adapt — even if it means needlessly spending a
lot of money on Western food. After dinner we went for a wander around
the Temple Street Night Market.

## 26 March 2005

I’m sitting on the plane from Macau to Bangkok and I’m already looking
forward to being back in Thailand. Not that I didn’t like Hong Kong —
quite the opposite: it’s a great city everyone should see and
experience. But the weather, the prices and the prospect of having a
decent room all clearly speak in favour of Bangkok.

The last two days were really nice. We spent most of our time on Hong
Kong Island and took the bus to Shek O, one of the last villages left in
Hong Kong’s concrete chaos — an oasis of calm, nice for strolling around
and relaxing on the pretty beach. The setting is wonderful too, but Hong
Kong Island in general is spectacular, and the bus rides along narrow,
winding roads over the hills are an attraction in their own right.

We also visited Stanley, a smart beach district in the south of the
island — anyone who owns an apartment here must be very rich. There’s a
big but fairly touristy market and a waterfront promenade lined with
restaurants. We opted for a picnic instead: pâté and Gruyère.

Since the weather was quite decent we took the bus back to Central via
Repulse Bay and rode the Peak Tram up to the lookout above the city. The
railway has been operating since 1888, and the ride up — but above all
the views down over the city and the harbour — are probably the
highlights in Hong Kong. We timed it so we could enjoy the view both in
daylight and later, once it was dark and the city had turned into a sea
of lights.

Afterwards I went to Lan Kwai Fong for a beer and some people‑watching.
It was the day before the Easter holiday and most of the bars were
packed — great atmosphere.

Yesterday the weather was pretty bad and we spent most of the day on
public transport, which is a great way to explore the city. Public
transport in Hong Kong has to be among the best in the world. The metro
is fast and efficient, the buses are double‑deckers, and from the top
deck you can watch city life go by or admire the scenery. The trams are
double‑deckers too, real originals, mostly still from the middle of the
last century. And then, of course, there are the ferries — perhaps the
most typical Hong Kong form of transport.

And although almost everything in Hong Kong is expensive, public
transport is very cheap and thanks to the brilliant Octopus Card — which
works on all modes of transport (and can even be used in many
supermarkets and even restaurants, etc.) — you never really need to
carry cash.

We took the bus from Central to Aberdeen, whose harbour is still home to
plenty of families living on their boats. From there we’d originally
wanted to take a ferry to Lamma Island, but the cool, grey weather
didn’t exactly make us feel like an island trip. Instead we just jumped
on random buses, saw neighbourhoods we didn’t know yet, and eventually
ended up in Causeway Bay, where we went for a walk through Victoria
Park.

From there we took one of those wonderful old trams that run along
almost the entire north coast of Hong Kong Island to North Point, a very
atmospheric, lively district with interesting markets and hardly any
tourists. After a bit of shopping there we took the bus to Kennedy Town
and the tram back to Central.

On our last evening we decided to eat in SoHo and, for budget reasons,
chose a Mexican place, El Taco Loco. They only had plastic cutlery, but
the food (nachos, enchiladas) was good and cheap (\$58), and the mojitos
were too (two for \$58). Afterwards we went next door to the Empire
Lounge for a few cocktails. You have to experience this place to believe
it exists: ultra‑kitschy booth corners, everything ornate and in shades
of red, the most terrible romantic love songs of all time… You only
really find something like this in Asia. It was cosy enough, though, and
while the cocktails were expensive, they were good.

We finished the day with a couple of happy‑hour Coronas (\$46 for two)
in Lan Kwai Fong.

## 31 March 2005

The last few days in Bangkok were unspectacular. I had a very nice,
comfortable room at Mango Lagoon Place on Rambuttri (700B) and enjoyed
the air con and the luxury (carpet, couch, big comfortable bed) after
the week in the Mansion. The days were very hot and I read a lot —
either in my room or in a café/restaurant — and I enjoyed Bangkok’s cosy
atmosphere, the low prices and the good food. Every now and then I met
up with Elaine for dinner or a few beers.

We’d decided to go our separate ways, since lately we’d been getting
into arguments more and more often over silly little things and it made
no sense to keep forcing ourselves to travel together. Very unfortunate,
but probably the most sensible solution.

One evening I met Peak for dinner — a well‑travelled woman from Bangkok
from the VT community — and we talked about this and that: our travels,
and the changes Bangkok, and especially Khao San, has gone through in
recent years… that was really nice.

The culinary highlight was dinner at Hemlock on Phra Arthit Road, which
quite rightly is considered the top Thai restaurant in Banglamphu. The
food is Thai cooking at its best, with a menu as thick as a book full of
great things. The red chicken‑and‑pineapple curry was absolutely top (80
baht), as were the mushrooms with cashews (80 baht), and despite the
outstanding standard of the kitchen Hemlock is hardly more expensive
than the many farang restaurants in the area — definitely not my last
visit.

After five years I decided it was time to visit southern Cambodia again
and see what has changed, so this morning I took the minibus to Trat
near the border (200 baht / 6 hours and uncomfortable, but I was too
lazy to take a public bus, since Bangkok’s bus terminals are all ages
away from Banglamphu). I got a room at the Trat Hotel by the market.
There are some pretty guesthouses here too, but since I don’t have a
guidebook with me and didn’t feel like walking around town in the
blazing heat with my backpack, I went to the first hotel I saw — and the
room is fine: spacious and fairly clean, with a private bathroom
(unfortunately only a squat toilet), 220B.

Trat hasn’t changed all that much in the last five years. Sure, there
are more guesthouses, internet cafés and farang restaurants, but the old
town with its wooden houses is still a very quiet, relaxed place — and
just for the market alone, where you can eat wonderfully, Trat is always
worth a short visit. Also, I haven’t seen many other farangs here.

## 1 Apr 2005 — Thailand → Cambodia (Hat Lek / Koh Kong / Sihanoukville)

Early in the morning I took the minibus to the border at Hat Lek (100
baht). Crossing the border was relatively painless. I paid 1,000 baht
for my visa — they wanted 1,100, but I knew they always try to rip you
off (the official price, which they never accept, would be US\$20).

The next rip‑off attempt came shortly after, but I was prepared for that
too. I took a motodop (US\$1) to Koh Kong when a persistent tout from
Mealy Chenda tried to lure me into an overpriced tourist van. He managed
to convince my motodop driver to take me straight to their agency, but I
threatened not to pay him if he didn’t take me to the regular bus
station — which worked. The tout still wouldn’t give up and followed us.

After I’d already negotiated a price (US\$4) to Sihanoukville with the
bus driver, the tout had a chat with the driver and suddenly the price
jumped to 400 baht. Mealy Chenda is a mafia‑style tourism outfit that
“Thailand‑ises” travelling in Cambodia by offering tourist‑only vans
between destinations at inflated prices — and they use all kinds of
methods to intercept and lure travellers to their business as they enter
Cambodia.

Fortunately I’d read about them beforehand and was determined to avoid
them. Granted, the Mealy Chenda vans are obviously much more comfortable
(but also much more expensive) than public transport — but what makes
Cambodia so interesting and challenging, namely travelling with locals
in overcrowded vehicles, gets completely lost for many travellers.

I told the tout to piss off, but in the end the bus driver still refused
to take me for US\$4. At least I bargained him down to 300 baht. The
trip to Sihanoukville was, as I’d expected, a nine‑hour nightmare with
25 (!) people in a knackered Honda van — but that was exactly what I
wanted: to experience Cambodia again the way it was five years earlier,
before tourist vans and all that.

Of course I could have taken the ferry to Sihanoukville, which would
have taken only three and a half hours — but I’d already done that trip
and I was curious about the new road through what used to be
impenetrable terrain. In Cambodia, “new road” doesn’t mean a nice paved
highway, though: it was the usual bumpy track, with four river crossings
on ferries that must have looked no different a century ago.

Luckily I had a decent window seat and could watch the untouched “Wild
West” landscape. I was pretty wrecked when I arrived in Sihanoukville,
but the trip was definitely worth it — if only for nostalgic reasons.
This was the Cambodian adventure I’d got to know and love back then, and
which no longer exists on the main tourist routes.

I got a room at the Geckozy, a very pretty guesthouse in the centre of
Sihanoukville — not near the beach, but I’m certainly not here for
sunbathing; I could have done that in Thailand. For US\$4 I got a very
nice, very individually decorated room (Japanese themed; every room has
a different theme) with private bathroom — unfortunately with a squat
toilet, the only downside — still excellent value for money, and the
atmosphere in the cosy lounge is great too.

The guesthouse is run by a German‑Japanese team, accordingly very well
kept, with an interesting menu at very fair prices (Lao beer, 75 cents).
I was starving when I arrived and ordered a Japanese udon salad — a real
treat, and a huge portion for US\$2.75 — though I could just as easily
have ordered meatballs with fried potatoes.

In the evening I wandered around Sihanoukville a bit, but the town
itself isn’t particularly attractive. On the other hand, it now offers a
wide choice of farang bars and restaurants where you can forget the
hardships of independent travel in Cambodia at reasonable prices. The
season seems to be over, though, because most places were pretty empty.

I had a good cappuccino (US\$1.50) at Espresso Kampuchea, and since I
was the only guest I ended up having a longer chat with the English
owner, who then even gave me a glass of Australian Cabernet Sauvignon on
the house.

For dinner I ate a tasty pâté baguette (2,000 riel) from a street stall
— one of the best legacies the French left in Indochina.

## 2 Apr 2005 — Sihanoukville (beaches / food / sunset)

I thought it would be a good idea to do something for my fitness again,
so I rented a bicycle to ride along Sihanoukville’s beaches. My first
stop was the biggest and most popular beach, Occheutal, east of town.
And I’m not revising my verdict: the beaches here really aren’t anything
special — white sand, sure, and bright blue sea, but for real tropical
feeling there are simply not enough coconut palms, and the beaches are
fairly narrow. And everywhere (like all over the country) there’s
rubbish lying around.

Five years ago the special appeal was that the beaches were still
relatively empty and unspoilt. That’s over too: Sihanoukville is now an
obligatory stop on any Southeast Asia trip. But enough negativity. The
area is very beautiful, and you really can have a good time here.

There are countless seafood restaurants and cocktail bars with sunbeds
and hammocks right on the beach, and in many of these places you even
get a room for free if you eat and drink there twice a day — with the
very low prices they charge, that’s an excellent deal.

On the way to Sokha Beach I got a flat tyre and had to push the bike
back to the guesthouse for half an hour. I decided a motorbike is better
suited to the roads here — and at US\$4 per day you almost have to rent
one.

I went to Sokha Beach, the prettiest and cleanest beach in
Sihanoukville, though most of it is taken up by a luxury resort. For
lunch I chose Victory Beach and the Hawaii Seafood Restaurant — despite
the silly name more of an beach hangout place for rich Khmers than a
tourist restaurant — and the food was among the best I’ve had in the
last six months. For US\$5 I got a huge plate of fried mud crabs in
garlic‑chilli sauce: a true gourmet meal right on the beach. Mud crabs,
with their delicious slightly sweet meat, are a highly sought‑after and
outrageously expensive delicacy in the Western world.

After that I checked out Weather Station Hill, ground zero for
backpackers in Sihanoukville, and the place where we’d stayed five years
ago. Not much has changed, except that there’s more choice now — and
with Papagayo there’s a wonderfully cosy cocktail‑and‑tapas bar with
Latin‑American chill‑out music. This is where I’m writing these lines.
For dessert I had some tasty stuffed gnocchi “Japanese style”, fusion
style, for US\$1.50.

As I said: even if Sihanoukville isn’t exactly paradise, you can pamper
yourself very well here for little money.

My next destination was Sihanoukville Mountain, a viewpoint on a hill
above town. The views over the city and the coast, with the many
offshore islands, were great — and from up there Sihanoukville looked
really quiet and tropical.

Before sunset I had a passable Cambodian fish soup on the beautiful
terrace of the Mary‑an Guesthouse. I enjoyed the glorious sunset at
Victory Beach. It may be the worst beach of them all, but it has the
best beach bars in Sihanoukville and unobstructed views of the red
fireball sinking into the sea right between the offshore islands — very
picturesque, especially with the fishing boats on the shore.

For dinner I treated myself to something really good again, since
everything here is so affordable. I dined at La Paillotte, a chic French
restaurant that feels a bit out of place in the backpacker ghetto of
Weather Station Hill. The interesting menu was personally presented by
the chef (a Madagascan) — in French. I had a fish carpaccio (US\$3) as a
starter and barracuda fillet in ganja sauce (Khmer fusion, I’d call it)
with thyme potatoes (US\$5) as my main. Both were excellently prepared
and very tasty.

I ended the evening with two (surprisingly good) cocktails (US\$1.50)
next door at Papagayo.

## 4 Apr 2005 — Kampot / Kep

Yesterday morning I took the public van (10,000 riel) to Kampot. The
road is nothing like it was five years ago: it’s fully paved now, and
the first 75 kilometres are in excellent condition. The trip took less
than two hours.

Kampot itself has thankfully hardly changed since my last visit. It’s
still a cosy provincial town on the river, with whole streets of French
colonial architecture, an interesting, authentic market, and a peaceful
atmosphere. Of course far more travellers pass through now. You can book
tours to Bokor, there are a few farang bars, espresso and internet — but
all still on a manageable scale, and the town hasn’t lost any of its
atmosphere. And every now and then, a little more tourism would be
welcome.

By now there’s even a proper traveller guesthouse with the fitting name
Blissful, offering everything a backpacker needs: a cosy chill‑out
lounge with hammocks, a cheap bar with happy hour, and clean rooms —
unfortunately all fully booked, so I had to settle for a dorm bed. That
was fine: the dorm was basically a spacious triple room, the shared
bathroom was big and clean, and for \$2.50 you really can’t complain.

I spent the day walking through Kampot’s streets, ate prawns with
lemongrass at the local place by the bridge (8,000 riel), and enjoyed
the glorious sunset by the river. For dinner I went to Ta Euv, a big
seafood place on the river where only locals were eating. I had squid
with broccoli (8,000 riel) — very tasty.

Today I rode a motorbike (\$3 a day) to Kep‑sur‑Mer, a former French
seaside resort 25 kilometres east of Kampot. Most of the French villas
are now just ruins, but at the moment they’re working hard to turn Kep
into a resort for rich Cambodians. Foreigners will find it difficult to
get excited about Kep because there aren’t any truly beautiful beaches.
Still, the scenery is quite pretty, with tropical islands offshore —
though there’s no accommodation on those beaches yet.

I liked Kep for two reasons: first, the colonial ruins make great
brilliant subjects for photos; second, you can eat fresh seafood
cheaply. There are lots of vendors along the shore who cook it for you,
and that’s reason enough to come here.

Back in Kampot I took a walk through the market and went to happy hour
(5–6:30) at the Rusty Keyhole, a farang bar by the river: 5,000 riel for
a big bottle of Angkor, and the sunset is free.

Luckily the happy hours here are well coordinated. At Blissful, happy
hour starts at 7 p.m., and the Blue Lagoons (\$1.50) are excellent.

What genuinely makes me happy — and what I missed in Sihanoukville — is
that in this region the Khmer smiles and helpfulness are still real.
Kids greet you with a grin and “hello”, not with an outstretched hand
and “you give me one dollar”. There are no motodop drivers trying to rip
you off, and when my motorbike broke down, everyone tried to help and
work out what was wrong, without asking for money.

This is the Cambodia I got to know and love five years ago, and I’m glad
it still exists — at least off the beaten track.

## 11 Apr 2005 — The End (Phnom Penh → Bangkok → Frankfurt)

The end! Back in Frankfurt.

I spent another five nice, cosy days in Phnom Penh (\$2 by minibus from
Kampot, three hours). For the first night I stayed in the backpacker
area at Boeung Kak Lake, at the simple Grand View Guesthouse. The room
was small, but had a window with a lake view, a bathroom, and a big bed
for \$3.

Although there were plenty of cheap bars and traveller cafés and the
lakeside area, with its many wooden houses, was quite interesting and
traditional, I decided to change location the next day — I had
absolutely no appetite for backpacker culture here. I got myself a nice
\$10 room at Tiger Feet near the riverside promenade, with air‑con, TV,
minibar, etc., and simply wanted to enjoy the last days of the trip.

I met Elaine again and spent quite a bit of time with her, went shopping
at the Russian Market, took long walks through Phnom Penh’s streets, and
spent a lot of time in the lovely street cafés along the wonderful
riverside promenade, or at Le Tamarin in gastronomically solid 240
Street. One night we had an excellent dinner there on the balcony by
candlelight, with cocktails, a bottle of French wine, and tasty tapas —
mostly Mediterranean with Asian influences. Fair prices, nice
atmosphere, good food.

I also really liked the Salt Lounge, a modern‑chic scene bar that struck
me as very gay‑friendly and, in a city like Phnom Penh, almost feels out
of place: comfy sofas, electronic beats, good cocktails. Something like
this certainly didn’t exist here five years ago — but otherwise the city
has hardly changed in any noticeable way.

For fun we took a one‑hour rickshaw ride for \$1 to the main sights. It
was very nice, even though I already knew the city fairly well.

Personally, I think Phnom Penh is an extremely attractive city with a
strong feel‑good factor — a place where you can easily spend a week or
more, and a worthy end to my trip. Of course that’s purely from a
tourist’s perspective. In Cambodia, even in the capital, many things are
still going catastrophically wrong. To see that, you only have to walk
around with open eyes and occasionally glance at the Phnom Penh Post.

Since a flight to Bangkok cost over \$100, I decided to go back by bus
(\$16) — as fast as possible via Battambang. It was still a long 15‑hour
trip, and I only arrived in Bangkok late in the evening. I treated
myself to something properly nice one last time and took a small,
luxurious room at Majestic Suites on Sukhumvit for 1,200 baht.
