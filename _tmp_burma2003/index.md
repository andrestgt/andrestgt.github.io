# Burma 2003

## Days 1–3 — Rangoon

We spent three fairly relaxed days in this pleasant, interesting
metropolis, which offers Southeast Asian flair, crumbling colonial
architecture and golden pagodas. The main attraction is the justifiably
world-famous Shwedagon Pagoda (US \$5), whose sheer scale only becomes
apparent once you have climbed the many steps up to the platform.
Otherwise it was simply a pleasure to stroll through the attractive rows
of buildings and soak up the atmosphere. A walk through the park and the
eponymous market is well worth it. The area around Sule Pagoda in the
city centre is attractive, too.

### Accommodation

White House — a traveller hotspot close to the centre. Small, simple
rooms with shared bathroom, fairly clean. The generous breakfast was
included in the pricey room rate (US \$7 per person).

### Food

Danubyu Daw Saw Yi Restaurant — simple place; inexpensive Burmese food,
decent but nothing special.

## Day 4

Bus journey from Rangoon to Shwenyaung (air-conditioned bus, 6,000 k, 18
h), then by taxi on to Nyaungshwe.

## Days 5–6 — Inle Lake

After 18 exhausting hours on a decidedly uncomfortable bus, we arrived
in Nyaungshwe, the main village of the Inle region, about five
kilometres north of the lake.

We spent the first day taking it easy in town, which felt like an oasis
of calm after Rangoon. Nyaungshwe is set up for tourists, but there
weren’t in fact that many people here. The village itself is quite
attractive, with some picturesque pagodas and canals. In the afternoon
we spent two relaxed hours drifting by canoe (1,000 k) through the rural
idyll criss-crossed by canals, visiting a monastery along the way and
watching the people on the water go about their daily activities.

On the second day we did the standard Inle Lake boat tour (7,000 k for
the boat). The lake is framed by high mountains, with pagodas dotted
along their slopes. Our first stop was the floating market of Ywama, a
tourist trap that is nevertheless quite pretty, in an attractive
village. The second stop — and the highlight of the day — was Indein, a
village that can be reached from the lake in about half an hour along a
canal. Here, hundreds of old, mostly unrestored stupas stand scattered
across the landscape. It is a very atmospheric place, and from a hill
above we had a wonderful view of the site and the surrounding area.
After a tasty lunch (fish for 1,200 k) in a restaurant near the Phaung
Daw U Pagoda, we looked at the floating gardens where vegetables are
grown on the lake, and then went to the Jumping Cat Monastery, where the
monks have trained cats to jump through hoops — something that triggered
an incomprehensible delight among both locals and tourists. The trip
ended with a swim in the shallow but pleasantly cool lake.

### Accommodation

Joy Guesthouse (US \$6 for a double room) — simple, but with a wonderful
shared veranda over the canal.

### Food

Big Drum Restaurant — average-quality Shan set menu (1,200 k), but a
nice atmosphere in bungalows by the canal. Terrible pineapple juice!

Mingalar Café served fantastic strawberry shakes.

## Days 7–8 — Kalaw

Pick-up truck ride to Shwenyaung (200 k, 30 min), then a pick-up truck
to Kalaw (800 k, 2.5 h).

This former British hill station lies at an altitude of 1,300 metres,
and even in the hot season it gets very cold at night. During the day,
however, the climate is wonderful. The town is quite attractive, mainly
because of its setting and what colonial architecture remains, but also
because of its relaxed atmosphere. On the afternoon of our arrival we
took a leisurely walk to the pretty caves behind the Shwe U Min Pagoda,
which contain numerous Buddha figures. At the monastery opposite we then
smoked cheroots with a friendly monk. A lovely little outing.

The next day we did a day hike to a Palaung village with a guide (5,000
k per person). The hilly surroundings of Kalaw are quite pretty, and we
passed vegetable fields and tea plantations. In the hill tribe village
we took a break with tea and bananas in a traditional longhouse. On the
way back we had delicious pumpkin curry for lunch in a beautifully
situated viewpoint restaurant.

### Accommodation

Golden Kalaw Inn: bright, spacious room with en-suite and large balcony
— US \$6.

### Food

Mediocre food at Everest Nepali Food Center.

The seven-hour night bus ride to Mandalay went by (as usual) while we
slept.

## Day 9 — Mandalay

I’d heard that Mandalay doesn’t live up to the romantic, exotic clichés
people attach to the name. Nevertheless, Mandalay is an interesting,
fascinating city: dirty, dusty, poor, with lively markets, pagodas,
traditional neighbourhoods with teakwood houses under coconut palms, and
various sights in the surrounding area. Since hefty entrance fees are
charged for almost all major pagodas in Mandalay, we limited ourselves
to the essentials: sunset on Mandalay Hill with great views over the
city, the Mahamuni Pagoda with the country’s second-holiest Buddha
image, as well as Mingun and Amarapura, two nearby towns.

Mingun (US \$3) is fairly touristy and is a 45-minute boat ride on the
Ayeyarwady from Mandalay. The unfinished Mingun Paya, which you can also
climb, is impressive. The highlight of Amarapura is the U Bein Bridge,
the longest teakwood bridge in the world (1.2 km). On the far side,
there’s a traditional Burmese village with a great atmosphere. Sunset on
the bridge is an unforgettable experience.

### Accommodation

Royal Guesthouse — so clean you could eat off the floor. Nice rooms for
US \$6 (without bathroom) or US \$10 (with bathroom and air
conditioning). Great value for the money.

## Day 11 — Pyin Oo Lwin (Maymyo)

This former colonial hill station has a pleasant climate and a large
population from the Indian subcontinent. Public transport here operates
in a kind of Wild West style, in colourful, enclosed horse-drawn
carriages. Ten kilometres outside town there is a beautiful, impressive
waterfall in the jungle, complete with pools for swimming and an
adventurous Indiana Jones-style suspension bridge. The Anisakan Falls
are not easy to reach, however. First we had to take a jeep to Anisakan,
then continue along a paved road and finally on foot down a steep canyon
(about one hour) — and of course back up again. Worth the effort,
though.

### Accommodation

Grace Hotel 2 — right by the clock tower, clean rooms for US \$5. Shared
facilities were pretty poor.

## Days 12–16 — Hsipaw

After a short ride to the railway station in one of the magnificent
horse-drawn carriages, we took the train to Hsipaw. An interesting
seven-hour journey — very bumpy even in the “first class” compartment.
Along the way you pass the impressive Gokteik Viaduct, built 100 years
ago and the second-highest railway bridge in the world.

A surprisingly large number of travellers come all the way out to the
small, remote Shan town of Hsipaw in search of relaxation and a more
authentic atmosphere. You can stroll through the village markets, hike
to hill-tribe villages or take boat trips on the river. There’s nothing
spectacular as such; the appeal lies in the remoteness and authenticity
of the area.

### Accommodation

Mr Charles Guesthouse is the travellers’ hangout in Hsipaw. The rooms
are good, though overpriced (US \$8 with bathroom). The nice terrace is
the evening gathering place for travellers. The management is
professional, organises boat trips and hands out free hiking maps for
the surrounding area.

### Food

Hwai Ta Restaurant — we ordered a whole steamed fish in a delicious
sauce for a ridiculous 1,000 k.

Bus ride back to Mandalay (1,800 k, 7 h).\
In the evening, we caught a cultural performance by the Moustache
Brothers.

Bus ride to Bagan (3,100 k, 9 h).

## Days 17–19 — Bagan

Burma’s biggest tourist attraction (US \$10) is this vast collection of
more than 800-year-old temples and pagodas on the dry plain of Bagan. A
fascinating place, whose exploration by bicycle along dusty tracks was
great fun. Because we were here in the hot season, we often had many of
the lesser monuments entirely to ourselves, and even in the main temples
there was very little going on. Even though the individual temples are
not as impressive as those in Angkor, it is the sheer number and the
panoramic views over the pagoda-studded plain that make the place so
fascinating.

### Accommodation

Yar Kinn Thar Hotel — clean mid-range rooms with air conditioning, TV
and bathroom at guesthouse prices (US \$10 double room). Good location
on the road to Old Bagan and close to many restaurants.

### Food

In the tourist restaurants on the street near our hotel, you could dine
in pleasant surroundings in the evenings. There was a choice of Chinese,
Indian, Italian and Burmese food; the quality was decent and the prices
moderate. The vegetarian restaurant next to Yar Kinn Thar served
dirt-cheap cocktails (350 k!).

## Day 20 — Pyay

Pyay is one of the largest cities in the country, but you’d never guess
it from the view from the magnificent golden Shwesandaw Pagoda on a hill
above the town. The fantastic view takes in coconut palms, pagodas and
the mighty Ayeyarwady in the background. From Pyay we took two short
trips. The first, however, was unintentional, as we got into the wrong
pick-up truck. It took us to Shwedaung, a traditional village south of
Pyay. Here, in the Shwemyetan Pagoda, is the only Buddha image in the
world wearing glasses (entirely of gold, two metres long, weighing ten
kilograms). Back in Pyay we then took a taxi to Hwaza, the starting
point for the ox-cart ride to the remains of the ancient Pyu kingdom.

We continued overnight by bus to Thandwe (4,500 k, 13 h). This was the
worst bus journey: the interior was packed with passengers and cargo
(including fish sauce), and all the seats were broken. So I chose to lie
on the floor on top of sacks of rice, just to get some sleep. I may have
smelled like a polecat the next morning, but at least I was reasonably
rested. Pick-up truck from Thandwe to Ngapali Beach (200 k, 20 min).

## Days 21–25 — Ngapali Beach

Surprisingly few travellers visit Burma’s most famous beach resort,
which by now has a decent tourist infrastructure. The reason the beach
is still a well-kept secret may well be the two dreadful, long bus
journeys you have to endure to get here from Rangoon or Pyay — unless
you want to spend US \$75 on a one-way flight.

Ngapali Beach is paradise on earth: a long, wide sandy beach lined with
coconut palms, which you usually have almost entirely to yourself. In
the evenings, the fishermen’s families in the village will cook for you
the most delicious, freshest and cheapest seafood in the world. Ngapali
Beach has become one of my absolute favourite destinations.

### Accommodation

Linn Thar Oo — the only decent budget option directly on the beach. A
fairly large but still pleasant resort with a wide range of bungalows
and rooms in different price categories. We had what must have been the
cheapest room, for US \$4 per person, in a simple wooden hut in the
second row, with an outdoor shower — perfectly adequate for our
purposes. A good, fairly extensive breakfast buffet was included in the
rate.

### Food

Ngapali is a dream for any seafood lover. Lobster, crab, tuna, red
snapper, squid — always fresh, always delicious.

Next to Linn Thar Oo there were a row of simple seafood restaurants, all
offering similar dishes at similar prices. We tried them all, and our
favourite was Paradise Restaurant. In the restaurant next door, however,
you could get a “local” caipirinha for 500 k.

Prices:

Large BBQ red snapper: €1–2\
BBQ baby tuna: €1.50\
BBQ lobster: €4–6

Overnight bus journey along the coast via Gwa to Rangoon (5,000 k, 13
h).

## Day 26 — Rangoon

Relaxing in Rangoon. This time we stayed at Motherland Inn 2, in my
opinion a better choice than the White House. It is about a 20-minute
walk from the city centre, but very friendly and clean, and the dorm
beds for US \$3 are among the cheapest places in town.

## Day 27 — Kyaikto

In the morning we took the bus to Kinpun (3,000 k, 5 h). In the
afternoon we took a pick-up truck to the second camp, from where the
ascent to the Golden Rock Pagoda began. This pagoda in Mon State has
only been open to foreign tourists again since 2000. The hike up took a
good 45 minutes, past interesting souvenir stalls for pilgrims selling
such delightful items as monkey blood and severed monkey hands, whose
sticky, pungent smell completely took your breath away on the already
steep ascent. Naturally, the pagoda is more than worth the effort — a
fascinating and unusual sight. Whoever built this strange thing here in
the middle of nowhere in the 12th century must have been out of his
mind.

### Accommodation

Sea Sar Guesthouse in the Kinpun base camp is made up of several
bungalows of varying quality. The best ones, with air conditioning and
private bathroom, could be had for just US \$7 after extensive
bargaining. The furnishings are fairly basic, but it is good value.

## Day 28 — Bago

In the morning we took the bus to Bago (1,500 k, 4 h). This provincial
town not too far from Rangoon is a popular tourist destination because
of its numerous famous pagodas, for which you’d have had to pay a flat
entrance fee of US \$10. After travelling almost the entire country, the
prospect of paying that much money just to see yet more pagodas was not
especially appealing, so we decided that a look from the outside would
have to do. Otherwise, Bago is a rather unspectacular provincial town —
perfectly pleasant, but certainly not a “must-see”.
